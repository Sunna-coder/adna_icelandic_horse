The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record — it has remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted what is one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of horses to Iceland [**Sunna: citation needed — e.g., Grágás (the medieval Icelandic law code) or the standard reference used in your National Museum proposal**]. Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. This legislation, maintained without interruption from the Viking Age to the present day, has produced a population of extraordinary scientific value: a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown at the genomic level. We do not know which Scandinavian populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in one event or many, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most distinctive traits — its gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under isolation. These are not merely academic questions: the Icelandic horse is a living part of Iceland's national identity, a significant economic resource, and an exceptional scientific model for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation in a domestic species. This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding:** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves of immigration? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions? The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock across the North Atlantic; documentary sources indicate that Viking settlers transporting horses by sea would have brought animals from their departure regions rather than acquiring them en route [**Sunna: citation needed — please check whether your existing literature covers this; alternatively remove the sentence if you cannot support it**], so the founding Icelandic horses likely reflected the departure points of the colonists. The ancient human genomics of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry in proportions of approximately 0.57 Norse : 0.43 Gaelic during the settlement period, shifting to roughly 0.70 : 0.30 by 1300 CE, with the Norse component disproportionately drawn from southern and western Norway, and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and, to a lesser extent, Scotland. Many settlers did not depart directly from Norway but from Norse settlements in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where Scandinavian populations had lived alongside Celtic-speaking people for a generation or more. If the people who settled Iceland were mixed Norse-Gaelic, the horses they brought very plausibly were too: a settler from the Hebrides would have brought horses carrying a mixture of Scandinavian and native Celtic or British pony ancestry. The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds — small stature, extreme hardiness, dense coat — has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis," but it has never been tested with ancient genomic data. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE in the Viking Age, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock. This project will answer that question for the first time.

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation:** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises — the Black Death (~1402 CE), the catastrophic Laki volcanic famine (1783–1784 CE), which reduced the horse population by an estimated 70% within two years [**Sunna: citation needed**], and periodic livestock epidemics — leave detectable signatures in the genomic record? The known historical timeline, with major demographic events precisely dated in written and documentary sources, allows a level of calibration between genomics and history that is almost never achievable in ancient population genomics. Whether such events are visible as reductions in effective population size, increases in runs of homozygosity, or shifts in allele frequencies is a question this project is uniquely positioned to answer.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution:** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How do the genetic variants associated with the breed's most distinctive traits change in frequency through time? The Icelandic horse's most celebrated trait, the tölt, depends on a single premature stop codon in DMRT3 (Andersson et al. 2012): when did this allele reach fixation — was it selected from the founding stock, or did it rise to dominance gradually over centuries? Similarly, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation responsible for the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome — documented at clinically significant frequencies in the breed (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011) — has an unknown history in the Icelandic population. Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), which affects an estimated 30–40% of exported Icelandic horses [**Sunna: citation needed — e.g., Eriksson et al. or the Shrestha et al. 2015 paper already cited below; please confirm**], is associated with genomic regions on ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, and ECA18 (Shrestha et al. 2015) and the ECA20 MHC region (Schurink et al. 2012). The MHC — major histocompatibility complex — is the genomic region encoding the proteins responsible for immune recognition and pathogen defence; it is one of the most variable regions in vertebrate genomes, and MHC diversity is widely regarded as a proxy for a population's capacity to resist infectious disease. The temporal dataset will allow us to determine when susceptibility alleles first appeared and whether they rose through drift or selection. Notably, despite their bottleneck history, Icelandic horses display unexpectedly high MHC heterozygosity — a recent study found 79 distinct MHC haplotypes, attributed to a recombination hotspot mechanism maintaining immune gene diversity under isolation (Maccari et al. 2019) — and exceptional longevity (routinely 30–35 years, records of 40+), likely connected to this breadth of immune defence. Whether MHC diversity was present from founding or was sustained by balancing selection during isolation is directly addressable with the temporal dataset proposed here.

The project is highly feasible, resting on four pillars of prior work that together eliminate the main risks associated with ancient animal genomics projects. **Proven aDNA methods for Icelandic material:** the applicant has developed aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols specifically optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material and applied them at scale in Ebenesersdóttir et al. (*Science* 2018) to recover genome-wide data from 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals, including specimens over 1,100 years old; an expanded dataset of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation) further demonstrates the reliability and scalability of this approach. Crucially, five ancient Icelandic horse specimens have already been extracted and sequenced using the same protocol, yielding 10–40% endogenous horse DNA — far above the ≥1% threshold required for high-quality genomic analysis, and confirming that Icelandic horse bone and dental material preserves aDNA at excellent levels. **Existing sample collection:** sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, covering burial contexts (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and assemblages from more than 35 archaeological sites distributed across all major regions of Iceland [**Sunna: please verify exact site count before submission**]. In northern Iceland, sites include burial grounds at Dalvík/Brimnes (Eyjafjörður), Enni (Skagafjörður), Glaumbær (Skagafjörður), Stærri-Árskógur (Eyjafjörður), Granastaðir (Þingeyjarsýsla), and Grímsstaðir near Mývatn. South Iceland is represented by Hemla (Landeyjahreppur), Traðarholt, Mörk (Landmannahreppur), Niðurföll (near Þykkvabæjarklaustur), and Lækur in Flói. East Iceland contributes specimens from Berufjörður, Breiðdalur, and Seyðisfjörður, and the Westfjords are represented by sites in Barðastrandarsýsla. Particularly significant for Aim 2 are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE — horse remains coinciding almost exactly with the Laki famine — which will allow direct genomic observation of the population immediately before and after that catastrophic event. Seven libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) have been prepared, five of which have been quality-assessed and are ready for Phase 2. **Infrastructure and computing:** all wet laboratory work will be conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík — the facility in which the applicant developed the protocols now used in this project; a collaboration agreement is in place providing access to the dedicated aDNA clean-room laboratory, the Illumina NovaSeq sequencing platform, and the deCODE high-performance computing cluster; no new infrastructure investment is required [**Sunna: please confirm the current status of the deCODE agreement and whether a copy can be attached to the application**]. **International collaboration and reference data:** the project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), whose laboratory has generated the world's largest collection of ancient and modern horse genomes and developed the imputation reference panel (901 modern + 376 ancient horse genomes) on which Phase 2 depends; the applicant and Prof. Orlando are already active co-authors on Ahmed et al. (under review), demonstrating an established and productive international collaboration.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the Norse Atlantic world. This project is the first genome-wide investigation of the Icelandic horse across time. Its originality rests on four dimensions. **First, geographic and biological uniqueness:** the Icelandic horse is the direct biological descendant of Viking Age Norse horse husbandry, preserved in near-complete genomic isolation by the livestock import ban; the entire North Atlantic remains unsampled in the ancient horse genomic record, and Ahmed et al. (under review) has established the NORD ancestry component that defines Icelandic horses emerged at 810 CE — our project will be the first to examine what happened to that founding stock after it arrived in Iceland. **Second, temporal depth and historical resolution:** a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single isolated population with a fully documented historical record allows calibration of genomics against precisely dated historical events — a resolution level that is almost never achievable in ancient population genomics. **Third, scientific model system:** the Icelandic horse's combination of genetic isolation, demographic documentation, and phenotypic distinctiveness makes it a powerful model for questions central to evolutionary biology and conservation genetics (Frankham 1997 reviewed foundational evidence that genetic diversity loss in small, isolated populations carries measurable fitness consequences — exactly the process this project will trace empirically across more than a millennium) — how do founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants, what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. **Fourth, paired human and horse genomic data:** the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) has generated genome-wide data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals from 58 sites across Iceland, at least 17 of which [**Sunna: please verify this number against the cross-reference Excel file**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project; combining these datasets creates paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events — a scientific resource without parallel in the current ancient genomics literature [**Sunna: paired human–horse genomic datasets from individual burial contexts are extremely rare; you may want to soften this to "without parallel at this temporal depth and sample size" if you are concerned about overclaiming**].

The impacts of this project will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, it establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources; the aDNA methods, bioinformatic pipelines, and reference data generated here are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods applied to Icelandic archaeological material [**Sunna: there are ancient DNA studies of these species from Scandinavia and Britain, but please check whether any specifically use Icelandic skeletal material; if so, the claim should be narrowed further**], creating both a scientific result and a methodological template for future work on Icelandic bioarchaeological material. For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's central role in settlement-era Icelandic society — as a working animal, a ritual object, a mark of social status, and a companion in death (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016, the foundational catalogue of Viking Age burials in Iceland, systematically documents horse remains across kuml sites; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019 applied molecular sex determination to horse remains from Icelandic Viking Age graves, confirming the feasibility of ancient molecular work on this material) — is well documented in the sagas and the archaeological record, and reconstructing the founding of the breed in the Viking Age will contribute directly to public understanding of Icelandic history. For the Icelandic horse industry, there are approximately 80,000–100,000 registered Icelandic horses worldwide [**Sunna: citation needed — WorldFengur / FEIF statistics would be the standard reference here**]; sweet itch affects an estimated 30–40% of exported horses [**Sunna: citation needed — see above**] and ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011); the first historical genomic baseline for the breed will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context and to clarify when and how disease-associated alleles rose to their current frequencies. For global science, the Icelandic horse is an exceptional model for evolutionary biology and conservation genetics; the methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project, and the findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and selection under isolation are relevant to conservation biology broadly.
