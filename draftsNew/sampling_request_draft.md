## Introduction

We are writing to request permission to take small destructive samples from a set of archaeological horse remains held at the National Museum of Iceland (Þjóðminjasafnið) for ancient DNA (aDNA) analysis. This request extends our existing collaboration with the Museum, under which permission was previously granted to sample 24 horse specimens from the settlement era. Sigríður Sunna Ebenesersdóttir is now applying for a Marie Curie Postdoctoral Fellowship (MSCA-PF) from the European Commission, as well as research funding from Rannís and the University of Iceland postdoctoral fund, to carry out a comprehensive genomic study of the Icelandic horse together with Agnar Helgason. We wish to include a broader temporal and geographic range of specimens to maximise the scientific scope of the project. Please note that if funding from these sources is not secured, the project will not proceed, and no sampling will be performed.

The proposed study will produce the first genome-wide reconstruction of the Icelandic horse's evolutionary history, tracing the population's founding, its demographic changes over 1,100 years, and the genomic consequences of more than a millennium of geographic isolation. We believe this research will be of significant scientific and cultural value to Iceland and to the broader international community of researchers working on horse domestication, Norse history, and island biogeography.

## Scientific background

The Icelandic horse is one of the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world. Horses were first brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, and their descendants are thought to have remained separated from all other horse breeds since the establishment of one of the world's oldest livestock import bans, introduced around the 10th century CE (Aðalsteinsson, 1981; Björnsson & Sveinsson, 2004). Despite this uniquely continuous and documented lineage, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is virtually unknown.

Our research group has extensive experience in recovering and analysing ancient DNA from Icelandic skeletal material. In a study published in *Science* (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018), we successfully generated genome-wide data from 27 ancient Icelandic individuals using the same methodological framework we now propose to apply to horse remains. More recently, we have expanded this dataset to 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes spanning 870–1900 CE (manuscript in preparation; submission planned for 2026).

The ancient DNA extraction and sequencing pipelines developed in our laboratory at deCODE have consistently performed exceptionally well on degraded Icelandic skeletal material, including specimens more than 1,000 years old. As a proof of concept for the proposed approach, we tested our methods on five archaeological horse samples dated to 870–1000 CE (VHR085, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, and VHR105). These samples yielded 15–30% endogenous DNA content and were subsequently sequenced to depths of 0.8–8× coverage, demonstrating the robustness and suitability of our laboratory protocols for challenging archaeological material.

The proposed work will be conducted in collaboration with Professor Tom Gilbert, Director of the Danish National Research Foundation Centre for Evolutionary Hologenomics and group leader of the Hologenomics Group at the GLOBE Institute, Denmark, and Professor Ludovic Orlando at the Centre for Anthropobiology and Genomics of Toulouse (CAGT), University of Toulouse, France. Professor Orlando is a leading expert in ancient horse genomics, horse domestication and breed history. His laboratory has developed a reference panel comprising more than 900 modern and ancient horse genomes, which will be used to impute and analyse the ancient Icelandic horse genotypes generated in this study.

## Research objectives

This project has three specific aims:

**Aim 1:** Reconstruct the genomic ancestry and demographic history of ancient Icelandic horses, identifying the founding populations, the number and timing of introduction events, and demographic changes over 1,100 years.

**Aim 2:** Identify the Scandinavian, British, and Eurasian genetic origins of the Icelandic horse by comparing ancient Icelandic horse genomes with ancient and modern horses from Scandinavia, Britain, continental Europe, and the Eurasian steppe using imputation-based comparative analysis.

**Aim 3:** Assess the genomic impact of long-term geographic isolation, including levels of inbreeding, signatures of adaptation to Iceland's environment, and the prevalence of disease-related and trait-associated genetic variants.

To achieve these aims, we aim to sequence up to 90 ancient Icelandic horse specimens spanning the settlement period (ca. 870 CE) through to the late 19th or early 20th century.

## Sampling methodology

All sampling will be carried out by Sigríður Sunna Ebenesersdóttir and Joe Walser III, or under his direct supervision. We use the same minimally destructive approach successfully applied to the 22 previously sampled Icelandic horse specimens collected by Albína Hulda Pálsdóttir and to over 200 Icelandic human skeletal specimens collected by Sigríður Sunna Ebenesersdóttir and Joe Walser III.

**Material requested per specimen:** Approximately 50–100 mg of cortical bone powder, obtained by drilling or surface scraping of a non-diagnostic, non-visible area of the bone. We will preferentially sample teeth or the petrous portion of the temporal bone, as these substrates typically provide superior biomolecular preservation. For teeth (the preferred substrate), we sample the root or an internal section of the crown. For loose bone assemblages, we will select the most morphologically redundant element available.

**Surface decontamination:** All bone surfaces are cleaned with a low-speed drill prior to sampling to remove potential modern contamination. Sampling is carried out under conditions that minimise environmental contamination.

**Documentation:** All sampling actions will be documented with before-and-after photographs, and a written sampling report will be provided to the Museum upon completion.

We do not require whole bones or teeth to be removed from the Museum. All work can be performed on-site at the Museum, or samples can be taken as small amounts of bone powder using a hand-held drill, leaving the specimen otherwise intact.

## Laboratory analysis

All aDNA work will be performed at the dedicated ancient DNA clean-room laboratory at deCODE Genetics, Reykjavík, which was established and is maintained by the applicants. This facility follows strict contamination-control protocols standard in the field of ancient genomics.

The analytical pipeline involves:

- DNA extraction using silica-based methods optimised for degraded skeletal material at deCODE Genetics
- Illumina sequencing library preparation with unique molecular indexing at deCODE Genetics
- Sequencing on Illumina platforms (NovaSeq or HiSeq) at deCODE Genetics, the CAGT, University of Toulouse, France or the GLOBE Institute in Denmark
- Read mapping to the horse reference genome (EquCab3)
- Genotype imputation using the Orlando laboratory reference panel (>900 horse genomes)
- Population genomic analyses: ancestry estimation, demographic modelling, selection scans
