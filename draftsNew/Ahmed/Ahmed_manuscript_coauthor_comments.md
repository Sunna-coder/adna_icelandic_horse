# Co-author Comments on Ahmed et al. (under review)
# Different breeding traditions shaped horse genetic diversity within and outside Asia

*Comments prepared by Sigríður Sunna Ebenesersdóttir as co-author. Comments cover both writing/grammar and scientific/factual issues. These are internal comments to help improve the manuscript before submission.*

---

## Title

**"Different breeding traditions shaped horse genetic diversity within and outside Asia"**

The phrase "within and outside Asia" is slightly awkward because "within" and "outside" are not a natural pair — you are inside something or outside it, so "inside and outside Asia" reads more cleanly and symmetrically. The SI title already uses "in and outside Asia" (page 1 of SI), which is inconsistent with the main manuscript title. The two titles should be made to match, and the slightly more formal "inside and outside Asia" would work well in both.

Additionally, the phrase "shaped" is appropriate but the passive framing — breeding traditions shaped diversity — buries what the paper actually shows, which is *how different* those traditions were, not merely that they existed. The title does its job, but could be sharpened.

---

## Abstract

> "We find distinct breeding traditions across Eurasia already apparent by ~3,750 ya, after which Asian breeds have larger effective sizes, limited differentiation, and significant introgression from Przewalski's horses, native to Mongolia."

This sentence carries a lot of information and is slightly difficult to parse. "After which" implies a temporal relationship (distinct traditions arose, and after that point, Asian breeds show certain properties), but the Asian properties are not specifically the consequence of reaching 3,750 ya — they are the properties that distinguish the traditions. The sentence could be restructured for clarity. Something like: "By ~3,750 ya, distinct breeding traditions are already apparent across Eurasia: Asian breeds show larger effective sizes, limited differentiation, and significant introgression from Przewalski's horses native to Mongolia."

> "Outside central and eastern Asia, horse populations remained poorly differentiated until ~700-1,350 ya, when five of the seven distinctive genetic ancestries characterizing modern breeds emerged, leading to a more diverse European landscape."

The range "~700-1,350 ya" corresponds to roughly 676-1326 CE, but the emergence dates given in the Results are: ARAB ~608-722 CE, NORD ~810 CE, DRAFT ~884 CE, BRIT ~1029 BCE (Iron Age), THOR ~1295 CE. The BRIT ancestry dates to ~1029 BCE, which is approximately 3,000 years ago — far earlier than the 700-1,350 ya range stated in the abstract. If BRIT is included among the five, the 700-1,350 ya range is inaccurate. If BRIT is excluded from the five (which would be justified, since it represents preservation of Iron Age ancestry rather than a new emergence), then the abstract should say "four of the seven" or clarify which five are meant. This apparent inconsistency should be resolved explicitly.

> "...leading to a more diverse European landscape."

This ending is vague. More diverse relative to what — to Asia, or to earlier European horses? The preceding sentence already implies the comparison with Asia. "Leading to the relatively diverse landscape of modern European breeds" would be more precise.

## Introduction

> "Today, approximately sixty million horses live on the planet (1)."

The reference is to the FAO FAOSTAT Database (2024). It is worth verifying that 60 million is consistent with the 2024 FAO data — FAO figures for horses in recent years have typically been around 58-60 million. This is fine as stated.

> "However, before combustion engines were invented in the late 19th century, horses were indispensable to urban transportation, long-distance travel, war making and even farming, shaping civilizations and economies worldwide (2)."

"war making" is hyphenated in standard usage as "war-making." The compound adjective before a noun can go without a hyphen, but as a gerund noun phrase here, it reads more naturally as "war-making." Also, the reference given is a book about horses in cities in the 19th century — it may not adequately support the broader claim about "shaping civilizations and economies worldwide." Consider adding a broader reference for the global claim.

> "This lineage spread remarkably quickly, crossing western Europe and Central Asia within just a few centuries (3-5)."

The geographic compass is inconsistent: "western Europe" uses lower-case "western" while "Central Asia" uses upper case. Standardise — either "Western Europe and Central Asia" or "western Europe and central Asia" throughout.

> "Its successful integration into human economies ultimately led to the extinction of an extraordinary diversity of wild lineages that had survived since the last Ice Age (5, 6)."

This is a strong claim. "Extinction" of wild lineages is framed as a direct consequence of domestic horse integration. This is a reasonable interpretation but may benefit from a qualifier — "contributed to," or "is thought to have contributed to" — since other factors (climate change, human hunting) also played a role.

> "With an estimated census size of ~3,000 (7, 8), the Przewalski's horse, native to the Mongolian steppe, is referred to as the last truly wild horse (9)."

The census size figure of ~3,000 is somewhat outdated. Reintroduction efforts in Mongolia have been ongoing for decades, and by the mid-2020s the census size has grown. Reference 8 (Turghan et al. 2022) gives an updated figure. If the ~3,000 figure comes from that source, it should be verified against the 2022 data; if it is from Ryder 1988 (reference 7), it needs updating. Please confirm which reference supports ~3,000.

> "It shares strong genetic affinities with a lineage bred for milk and meat by the mid-fourth millennium BCE (4), perhaps providing evidence of horse husbandry in Central Asia a thousand years before DOM2 emerged (10, 11)"

Missing period at end of sentence.

> "After humans, horses are the species with the largest collection of published ancient genomes (12)."

This is a memorable claim. Reference 12 (Orlando 2020, BioEssays) is a review article, and the claim is now several years old. Given the publication date of this manuscript (2026), it is worth confirming whether this ranking still holds, or whether cattle or another species has since surpassed horses in published ancient genome count.

> "However, the genetic characterization of modern horse populations has lagged behind that of other major livestock, where extensive sequencing efforts–such as the 1000 Bull Genomes project (13)–have been undertaken for studies of natural selection and genotype-phenotype association."

The em-dashes (–) here are used with no space, which is inconsistent with the spaced em-dashes used elsewhere in the manuscript. Standardise punctuation style throughout. Also note "genotype-phenotype association" should arguably be "genotype-to-phenotype associations" or "genotype–phenotype associations" (using an en-dash, which is standard in this compound).

> "This relative lack of modern genomes has considerably limited efforts to understand the breeding history that shaped horse genetic diversity worldwide."

The preceding sentence makes the framing "relative lack" relative to cattle. This is fair, but the Introduction then pivots directly to what the current paper provides. A brief sentence noting what was previously done (e.g., the existing SNP-chip studies, or specific earlier genome-scale papers) would help contextualise the advance being claimed here. As written, the reader gets the impression that almost nothing has been done, when in fact several genome-scale horse studies exist (including Fages et al. 2019, cited as reference 6, and others).

## Results

### Genome dataset

> "We sequenced the genomes of 368 horses (184 males, 184 females) at an average depth-of-coverage of 27.00-fold (range=8.02 to 72.71-fold) using the Illumina X Ten platform"

"Illumina X Ten" should be "Illumina HiSeq X Ten." The SI (page 2) correctly says "Illumina HiSeq Xten instrument," but the main text drops "HiSeq." These should match.

> "The newly generated genomes included a total of 125 breeds, 17 semi-feral, and 6 feral populations (East Taseko, Romanian, Dartmoor, the Namibian desert, Sable Island, and American mustangs), for which no or limited genome data were available (Table S1)."

"for which no or limited genome data were available" — this phrase is ambiguous: does it apply to the 125 breeds plus 17 semi-feral plus 6 feral, or only to the 6 named feral populations? Restructuring would help: "...sampling also included 17 semi-feral and 6 feral populations (East Taseko, Romanian, Dartmoor, Namibian desert, Sable Island, and American mustangs), for which no or limited genome-scale data were previously available."

Also, listing "the Namibian desert" as a population name is odd — a desert is a place, not a breed. If this refers to Namib Desert horses, say so.

> "It featured 17 endangered populations (including new Przewalski's horses, N=10), 34 at risk of extinction, and 51 of unknown status."

"new Przewalski's horses" — "new" is unclear. Do you mean newly sequenced Przewalski's horses? Rephrase to avoid confusion: "newly sequenced Przewalski's horses (N=10)."

> "Additionally, we examined published genomes from 543 modern horses..."

The main text says 543 previously published genomes here and in the abstract, but the SI (page 2, line 69-70) says "544 that were previously published." The totals given downstream also vary: "912 genomes" (= 368 + 543 + 1 donkey = 912, if 543 is correct) vs. "912 genomes" (= 368 + 544 = 912, if 544 is correct and the donkey is not counted in the 544). The discrepancy of 543 vs. 544 previously published horse genomes should be reconciled. Check Table S1.

> "This provided a total of 912 genomes with a wide representation of indigenous and regional breeds for joint variant calling using GraphTyper (16)."

The abstract says "901 horse genomes" for the diversity panel, but this sentence says 912. The 912 includes the donkey outgroup plus the horses excluded at QC. The discrepancy is not confusing if explained, but the casual reader may find 901 vs. 912 contradictory. A brief parenthetical noting that 912 is the total input and 901 pass QC filters would be helpful.

> "After filtering, the 'high-quality' panel yielded 22,579,858 high-confidence biallelic single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) variants, where the minor allele was carried by at least two individuals."

The abbreviation SNP has already been introduced implicitly through the paper's context, but this is technically the first formal definition in the Results. That is fine.

> "Importantly, the 'diversity' panel contains 38.61 times more SNPs than current high-density equine genotyping arrays (18) and the collection available in the European Variation Archive (19), and better captures the variation present in the full range of horse breeds."

"the collection available in the European Variation Archive" — what collection specifically? This sentence compares the panel to (a) the 670K array (reference 18) and (b) some unspecified EVA collection. If it is the Jagannathan et al. 2019 88-horse dataset (reference 19), say that explicitly: "the largest previously published SNP collection available in the European Variation Archive (Jagannathan et al. 2019; ref. 19)."

> "Key analyses, including PCA and ADMIXTURE, were performed on both the 'high-quality' and 'diversity' panels. Results were highly consistent, confirming that major patterns of population structure and ancestry differentiation, particularly between Asian and non-Asian horses were robust to differences in sequencing depth and panel composition."

Missing comma after "non-Asian horses" before "were robust."

> "To track the rise of modern sequence variation in horses, we also compiled a collection of 376 previously published ancient DOM2 genomes, sequenced at minimum 0.75-fold depth-of-coverage (3, 4, 6, 10, 20-22), which were then subjected to imputation (Table S2)."

Six references (3, 4, 6, 10, 20-22 = seven actually, as "20-22" covers refs 20, 21, 22) are cited here. This is fine given that the ancient data are compiled from multiple prior studies. However, it would help readers to know approximately how many ancient genomes come from each study. A brief Table S2 description (or note to consult Table S2) suffices, but the main text currently gives no breakdown of provenance.

### Global patterns of modern genetic diversity

> "The most extreme separation of breeds on the first MDS dimension and principal component (PC) was between Thoroughbreds (α) and other UK-native breeds (β) plus various bloodlines from Europe, including continental draft and Scandinavian horses (γ), as well as central and eastern Asia (δ) (Fig. 1C, Fig. S6 and S7)."

This sentence is difficult to parse. The structure "between X and Y plus Z as well as W" is confusing about what is being contrasted with what. It reads as if the separation is between Thoroughbreds on one side and {UK-native breeds + European breeds + central/eastern Asian breeds} on the other, but that would mean nearly everything clusters away from Thoroughbreds. Clarify whether the "most extreme separation" is between Thoroughbreds and UK draft horses specifically (which is what the second MDS dimension clarifies), or whether the first dimension truly separates Thoroughbreds from everything else. If the latter, say so more directly.

> "Individuals from central Asia generally clustered close to Mongolian and eastern Asian bloodlines (δ). The notable exception was the Akhal-Teke (λ), a breed native to Turkmenistan showing greater affinity to Arabian horses (ε)."

The Greek letter labels (α, β, γ, δ, ε, θ, λ) are introduced without a legend in the text — the reader must consult Fig. 1C to understand them. A one-line parenthetical defining the symbols at first use (e.g., "(α = Thoroughbreds, β = UK draft breeds, γ = continental European breeds, δ = central/eastern Asian breeds, ε = Arabian horses, θ = Jeju horses, λ = Akhal-Teke)") would make the text self-standing.

> "The ADMIXTURE component predominant in Przewalski's horses was detectable in most DOM2 horses, with a higher representation inside Asia than outside (mean=1.41% vs. 0.18%, median=1.73% vs 0.001%, range=0.001-2.98% vs. 0.001-1.44%; Wilcoxon test, P-value=7.2210-36; Fig. 1D)."

The P-value is missing the × symbol: it should read "P-value=7.22×10⁻³⁶." This appears to be a typesetting error — check all P-values throughout the manuscript for similar omissions.

> "The segments that are Identical-By-Descent (IBD) between DOM2 and Przewalski's horses represented a significantly greater genome fraction among Asian breeds (Wilcoxon test, P-value=9.3410-57; Fig. 2A and B)."

Same issue: "9.3410-57" should be "9.34×10⁻⁵⁷."

> "Furthermore, three Przewalski's horses plus a first-generation hybrid between DOM2 and Przewalski's horses that was used as control carried ancestry from various DOM2 breeds, especially those from Asia (Fig. 1D)."

"that was used as control" should read "that was used as a control."

> "This is consistent with historical sources reporting repeated crossbreeding with Przewalski's horses in Mongolia, known locally as 'takhi' (8)."

The referent of "known locally as 'takhi'" is ambiguous — it could refer to Przewalski's horses or to the crossbreeding practice. The Mongolian name "takhi" refers to the horse, not the crossbreeding. Revise to: "This is consistent with historical records of repeated crossbreeding with Przewalski's horses in Mongolia; the Przewalski's horse is known locally as 'takhi' (8)."

> "Moreover, the ancestry component characterizing Przewalski's horses (PRZW) was over-represented in autosomal regions relative to the X-chromosome, leading to a significant excess of negative Admix values (Mann–Whitney U test, P-value=6×10-14; Fig. 2C, Fig. S12A and S13A)."

"6×10-14" — the superscript appears broken here too. Should be "6×10⁻¹⁴."

> "Admix measures the difference of genetic ancestry between autosomes (equally inherited from both parents) and the X-chromosome (more often transmitted by females than males), with an expectation of zero in the absence of sex bias."

"the difference of genetic ancestry" is slightly non-standard phrasing. Consider "the difference in genetic ancestry" or "differences in genetic ancestry proportions."

Also, the assertion that the X chromosome is "more often transmitted by females than males" is technically correct (sons inherit their single X from their mother, daughters inherit one X from each parent, giving females on average a 2/3 maternal and 1/3 paternal X contribution across the population), but this may not be intuitive to all readers. A brief clarifying phrase would help.

> "A similar sex bias, although more pronounced, and extending outside Asia, was observed when considering the ancestry component characterising Thoroughbreds (THOR; Mann–Whitney U test, P-value=4.658×10⁻¹⁴; Fig. 2D, Fig. S12B and S13B). This shows that the extensive global spread of Thoroughbred ancestry was primarily driven through stallions."

The P-values for PRZW and THOR sex bias are very similar (6×10⁻¹⁴ and 4.658×10⁻¹⁴). Yet the text says the Thoroughbred bias is "more pronounced." More pronounced than the Przewalski bias? The test statistic or effect size would help support this claim, since near-identical P-values do not tell us about effect size. Consider adding the actual ΔAdmix values or effect sizes.

> "Arabian bloodlines comprise another major ancestry source (ARAB), accounting for the main genetic ancestry found in Kurdistan (Kurdish/Barzan; Fig. 2G), Sicily (Sicilian oriental purebreds) and northern Africa (Barbs) (Fig. 2H). The range of Arabian influence extends across southwestern (Dareshouri), central (Turkoman and Akhal-Teke) and eastern (Marwari, Sindhi and Waziri) Asia (Fig. 2G), plus in the Caucasus (Kabardin) and Europe (Lipizzaner, Lusitano, Senner horses, and; Reit ponies) (Fig. 2H)"

Two issues: (1) "and;" before "Reit ponies" is a typo — the semicolon should be removed. (2) Missing period at the end of the sentence/paragraph.

> "Strikingly, with a SOURCEFIND (28) contribution of ≥15% THOR ancestry in 251 out of the 483 individuals analyzed, Thoroughbreds appear as the most influential of non-Asian sources outside Asia (Chi-square tests, P-values≤1.73×10−8; Fig. 2G and H, Fig. S14)."

The denominator (483 individuals) is not defined in the main text — this appears to be the number of non-Asian horses in the SOURCEFIND analysis. Stating this explicitly ("in 251 out of 483 non-Asian individuals") avoids confusion for readers who may try to reconcile this with the 901 total or 272 Asian horses mentioned in the next paragraph.

> "Modern domesticated horses outside Asia exhibit significantly higher inbreeding coefficients (mean=0.15) compared to Asia (0.082; Wilcoxon test, P-value=3.39×10−33)"

Inbreeding coefficients of 0.15 (non-Asian) vs. 0.082 (Asian) are high in absolute terms. For context: F = 0.25 corresponds to a full-sibling mating. It would be useful for readers to briefly note what these values correspond to biologically (e.g., equivalent to approximately X generations of half-sibling mating), or at minimum direct readers to Fig. 2E or Supplementary figs for the distributions.

> "This recent demographic decline aligns with more extended among modern individuals defining the BRIT, DRAFT, and NORD ancestries relative to those defining the MONG ancestry (Fig. S2)."

This sentence appears incomplete or garbled. "aligns with more extended among modern individuals" is missing a noun — "more extended ROH among modern individuals"? Please revise.

> "These were measured using Alpha, a proxy equalling to 0° when pairwise genetic distances strictly mirror geographic distances, indicating no mobility."

"equalling to" is ungrammatical. Use either "equalling 0°" or "equal to 0°."

> "In Asia, Alpha remained consistently high throughout the last ~3,200 years, reflecting intense mobility limiting population structure in the region."

"reflecting intense mobility limiting population structure" is a compressed construction that is slightly hard to read. Consider: "reflecting intense mobility that limited population structure in the region."

> "Importantly, the sharpest mobility decline in Europe occurred between 450-550 CE, coinciding with the fall of the Roman Empire (476 CE; (30)), the reduced reliance on mules for long-distance transportation (31), and the emergence of more local economies (32, 33)."

Reference 31 (Clavel et al. 2022, on sex-specific management of equine resources in France) is cited here as support for "reduced reliance on mules for long-distance transportation." This reference is about sex management of horses/equids in France — it may support the claim indirectly, but it seems a mismatch for the specific claim about mules and long-distance transport. Check whether a more directly relevant reference exists.

> "Breeding practices largely involved minimal inbreeding throughout the last 4,000 years both in Asia and Europe, with only 21 out of 376 (5.59%) individuals showing F>0.10 (Fig. 3F, Fig. S27-S28). The equivalent proportion among modern individuals is significantly lower (445/875=50.86%; Fisher's exact test, P-value=9.44×10−62; Fig. 3F, Fig S27-S28)."

"The equivalent proportion among modern individuals is significantly lower" — this is stated backwards. If 50.86% of modern horses show F>0.10 vs. 5.59% of ancient horses, modern horses have HIGHER inbreeding, not lower. The sentence should read "significantly higher" or the framing should be reworded. This is likely a drafting error rather than a conceptual one (the conclusion in the next sentence is correct), but it will confuse readers.

> "The total genome fraction comprising ROHs >4Mb is also significantly greater in modern horses, both within and outside Asia (Fig. S28C and D, Wilcoxon test, P-value=0.008 and 1.73×10−73, respectively)."

The two P-values correspond to within-Asia (P=0.008) and outside-Asia (P=1.73×10⁻⁷³) comparisons, but the text does not say which is which. Please specify: "(within Asia: P=0.008; outside Asia: P=1.73×10⁻⁷³)."

> "Mitochondrial haplotype and nucleotide diversity remained high during the past 3,600 years (Figs. 4A and S30), with all major modern haplogroups detectable throughout (Fig. S31-S33)."

The reference to Fig. S33 here implies it supports haplogroup detectability throughout the time period, but the SI describes Fig. S33 as showing ancient horse mtDNA phylogeny and k-mer clustering, not temporal haplogroup frequencies. Fig. S31 is the temporal frequency plot. Check whether Fig. S33 is appropriate to cite here.

> "Conversely, the proportion of ancient Y-chromosomes not closely related to any modern haplogroup (hereafter referred to as unclassified) was high until ~1500 BCE, but declined since, especially during the last ~1500 years (Fig. 4B, Fig. S34-S36)."

"declined since" should be "has declined since" for grammatical consistency with the preceding clause. Also, the use of "~1500 BCE" and "the last ~1500 years" in the same sentence, with different senses of "1500," is potentially confusing for readers who read quickly.

> "Together with the relatively stable mitochondrial diversity, the recent burst of Crown haplogroups after 726-1012 CE (Fig.4B) is consistent with increasingly sex-biased breeding practices, considerably enhancing the reproductive success of specific stallion lineages."

Missing space after "Fig." before "4B" — should be "Fig. 4B."

> "These analyses largely agree with the timing of the Y-chromosomal turnover previously estimated using molecular clock calibrations (35), but show that Crown haplogroups (daC_T) emerged much earlier, and no later than 204 BCE (350-58 BCE)."

"and no later than 204 BCE (350-58 BCE)" — the parenthetical gives a range that includes 204 BCE as an interior point, but the text implies 204 BCE is the upper (most recent) bound. If 350-58 BCE is the 95% confidence interval, clarify: "no later than 204 BCE (95% CI: 350-58 BCE)" or similar. Also, the grammar "emerged much earlier, and no later than 204 BCE" is a bit awkward — "emerged much earlier than previously thought, with the daC_T haplogroup appearing no later than 204 BCE (350-58 BCE)" is cleaner.

> "Notably, the daC_A Crown haplogroup, predominant in modern Arabian horses (36), was first detected in Turkey ~1012 CE (Tur173, 992-1032 CE; Fig. 4B). The prevalence of Arabian genetic ancestry (ARAB), however, started to increase from ~608-722 CE both in Europe and Asia..."

This is a potentially interesting discrepancy: ARAB ancestry rises from ~608-722 CE but the daC_A Y-chromosome haplogroup is not detected until ~1012 CE. This implies either that (1) ARAB ancestry spread initially through mares/lineages without daC_A stallions, and the daC_A Y took longer to become established, or (2) our Y-chromosome sampling has a gap before 1012 CE. The text does not comment on this difference; even a brief sentence noting that the ARAB ancestry increase precedes the first detection of daC_A Y-chromosomes, and that this may reflect initial female-mediated spread, would strengthen the discussion.

> "Interestingly, BRIT ancestry, mostly prevalent in modern British native breeds, emerged within continental horses considerably earlier (~1029 BCE; Fig. 4G), suggesting that British populations preserve some of the genetic ancestry in mainland Europe during the Iron Age."

"preserve some of the genetic ancestry in mainland Europe" should probably be "preserve some of the genetic ancestry that was present in mainland Europe." As written, it implies the ancestry is currently in mainland Europe; the meaning is that British breeds preserve what was once widespread in mainland Europe.

## Discussion

> "The data resources developed for this study mark a new era for genetic research in horses."

This is a strong opening claim that functions as self-congratulation before the science is summarised. Consider opening with the key finding instead and letting the impact follow from the evidence. "This study provides the most comprehensive genome-wide characterisation of horse diversity to date, spanning..."

> "We provide an extensive time series of genomes, characterizing past and present sequence variation across the world, beyond merely the breeds of current major interest."

"beyond merely the breeds of current major interest" is slightly awkward in tone. Something like "extending beyond the major commercial bloodlines that have dominated previous work" would read better.

> "These resources therefore considerably empower future studies aimed at mapping the genetic basis of health-related traits in horses by providing a large panel of diverse horses and identifying a much more comprehensive set of sequence variants."

"aimed at mapping the genetic basis of health-related traits" — the connection to the preceding sentence (about "future studies") is fine, but "health-related traits" feels like a somewhat narrow framing for what the panel enables. The panel is also relevant for breeding, adaptation, and selection studies. The next sentence does address this more broadly, but the order is slightly inverted — the methodological advance is discussed before what science it enables.

> "Additionally, by providing explicit temporal trajectories for 10,786,605 sequence variants, our time-stamped dataset holds the potential to identify new candidates of potential adaptive and breeding value (26, 38)."

"candidates of potential adaptive and breeding value" — "candidates" lacks a noun here. Candidates for what? "candidates for selection" or "candidate variants of adaptive or breeding value" would be clearer.

> "It offers a valuable alternative to current genotyping arrays, which are relatively limited in density (39), impaired by ascertainment bias, varying SNP call rates (40), invalid and duplicated positions (41), and increasingly superseded by ever more cost-effective sequencing technologies (42)."

The list "impaired by ascertainment bias, varying SNP call rates, invalid and duplicated positions" is a mixed construction — these are noun phrases, but "impaired by ascertainment bias" is an adjective phrase. The sentence would read more smoothly as: "which suffer from relatively limited density (39), ascertainment bias, varying SNP call rates (40), and invalid and duplicated positions (41), and are increasingly superseded by more cost-effective sequencing (42)."

> "Imputation of low-depth ancient DNA data also overcomes some of the limitations related to DNA preservation, promising reduced experimental costs, and even helping with the genetic characterization of specimens showing limited DNA preservation, while providing finer-grained resolution into individual phenotypes, relatedness, population affinities and mobility (20, 43)."

This sentence is very long and contains an odd internal contradiction: it says imputation "overcomes limitations related to DNA preservation" and in the same breath helps "with specimens showing limited DNA preservation." These are consistent but the repetition is confusing. Splitting into two sentences would help.

> "This study clarifies the historical origins of the genetic diversity found in present-day horses around the world."

Good topic sentence for the second Discussion paragraph, but note that "clarifies" slightly undersells the contribution — the paper substantially advances understanding rather than merely clarifying what was already known.

> "In Asia, horse breeding yielded larger effective population sizes, retained more ancestral variation and significant admixture with Przewalski's horses, and experienced higher mobility reducing geographic differentiation."

"and experienced higher mobility reducing geographic differentiation" — as in Results, the compressed participle phrase is hard to read. "and experienced higher mobility, which limited geographic differentiation" is clearer.

> "Outside Asia, effective sizes and mobility were more dynamic and breeding practices resulted in more differentiated and homogeneous breeds."

"more differentiated and homogeneous breeds" — these are opposite properties. Breeds can be more differentiated from one another while being internally more homogeneous. The sentence needs clarification: "breeding practices resulted in more differentiated breeds, each internally more homogeneous."

> "There, horse mobility decreased during the Iron Age to reach minimal levels ~550 CE, possibly reflecting the collapse of long-distance Roman transportation networks (30). Horse mobility then gradually increased during the Early Middle Ages (500-1000 CE), a period marked by invasions, the rise of feudalism, and the expansion of Christianity (44). It briefly declined as the numerous small local polities and centralized kingdoms (45) of the High Middle Ages developed (1000-1300 CE), before it increased again alongside with improved road networks and the growing importance of cavalry in long-distance military campaigns (46)."

"alongside with" is grammatically incorrect — it should be either "alongside improved road networks" or "together with improved road networks."

Also, this paragraph ascribes a very specific and detailed historical narrative to the Alpha mobility proxy. The Discussion should acknowledge more clearly that these are proposed interpretations and that the Alpha metric captures a combined signal of actual horse movement plus gene flow through trade and breeding, which are not always the same as troop movements or road use. The text in the Results is more careful ("possibly reflecting"), but the Discussion presents this narrative with greater confidence than the data warrant.

> "Finally, our work shows that the ancestry that characterized horses from Iron Age Europe is preserved in modern Britain, in breeds such as Dartmoor and Exmoor."

The use of "finally" to introduce the last Discussion point is stylistically acceptable but slightly informal. No change needed, but note for consistency with journal style.

> "Another (Arabian) likely arose in Southwestern Asia and spread across Eurasia in the aftermath of the Muslim expansion (608-722 CE)."

"the Muslim expansion (608-722 CE)" — the dates 608-722 CE are actually the ARAB ancestry emergence dates from the Results, not the historical dates of the Muslim expansion per se (which began with the death of Muhammad in 632 CE and the subsequent first Islamic conquests). Using the ancestry emergence dates as if they define "the Muslim expansion" conflates the two. The historical Muslim conquests occurred from approximately 632-750 CE. The sentence should separate the ancestry timing from the historical event: "...spread across Eurasia in association with the Muslim expansion of the 7th century CE, appearing in ancient horses from ~608-722 CE."

> "The remaining ancestry present in modern Europe, characteristic of Thoroughbreds, emerged from a different background, in line with previous work, by the late 13th century (47, 48), before expanding globally."

"emerged from a different background" — a different background from what? This phrase is vague. What background? The sentence may be trying to say "emerged from a distinct ancestry source" or "arose independently of the Arabian and draft traditions." Clarify.

The Methods section is in the SI (titled "Materials and Methods") rather than appearing in the main text. The main text contains no dedicated Methods section. This is presumably the journal's format (Science-style), but the Acknowledgements section follows directly after the Discussion, with no Methods pointer. Confirm that the journal format is followed correctly and that a clear statement directing readers to the SI Methods is present (in the submission there may be a standard note, but it is not visible in this draft).

## Supplementary Information (SI)

### Title inconsistency

The SI title page reads: "Different breeding traditions shaped horse genetic diversity **in and outside** Asia." The main manuscript title uses "**within** and outside Asia." These must be made consistent before submission. The SI preposition ("in") is actually slightly more idiomatic than the main text's "within," so the SI version may be the better one to adopt for both.

## General Writing Comments

1. **Superscript formatting in P-values.** Multiple P-values throughout the manuscript have malformed exponents (e.g., "7.2210-36" instead of "7.22×10⁻³⁶"). This is likely a conversion artifact from an equation editor. All P-values and scientific notation should be checked and corrected in the final document.

2. **"Inside" vs. "within" vs. "in" for Asia.** The manuscript uses all three ("within Asia," "inside Asia," "in Asia") at various points. Pick one and use it consistently. "Within Asia" and "outside Asia" are the most natural pair.

3. **Comma usage before "while."** Several sentences use "while" to introduce contrast without a preceding comma (e.g., "This contrasts with Europe, where horse mobility steadily declined..."). Check that comma use before "while" follows journal style guidelines.

4. **Greek letter labels (α, β, γ, δ, ε, θ, λ) in the text.** These are used extensively without a text-level legend, requiring the reader to refer back to Fig. 1C. For a paper likely targeting a broad readership, a brief textual glossary of symbols on first use would improve readability.

5. **Consistent abbreviation style.** "DOM2" is used throughout (consistent). "MDS," "PCA," "IBD," "ROH," "ADMIXTURE," "SOURCEFIND," "SNP" — all introduced early and used consistently. No issues identified here.

6. **References.** Reference 5 contains a likely typo: "The genomic hisotry of Iberian horses" — "hisotry" should be "history." This is a reference title, not the authors' own text, so it may be a transcription error in the reference list rather than an error in the published paper. Verify against the original.
