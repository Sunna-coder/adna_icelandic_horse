# Comments on Plague Manuscript (outline_Plague.pdf)
# Second Plague Pandemic — Lund, Trondheim, Vilnius

*Comments prepared for Sigríður Sunna Ebenesersdóttir as co-author. Comments cover both grammar/writing and scientific/factual issues.*

---

## General

This is a strong paper with a large, well-characterised dataset and a clear primary finding (no selection signal; reduced ancestry diversity post-BD). The selection result is important but will be controversial, and the ancestry finding is the more compelling story. The Discussion needs to more directly address the alternative explanations raised by Agnar (see below).

## Abstract

**Current text:** "Whether exposure to Y. pestis incurred strong positive selection on protective genetic variants in the human genome, and if/how it shaped migration patterns, remains debated, despite having been addressed in several prior ancient DNA studies."

[Claude comment: "incurred strong positive selection" is slightly awkward — selection acts *on* variants, not something exposure *incurs*. Suggest: "Whether exposure to Y. pestis drove positive selection on protective genetic variants, or shaped migration patterns, remains debated..."]

**Current text:** "Genome-wide scans for signatures of selection provided no evidence for strong positive selection acting on specific genetic variants due to Y. pestis."

[Claude comment: "due to Y. pestis" is ambiguous — it could mean the variants are due to Y. pestis or the selection is due to Y. pestis. Clearer: "...no evidence for strong positive selection on specific genetic variants driven by Y. pestis exposure."]

**Current text:** "Thus, our results indicate notable changes in the sociocultural factors that brought people from afar to Lund, Trondheim and Vilnius before the second plague pandemic."

[Claude comment: This sentence is slightly awkward — "sociocultural factors that brought people from afar" is wordy. Suggest: "...our results indicate notable changes in the patterns of long-distance mobility into Lund, Trondheim and Vilnius following the arrival of the pandemic." This also avoids the implication that sociocultural factors changed *before* the pandemic (the sentence currently implies the factors existed before, not that they changed after).]

## Discussion

[Claude comment: The Discussion currently attributes the reduction in ancestry diversity primarily to the Black Death disrupting commercial, ecclesiastical, and political networks. **Agnar's comments raise four important alternative explanations that should be directly addressed:**

**1. The winding down of the Viking Age** — the Viking Age ended ~1100 CE, well before the Black Death (~1350 CE). If the decline in ancestry diversity began before the plague, the Black Death interpretation is weakened. The paper should show (or acknowledge) whether diversity was already declining in the 12th–13th century samples, or whether the decline is specifically dateable to the mid-14th century.

**2. Abolition of slavery** — slavery declined in Scandinavia from the 11th century onwards. Enslaved people from Britain, Ireland, and continental Europe would have contributed to ancestry diversity in early medieval contexts. Their disappearance from the population (and from burial contexts) predates the Black Death and could account for part of the observed pattern. This should be acknowledged as an alternative explanation.

**3. The Little Ice Age** — onset ~1300 CE, peaking 1300–1450 CE, overlaps with the Black Death. Economic disruption from climate deterioration could independently have reduced long-distance trade and mobility. The paper cannot easily disentangle the Little Ice Age from the Black Death without samples between ~1300–1347 CE.

**4. Christianisation bias in burial data (the most serious point)** — if non-Scandinavian clerics, merchants, and missionaries were disproportionately buried in Christian cemeteries in the early medieval period, the pre-BD diversity estimates may be inflated by burial context, not reflecting true population diversity. This point is already acknowledged for Vilnius explicitly, but its implications for Lund and Trondheim need to be considered. The Lund and Trondheim sites are also ecclesiastical contexts where early Christian burials may be biased toward non-local individuals.

**5. Diversity already declining during-BD in Lund** — Agnar noted this. If the paper's own data show the decline starting *during* rather than *after* the Black Death, this is important and supports the plague interpretation but needs to be clearly stated and visualised.]

**Current text:** "The catastrophic mortality associated with the Black Death likely disrupted commercial, ecclesiastical and political networks that had previously facilitated long-distance mobility. Such disruptions could have reduced the movement of merchants, clerics and craftsmen who contributed to the cosmopolitan character of medieval urban centres."

[Claude comment: This is a reasonable interpretation, but as written it treats the Black Death as the only possible cause. Given Agnar's points above, suggest adding a paragraph explicitly acknowledging the Viking Age decline, Little Ice Age, and burial bias as alternative or contributing factors, and explaining why the Black Death interpretation is nonetheless preferred (e.g., the timing of the decline is specifically dateable to the mid-14th century in the stratified data).]

**Current text:** "In this sense, the genomic consequences of the pandemic may have arisen primarily through changes in the structure of human mobility rather than through direct selection on immune-related genes."

[Claude comment: Good conclusion, but "may have arisen primarily" is appropriately cautious. Consider whether the data support a stronger statement — if the decline in ancestry diversity is specifically dated to 1347–1353 (the during-BD group in Lund), you can be more assertive.]
