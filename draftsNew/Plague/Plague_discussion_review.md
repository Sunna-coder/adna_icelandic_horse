## 2. "Bits and pieces not yet integrated" — this section must be resolved before submission

**Page 15** contains a bold heading: *"Bits and pieces not yet integrated: Needs to be merged into the part below or the discussion?"*

This includes two items that are important and should not remain in limbo:

**a) IBD sharing with present-day locals:**
> "We observe lower IBD sharing with present-day locals pre-BD than post-BD in Lund (t-test p = 5.6×10⁻⁸) and Trondheim (t-test p = 6.6×10⁻³), though we note the absolute length of IBD shared between individuals is expected to decrease with the length of time between them."

This result belongs in the Results section ("Quantifying ancestry diversity based on IBD") not in an unintegrated appendix. The caveat about temporal distance and IBD length is important — it complicates interpretation — and should be stated clearly when reporting this result. Either it belongs in the results and the temporal-distance caveat is addressed there, or the result is not reported. At present it is floating.

**b) Vilnius discontinuity paragraph:**
The detailed paragraph about Vilnius (Ruthenian churchyard, mass burial outside city walls, Christianisation) is present in two places — once in the Results (p.16) and once in the "bits and pieces" section (p.15/19 notes). The version in the Results is more polished. The notes version can likely be deleted. But check that nothing in the notes version was omitted from the Results version. The notes also flag: *"Vilnius being not significantly affected by the first BD episode and subsequent outbreaks, retaining status of regional political and economic centre"* — this point is not reflected in the current Discussion text, which focuses on Trondheim losing status and Lund retaining it. Vilnius is largely omitted from the structural argument in the Discussion, even though its pattern is distinctive and the explanation (burial bias) is arguably the most important caveat in the whole paper.

## 3. The causal argument — alternative explanations not adequately addressed

The Discussion attributes the reduction in ancestry diversity primarily to the Black Death disrupting trade and mobility networks. This is plausible, but the draft does not directly engage with the major alternative explanations. A reviewer will ask about all of these:

**a) Viking Age wind-down**
The Viking Age effectively ended ~1050–1100 CE. Pre-BD burials in this dataset span the 10th–14th centuries. The intense long-distance mobility of the Viking Age (including slave raiding and trading) would have introduced considerable ancestry diversity. If diversity was already declining before 1347, the Black Death interpretation is weakened. The paper should either show (from the stratified data) that diversity was roughly stable from 1100 to 1347 and then dropped sharply — or acknowledge this as a competing explanation. The current Discussion does not mention the Viking Age at all.

**b) Abolition of slavery**
Slavery declined in Scandinavia from the 11th century onwards. Enslaved people from Britain, Ireland, and the continent contributed to ancestry diversity in earlier periods. This is a structural change entirely independent of plague. The Discussion does not acknowledge it.

**c) The Little Ice Age**
Onset ~1300 CE, overlapping with the pre-BD/post-BD transition. Climate deterioration reduced agricultural surplus and disrupted trade routes well before the Black Death arrived. The paper cannot easily disentangle the Little Ice Age from the pandemic without precisely dated samples in the 1300–1347 window. This limitation should be stated.

**d) Burial context bias (most important caveat for Lund and Trondheim)**
The Vilnius section explicitly acknowledges that the pre-BD site (old Ruthenian Christian quarter) is biased toward Christian immigrants and the during-BD site (mass grave outside the walls) probably represents the local pagan population. This is treated as the explanation for the Vilnius pattern. But the **same logic applies to Lund and Trondheim**: both pre-BD samples come from Christian churchyards. In medieval Scandinavia, churches were often founded, staffed, and used by non-local clergy, pilgrims, merchants, and craftspeople. The high pre-BD ancestry diversity at the Lund Trinitatis churchyard and the Trondheim sites may partly reflect who was *buried at a church*, not who *lived in the city*. Post-BD, if churches declined as hubs for non-local populations (fewer pilgrims, fewer foreign clergy), the diversity would drop even without any change in the broader urban population. This is the single most important alternative explanation and it is currently not discussed for the Scandinavian sites.

**Suggested framing:** The Discussion should contain a paragraph explicitly addressing these alternatives, explaining what the data can and cannot rule out. The strongest evidence for the Black Death interpretation specifically would be: (i) showing that diversity decline coincides precisely with ~1347–1350 rather than a gradual earlier trend, and (ii) showing the during-BD group in Lund already shows reduced diversity compared to pre-BD (this is mentioned in the Results but not emphasised in the Discussion).

## 4. The "general trend" note in the author's own notes (p.19)

The author's notes on p.19 explicitly say:

> "General trend of population turnover in Northeast European cities, not necessarily primarily caused by the second plague pandemic"

This is the key caveat — the authors themselves acknowledge it — but it does not appear in the Discussion. It should. The Discussion currently reads as though the plague is the primary or sole cause. Adding a sentence acknowledging that broader medieval demographic trends were already shifting the population composition, and that the plague may have accelerated or finalised these changes, would make the argument more defensible.

## 5. Hair colour finding — not followed up in Discussion

The Results note a "consistent significant trend toward lighter hair pigmentation in the post-BD Lund cohort" (Fig. S28). This result is reported but completely absent from the Discussion. Given that the paper's other major finding is a reduction in non-Scandinavian ancestry post-BD, there is an obvious and important connection: if pre-BD Lund included more individuals from continental Europe, France, and the British Isles — populations with, on average, darker hair than Scandinavians — the loss of those individuals would predict lighter post-BD hair pigmentation without any selection. This would be a nice illustration of the ancestry finding's phenotypic consequences. At minimum, the Discussion should state that the hair colour shift is probably a downstream consequence of the ancestry shift rather than an independent selection signal.

## 6. The Trondheim "Icelanders" — not mentioned in the Discussion

Pages 31–32 contain detailed notes on SK226, SK317, SK340, and other Trondheim samples with strontium isotope ratios consistent with Icelandic origin and 14C dates placing them in the 10th–13th centuries. This is a remarkable finding — people from Iceland buried in medieval Trondheim — and it connects directly to the IBD finding in Figure 4C (multiple Trondheim pre-BD individuals with elevated Iceland IBD sharing). The Discussion mentions Iceland in passing (PCA shows some Trondheim outliers consistent with Iceland) but does not discuss what this means historically. Iceland was settled from Norway, maintained strong trading ties with Trondheim, and Nidaros Cathedral was an important pilgrimage site for Icelanders. The presence of likely Icelanders in Trondheim is historically coherent and worth a sentence in the Discussion.

---

## 7. LUN114 (likely half-Ashkenazi, post-BD Lund) — not addressed in Discussion

This individual is described in the Results and in the notes. A person with Ashkenazi Jewish-related ancestry buried in 14th century Lund is historically significant — it provides direct genomic evidence of Jewish presence in medieval Scandinavia, consistent with what is known from limited historical sources about Jewish merchants in Hanseatic trading centres. The Discussion's paragraph on the cosmopolitan character of Lund mentions "merchants, clerics and craftsmen" generically. A sentence acknowledging that the pre/post-BD analysis also surfaces individual-level findings of this kind — including what may be the first genomic identification of an individual with Ashkenazi ancestry in medieval Scandinavia — would strengthen the paper's broader appeal.

## 8. Trondheim result inconsistency — is the change significant?

The Results section reports that for Trondheim the Mahalanobis distance analysis difference between pre-BD and post-BD is **not significant** (P=0.14), and the IBD-based comparison is also **not significant** (P=0.68). However, the Discussion states: "A similar, though less pronounced, pattern is observed in Trondheim." This risks overstating the Trondheim result. If the Trondheim change is not statistically significant by either method used in Lund and Vilnius, the Discussion should say so clearly. The pattern is consistent in direction with Lund, but the difference between "consistent direction but not significant" and "similar pattern" matters.

---

## 9. Vilnius in the Discussion — underrepresented

The Discussion's site-specific paragraph gives Trondheim a sentence, Lund two sentences, and Vilnius two sentences. But the Vilnius story is actually the most interpretively interesting — the apparent discontinuity is fully explained by burial context, not by plague or migration change. This is an important methodological point: sampling context can produce spurious signals of population change. The Discussion should use the Vilnius case more prominently as a cautionary example that motivates the careful contextual interpretation applied throughout the paper.

---

## 10. The final paragraph — strong, but could go further

> "Taken together, our results suggest that the most visible genomic legacy of the second plague pandemic in northern Europe lies not in strong signatures of natural selection but in shifts in population connectivity and mobility."

This is a good concluding statement. Minor suggestions:

- "most visible genomic legacy" is slightly awkward — something is either a legacy or it is not; "most detectable genomic consequence" might be cleaner.
- The paragraph does not gesture toward future work or the broader geographic question: Is this a northern European pattern only? Would the same analysis in London, Paris, or Hamburg show the same result? A sentence opening this up would strengthen the conclusion.
- The final sentence ends on "interpreting the evolutionary consequences of historical pandemics" — quite broad. Consider tying back to the specific finding: that demographic processes (mobility reduction) can reshape population genetic structure in ways that may be misinterpreted as selection signals if the demographic context is not characterised.

---

## 11. Methodological limitations paragraph — adequate but generic

The limitations paragraph covers imputation bias, stringent filtering, and IBD fragment length. One important caveat is underweighted: the assumption that allele frequency changes from post-BD to present-day are "largely independent of the BD." This assumption underlies the pre-BD vs. present-day GWAS (which provides the strongest signal in the selection analysis). If post-BD recovery involved selective migration into the cities, this assumption would not hold. The paper acknowledges it briefly in the Methods but the Discussion does not address it.
