## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016), it has remained distinct from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Medieval Icelandic law codes restricted the importation of horses (Grágás; Karlsson et al., 1992), and the breed's isolation continues today under Icelandic legislation prohibiting their importation (Act No. 54/1990 on the Importation of Animals, Alþingi). Under this system, any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. The origins of this restriction are often traced to the early Commonwealth period (from 930 CE onwards), although the precise date of its introduction remains uncertain (Stelter, 2014).

More than 1,100 years of unbroken isolation have produced a domestic animal of extraordinary scientific value: one whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone. Alongside this exceptional biological isolation, the Icelandic horse possesses one of the most complete genealogical archives of any domestic animal breed. Formal pedigree registration dates to the early 20th century, when a studbook was established for horses meeting breeding standards; these records were progressively digitised from 1991 and consolidated into WorldFengur, the official international studbook of origin for the breed, opened formally in 2001 (Lorange, 2011). WorldFengur today contains records for more than 300,000 horses across 31 countries (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), with the oldest individual in the pedigree archive a mare born in 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). The ancient genomic time series proposed here will extend this documented history back to the founding event itself, creating a genomic record reaching back to the first Norse settlers, which together with the WorldFengur archive will provide an almost unbroken record of the breed's history spanning more than 1,100 years.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is almost entirely unknown at the genomic level. Twenty-four Viking Age specimens have been sequenced, but all at very low depth of coverage; only two reached sufficient depth to be placed on a global phylogeny, and even then only tentatively (Fages et al., 2019; see Section B for details). We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in a single founding event or through multiple waves, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's distinctive traits — its unique gaits, exceptional hardiness, and characteristic disease susceptibilities — evolved under more than a millennium of isolation. This project will potentially generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using more than 140 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, comprising 24 specimens already available from a previous sampling permission plus approximately 120 additional specimens now requested, and will address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of multiple Norse and British-Celtic populations?

According to Landnámabók, the principal medieval narrative of Iceland's settlement, Norse settlers colonised Iceland in the late 9th century CE as farmers, not raiders. Their ships carried cattle, sheep, and horses not as trading goods but as the biological foundation of the farming communities they intended to establish (Benediktsson, 1968; Stelter, 2014). This colonisation-driven transport meant that livestock were likely selected for hardiness, temperament, and working ability, and that multiple waves of settlers, departing from different regions of the Norse world, likely brought animals reflecting the varied horse populations of those regions.

The founding Icelandic horse population was therefore likely a mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry, shaped by wherever the colonists had lived before crossing the North Atlantic. The applicant's study of early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, *Science*; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows they were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry, with the Norse component drawn disproportionately from southern and western Norway and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and Scotland. Many settlers did not depart directly from Norway but from Norse communities in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where Scandinavian and Celtic-speaking populations had lived alongside each other for a generation or more. A settler from the Hebrides or Ireland would have brought horses drawn from both Scandinavian and native British or Celtic pony stock.

The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds, including small stature, extreme hardiness, and dense coat, has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis" (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998). Molecular evidence supports it. Genotyping of the DMRT3 ambling allele — the gait-keeper mutation responsible for the tölt — in 90 ancient horses from across Europe found it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens, yet absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses (Wutke et al., 2016). It appears instead in two English horses from 850–900 CE from Jorvik, placing its likely origin in Viking Age Britain and suggesting it arrived in Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Phylogenetic analysis places the two ancient Icelandic horse genomes closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain and Viking Age British specimens (Fages et al., 2019). Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock, or in what proportions. This project will answer that question for the first time, using genome-wide data and a reference panel that includes modern Nordlandshest/Lyngen and Shetland Pony individuals (the two breeds most closely connected to the Icelandic horse by morphology and geography), alongside Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh, Eriskay, Fell, and Highland ponies representing the full range of British native breeds, a Faroese Pony for direct North Atlantic comparison, and ancient specimens from Viking Age Britain, Denmark, and Estonia.

**Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Modern genomic analysis of the contemporary Icelandic horse population has already detected two effective population size crashes in the genomes of living horses: one approximately 23 generations ago, consistent with the Laki/Skaftáreldar volcanic famine of 1783–1784 CE (which historical accounts estimate reduced the horse population by approximately 75%), and one during early 20th-century mechanisation; the current effective population size stands at 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). Pedigree analysis provides supporting estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that not all founders contributed equally to later generations (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014).

The genomes of modern horses, however, cannot reveal what happened before the 18th century. The founding bottleneck, the medieval centuries, the catastrophic livestock mortality of the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE), and the Sturlung Age civil conflicts remain genomically invisible. This project provides what modern genomics cannot: a direct, time-resolved genomic record through the full post-settlement history, anchored to Iceland's uniquely precise documentary record. Particularly significant are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki eruption. If specimens from both before and after the eruption are successfully recovered, this will allow direct genomic observation of the population through that demographic crisis, testing whether the historically documented 75% reduction leaves a detectable signal in the ancient genomes. Where the same archaeological contexts have yielded both horse and human remains, comparison with ancient human genomic data will provide additional context for interpreting the horse demographic signals.

The ancient genomes also enable inference from the sex chromosomes, which carry information that autosomal data cannot provide. Y-chromosomal haplotypes extracted from male specimens in the dataset will trace paternal lineage diversity from the founding event to the 19th century, tracking how many distinct stallion lineages contributed to the breed and whether some disappeared entirely during the demographic crises of the medieval and early modern periods. Y-chromosomal analysis of modern stallions has already shown that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed today (Weinzerl, 2024); the ancient time series will reveal when that distinctive paternal diversity was established and whether it contracted with the population crashes. The X chromosome, which has an effective population size approximately three-quarters that of the autosomes under equal sex ratios but is more sensitive to sex-biased breeding, will be analysed separately to detect any asymmetry between the number of breeding stallions and mares that may have accompanied the severe demographic events recorded in the historical record.

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation? And are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unknown or unexpected targets, traits we have not yet thought to look for?

Unlike breeds developed for warfare, racing, or heavy draft — where intense, directional artificial selection for speed, size, or strength has left strong genome-wide signatures (Fages et al., 2019) — the Icelandic horse has historically been shaped by a different combination of forces. According to the historical record, horses in Iceland were valued primarily for hardiness, sure-footedness in volcanic terrain, good temperament, and the ability to perform the tölt and flying pace that made them indispensable for farming and travel (Stelter, 2014; Leifsson, 2018). No breeding programme targeting extreme physical performance, large body size, or combat ability has operated in Iceland (Stelter, 2014). The genomic evidence is consistent with this: selection signals in modern Icelandic horses are concentrated on a small number of gait-associated loci, most prominently DMRT3, rather than distributed across the genome as in breeds under intense performance selection (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Wutke et al., 2016). The result is a breed where natural selection responding to climate, disease, and nutritional stress (documented in the two effective population size crashes recorded in modern genomes; Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024) and relatively modest artificial selection focused on working ability and gaits have acted together across more than a millennium. Disentangling these two forces is one of the distinctive contributions of a temporal genomic dataset: where allele frequency change is gradual and correlates with environmental events such as volcanic famines, natural selection is the more likely driver; where change is rapid and concentrated on specific performance-associated loci, directed breeding is more probable. The Icelandic horse is therefore an unusually good system for separating the genomic signals of environmental adaptation from those of human breeding decisions.

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked through the temporal dataset.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). It is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses, forming a clear ROH island present in over 90% of individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and was already at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century founding horses, with statistical support for positive selection thereafter (Bayes Factor = 8.68; Wutke et al., 2016). The key question is when fixation was reached and how rapidly selection operated. Additional gait loci (STAU2, RELN) suggest the genetic architecture of tölt quality is more complex than a single variant (Sigurðardóttir, 2025); the temporal dataset will track all relevant loci simultaneously.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition present in Icelandic horses at clinically significant frequencies (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). Whether this allele was present from founding or arose later under drift or selection is entirely unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a later development, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity, IBH) is an IgE-mediated allergic skin reaction triggered by *Culicoides* midge bites, and one of the most economically significant welfare problems in the breed. *Culicoides* midges are absent from Iceland, so IBH is not expressed there — but affects up to 50% of Icelandic horses exported to mainland Europe, where they encounter the midge for the first time (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). This means the genetic susceptibility is widespread in the Icelandic population but phenotypically hidden. GWAS studies have identified associated loci on multiple chromosomes including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and on ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012), but modern data alone cannot tell us when these alleles arose, whether they were already present in the founding horses, or whether they accumulated later under isolation. Did the susceptibility alleles come from the Scandinavian founding stock, from the British Isles component, or did they reach their current frequency through drift during one of Iceland's demographic crises? The temporal dataset will address these questions directly, providing the first historical context for a condition with real welfare and economic consequences for the breed. Despite its severe bottleneck history, the Icelandic horse retains 79 distinct MHC haplotypes, all exclusive to the breed and not found in any other horse population, a level of immune diversity that may reflect balancing selection acting across the demographic crises (Holmes et al., 2019). Whether this diversity was present from founding or was maintained by selection is also directly addressable with the temporal dataset.

The targeted tracking of pre-identified variants provides a feasible and interpretable core for this aim. But the temporal dataset will also be used to run genome-wide selection scans across the full time series, searching for additional regions showing signatures of positive or purifying selection that go beyond known trait loci, potentially revealing unexpected targets of natural or human-imposed selection that have not previously been identified in this breed.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* Ancient DNA extraction and sequencing library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well-established methodologies, developed by the aDNA research community over the past two decades and implemented in dedicated clean-room facilities worldwide (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007; Meyer & Kircher, 2010; Schubert et al., 2016; Jónsson et al., 2013; Li & Durbin, 2009). These protocols have been applied in studies of 27 (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, *Science*) and 201 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation). Horse skeletal material from Icelandic archaeological contexts has already been shown to preserve aDNA at excellent levels: the sexing study by Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) recovered a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA (range 0.4–39.8%) across 22 Viking Age specimens, all at low depth of coverage; only two reached sufficient depth to be included in a population-level survey (Fages et al., 2019), at 0.08x and 0.18x autosomal coverage respectively. Five Icelandic horse specimens processed using these same protocols yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA. There is no technical barrier to applying established human aDNA workflows to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted or is being sought at the National Museum of Iceland for more than 140 specimens in total spanning 870–1900 CE, comprising 24 specimens already available from a previous permission plus approximately 120 additional specimens now requested, covering burial mounds (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and open site assemblages from more than 50 archaeological sites across all major regions of Iceland. In northern Iceland, sites include Dalvík/Brimnes (Eyjafjörður), Glaumbær (Skagafjörður), Stærri-Árskógur (Eyjafjörður), Granastaðir (Þingeyjarsýsla), Hofstaðir (near Mývatn), Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur), and Daðastaðir. South Iceland is represented by Hemla (Landeyjahreppur), Traðarholt, Mörk, and Lækur in Flói. East Iceland contributes a large assemblage from Skriðuklaustur monastery (30+ specimens, 1450–1800 CE), together with specimens from Breiðdalur, Seyðisfjörður, and Papey. The Westfjords are represented by Vatnsdalur in Patreksfirði, and the post-medieval capital is covered by the Reykjavík urban sites of Alþingisreitur (4+ specimens, 1600–1800 CE) and Tjarnargata (10+ specimens, 1600–1800 CE). Particularly significant for Aim 2 are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki famine. Five libraries have been prepared from five quality-assessed specimens and are ready to enter Phase 2 immediately.

*Infrastructure.* All wet laboratory work will be conducted in the dedicated ancient DNA cleanroom at deCODE Genetics in Reykjavík. The applicant operates this facility under an existing collaboration agreement, with access to the clean-room, equipment, and dedicated laboratory space. Sequencing will be performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse available as fallback options. Bioinformatic computing will run on the deCODE high-performance computing cluster, with the University of Iceland computing cluster as an alternative if required. No new infrastructure investment is required.

*International collaboration.* The project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), whose laboratory provides the horse imputation reference panel comprising 901 modern horses from more than 100 breeds and approximately 381 ancient horse genomes. For the Aim 1 ancestry comparisons, the modern panel includes breeds relevant to the Norse-Atlantic founding hypothesis: Nordlandshest/Lyngen (2 individuals), Norwegian Fjord (2), Faroese Pony (2), and Shetland Pony (8), alongside the full range of British native pony breeds: Exmoor (3), Dartmoor (6), Welsh Pony (7), Eriskay (3), Fell (2), Dales (2), and Highland Pony (1). Ancient specimens from Viking Age Britain, Denmark (862 CE), and Estonia (832–858 CE) provide the temporal comparators. The applicant is a named co-author on Ahmed et al. (under review), which means the reference panel, the analytical pipeline, and the collaboration with Prof. Orlando are already in place, not dependent on new negotiations. The modern whole-genome data from that study will also allow imputation quality to be validated against high-coverage modern Icelandic horse genomes, strengthening confidence in the imputed ancient dataset. The project is designed to proceed with approximately 60–70 high-quality imputed genomes if the full target of 140+ cannot be recovered.

**Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al., 2012), molecular markers on modern breeds (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), a candidate locus on ancient specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), sex chromosome read ratios for biological sex determination (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019), modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), or low-coverage shotgun sequencing of 22 Viking Age specimens, of which only two reached sufficient depth to be included in a global population survey (Fages et al., 2019, without imputation). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse population: the first to generate data sufficient for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and selection analysis, and the first to recover genomic data spanning the full post-settlement history of any horse population. Its originality operates at four levels.

Geographically, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic, including Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the broader Norse Atlantic world. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient animal population genomics. The only comparable project from Iceland is the parallel human study currently in preparation (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), which covers the same period for the founding human population. The genomic time series can be checked against specific, dated historical events, a resolution that is rarely achievable anywhere in the ancient genomics literature. Crucially, the ancient genomic series complements the WorldFengur pedigree archive (opened 2001; Lorange, 2011), which traces individual lineages back 15 generations to horses born in the 1860s (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). Pedigree analysis has identified 4,547 founders for the Iceland-born population, yet the effective number of ancestors is only 42, reflecting the outsized genetic contribution of a small number of particularly influential lines. Combined with modern SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), the result is a virtually unbroken record from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day. No other domestic animal population offers this combination of archaeological depth, genealogical continuity, modern genomic characterisation, and verified isolation.

As a model system, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions about the genomic consequences of long-term isolation: how founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how sustained inbreeding distributes deleterious variants, and what drives phenotypic selection in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case of this pattern, and this project will trace it empirically through more than a millennium.

Through paired data, the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation) is relevant for two reasons. First, Icelanders and their horses shared the same geographic and demographic isolation for over 1,100 years, making the human and horse populations natural parallels for studying the genomic consequences of long-term bottlenecks. Second, and more concretely, the human study sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, at least 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (kuml) documented in *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial contexts at this combination of temporal depth and sample size has no equivalent in the current ancient genomics literature.

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including sheep, cattle, and dogs, and the work creates a template for future projects on those species.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's role in settlement-era society was profound: a working mount, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and stallion contests, and a companion in death (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). Reconstructing the founding genetics of these animals offers direct insight into the human and environmental history of Iceland's early settlement period.

The Icelandic horse industry is substantial, with approximately 300,000 registered horses across 31 countries worldwide (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and the genetic health of the breed is a practical concern. Sweet itch affects up to 50% of Icelandic horses exported from Iceland (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). Ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context, clarifying when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current levels of genetic uniformity are a product of the original founding or of later management decisions. WorldFengur's integration of DNA parentage verification and individual health records, including mandatory veterinary reporting of disease and treatment for all horses in Iceland, means that the genomic findings from this project can be connected to a living breeding and health surveillance infrastructure (Lorange, 2011). FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations), which manages WorldFengur and sets international breeding standards for the breed, represents a direct non-academic beneficiary of the findings — particularly regarding the historical origins of disease-associated alleles and the long-term management of genetic diversity across the breed's 31 national member associations.

For native breed conservation, the findings carry a relevance that extends beyond Iceland. Ahmed et al. (under review) identified a BRIT ancestry component — preserved Iron Age ancestry from the British Isles — concentrated in critically endangered British native breeds such as the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony. The historical and genetic evidence presented here suggests that horses from the British Isles contributed to the Icelandic founding stock. If so, the Icelandic horse population may have preserved genetic diversity from British native horse populations that has since been severely reduced on the mainland through modern breed formation and population bottlenecks. Both the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony are currently on the critical list of the Rare Breeds Survival Trust, and Exmoor ponies are now widely used in rewilding and conservation grazing projects across northern Europe. Identifying which British-origin genetic variants were carried to Iceland, and tracking whether any are absent or rare in modern British native breeds, is a direct contribution to the conservation genomics of these endangered populations. This opens a line of future work connecting the Icelandic ancient horse dataset to broader conservation and rewilding initiatives across the North Atlantic region.

For global science, the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to evolutionary biology and conservation theory well beyond the Icelandic horse. The methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse occupies a unique position in the genomics of domestic animals. It is not simply an ancient breed, since many breeds have long histories, but an ancient, isolated, and thoroughly documented one. The combination of genomic closure, environmental challenge, and historical precision makes it a rare natural experiment in how populations evolve under sustained isolation.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia, long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) used a dataset of 273 ancient and 10 modern horse genomes to pinpoint the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, documenting rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. Most relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, establishing that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data, confirmed that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds, a pattern fully consistent with over a millennium of gene pool closure. The most comprehensive modern genomic study to date (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024, *BMC Genomics*) used a 670K SNP array in 380 Icelandic horses to estimate a current effective population size of 125 individuals, a genomic inbreeding coefficient (F_ROH) of 0.08–0.20, and clear ROH islands over DMRT3 and several other genes associated with performance and adaptation. Pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014, *Icelandic Agricultural Sciences*) provides supporting estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that a small subset of founders has had an outsized influence on the modern gene pool. Y-chromosomal analysis of 12,896 Icelandic stallions confirmed that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed, with no evidence of recent external introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic stability across the 19th century using museum specimens. What emerges from these studies is a picture of a breed carrying the imprint of its founding, sustained by only a fraction of its original founders, and shaped by a breeding programme that has steadily concentrated genetic material from a handful of highly influential lines.

**Genealogical and pedigree data.** Uniquely among ancient DNA study organisms, the Icelandic horse has an extensive and well-curated genealogical archive that complements the ancient genomic record proposed here. WorldFengur, the official international studbook of origin for the Icelandic horse, was opened formally in 2001 (Lorange, 2011), bringing together pedigree records that date to the early 20th century. It today contains records for more than 300,000 horses across 31 countries (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). The oldest individual on record is a mare born in 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), and pedigree analysis can trace lineages back 15 generations from horses born today. Hreiðarsdóttir et al. (2014) analysed pedigree data from more than 300,000 individuals, microsatellite genotypes from 4,822 horses, and mitochondrial DNA from 443 horses. Their results showed 4,547 recorded founders for the Iceland-born population, but an effective number of founders of only 151 and an effective number of ancestors of only 42 — a small number of highly influential animals has left an outsized mark on the gene pool. Regional structure is present, with Skagafjörður and Hornafjörður maintaining very high within-area genetic material use, but overall population viability has been maintained despite more than a millennium of isolation and severe demographic crashes. The pedigree archive covers approximately 160 years. The present project extends the genomic record back to the founding event in 870 CE, which together with the WorldFengur genealogical data creates a nearly complete picture of the breed's history from settlement to the present day.

**Earlier research on the origins of the Icelandic horse.** Icelandic researchers have long suspected a Norwegian primary ancestry. Eldjárn (1991) proposed the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage on morphological grounds, and Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson (1998) confirmed striking physical resemblance between the two breeds, including the tölt gait, through field comparisons in northern Norway. Five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform the tölt; the physical similarity between the breeds is striking enough that they can be nearly impossible to distinguish by eye. The first molecular evidence for a close genetic relationship between the Icelandic horse and Norwegian breeds comes from microsatellite analysis by Bjørnstad et al. (2003), in which the Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses cluster together in phylogenetic reconstruction with strong bootstrap support. That study frames the Icelandic horse as representing preserved ancient Norwegian horse genetics, a conclusion fully consistent with the historical record of Viking-Age introduction from Norway around 870 CE. A British Isles contribution has also long been considered plausible on historical grounds: Norse settlers were established in Shetland before moving on to Iceland, and horses bred there would have shared ancestry with Norwegian stock. Both the Nordlandshest/Lyngen and the Shetland Pony are represented in the imputation reference panel used in this project, alongside Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh, Eriskay, Fell, and Highland ponies, making it possible for the first time to test the proposed Norwegian and British founding contributions genomically rather than relying on marker data or morphological observation alone. The question of whether British or Celtic ancestry contributed to the Icelandic gene pool cannot be resolved without ancient genomic data from Iceland itself.

**Ancient DNA work on Icelandic horses.** Four studies have generated molecular or genome-scale data from archaeological Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across multiple European sites, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses, with strong statistical evidence for positive selection (Bayes Factor = 8.68). This pattern is consistent with an origin of the ambling allele in Viking Age Britain and its transport to Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts using low-depth shotgun sequencing (range: 1,010–2,180,600 mapped reads; mean endogenous DNA 9.3%), confirming a male bias in burial horses and demonstrating that Icelandic horse skeletal material preserves ancient DNA well. The zooarchaeological context for those same specimens is provided by Leifsson (2018), who documents riding-related skeletal lesions, butchery evidence, and the social protocols governing horse burial in Viking Age Iceland, providing the cultural framework within which the genomic data from this project will be interpreted. Of the 22 specimens, only two (VHR031 from Granastaðir and VHR102 from Berufjörður) had sufficient sequencing depth (0.08x and 0.18x autosomal coverage) to be included in the global ancient horse genome survey of Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*). In that global context, phylogenetic analysis placed these two early Icelandic horses closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse, consistent with a founding stock that included a British Isles component alongside the primary Norwegian ancestry. Campana et al. (2012) demonstrated DNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone specimens. These studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is suitable for ancient DNA analysis. What they cannot provide is the depth or temporal range required for systematic population genomic inference.

**The gap and what makes it addressable now.** Two developments make this project possible at this moment. The first is methodological: the GLIMPSE2 imputation framework (Rubinacci et al., 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3x coverage, reducing sequencing cost per genome by an order of magnitude and making a project of this scale manageable within a postdoctoral fellowship budget. The second is the reference panel: Prof. Orlando's collection of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles from the Viking Age itself, provides the dense reference needed for imputation and the temporal comparators needed for Aim 1. Five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries, yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA, confirm that the material is suitable and that the project is ready to begin.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; computing runs on the deCODE cluster or, if required, on the University of Iceland cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse. The main risk — that some specimens will yield too little endogenous DNA — is reduced by the large initial sample pool of more than 140 specimens and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is enough to support the core population genomic analyses.

**Phase 1: Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10).** Work begins with specimen selection in collaboration with curators at the National Museum of Iceland, prioritising temporal coverage and geographic spread. Teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics; cortical and petrous bone are used where teeth are unavailable. Silica-based DNA extraction is performed in the deCODE dedicated ancient DNA clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing (Meyer & Kircher, 2010). All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (approximately 1 million reads per library) for quality screening. Endogenous DNA content is assessed against the EquCab3 horse reference genome using BWA (Li & Durbin, 2009) after adapter trimming with AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al., 2016), and characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends) are confirmed with mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al., 2013). Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5x target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. Duplicates are removed using Picard MarkDuplicates; base-quality rescaling is applied with mapDamage2. Biological sex is estimated from X- and Y-chromosome read ratios. Contamination control relies on unique dual indexing, damage authentication, and endogenous content thresholds; the standard ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse material.

**Phase 2: Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24).** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023) with Prof. Orlando's reference panel, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) characterise founding ancestry and test competing models of the founding event. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) tracks genealogical relationships across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC, Li & Durbin, 2011; SMC++, Terhorst et al., 2017) reconstructs effective population size through time. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE).

**Phase 3: Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36).** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, Sabeti et al., 2007; iHS, Voight et al., 2006; PBS, Yi et al., 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 and additional gait-associated loci (STAU2, RELN; Sigurðardóttir, 2025), coat colour and vision loci (ASIP, MC1R, PMEL17; Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012), and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

Dr Sigríður Björnsdóttir DVM, PhD, Professor in Equine Science at the Agricultural University of Iceland (Landbúnaðarháskóli Íslands), contributes access to contemporary Icelandic horse material for comparative analysis. Contemporary horse samples are needed to anchor demographic models at the modern end of the temporal series and to provide baseline allele frequencies at the trait-associated loci targeted in Aim 3. Prof. Björnsdóttir's network within the Icelandic horse breeding community ensures access to well-documented, pedigree-registered individuals from diverse regional breeding populations. A high-density SNP array study (670,000 markers) already covers 380 Icelandic horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), providing a solid modern baseline. The modern whole-genome data from Ahmed et al. (under review) extends this further, with genome-wide heterozygosity estimates, fine-scale runs of homozygosity, and more precise population structure inference beyond what the SNP array alone can resolve. Prof. Björnsdóttir's collaboration makes it possible to expand contemporary sampling further as the project requires.

International collaboration is centred on Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), whose track record in ancient horse genomics includes landmark studies of horse domestication (Librado et al., 2021) and five millennia of horse genome evolution (Fages et al., 2019). The working relationship with his laboratory is already established through co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review): the imputation reference panel, the analytical pipeline, and the horse population genomics expertise that Aims 1 and 2 depend on all come from that existing collaboration. The reference panel is well suited to the Icelandic horse project: among the 901 modern genomes it includes Nordlandshest/Lyngen, Norwegian Fjord, Faroese Pony, Shetland Pony, and a comprehensive set of British native pony breeds, covering all the ancestry hypotheses tested in Aim 1. Prof. Orlando hosts one research visit per year at CAGT Toulouse and provides sequencing services as a fallback if capacity is unavailable at deCODE Genetics. A letter of support is included.

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a real research project for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics, a field in which no established training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1: The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology). As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2: Paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík** (archaeology or biological anthropology). The pre-Christian burial ground at Brimnes, Dalvík (Svarfaðardalshreppur) is one of the largest and most extensively studied Viking Age burial sites in Iceland. Discovered in 1908 and excavated in 1909, it yielded thirteen human skeletal remains, eight horse burials, and the remains of three dogs, including one grave in which the deceased had been placed in a sitting position inside a boat. Six of the human individuals have been sequenced in the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), making Brimnes, Dalvík the Icelandic burial site with the richest combination of genomic human data and associated horse burials. The student will examine whether the horses are genetically related to one another, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the human interred alongside it (the protocols governing who received horse burial in Viking Age Iceland are documented by Leifsson, 2018), and whether the Dalvík horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. If the student has a background in zooarchaeology, they will additionally assist in identifying horse remains within the broader osteological collection, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as "animal bones." This screening work would expand the pool of candidate specimens for the project and provide valuable faunal data to the holding institutions.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Six months of salary support is included in the budget. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.

## G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT PLAN

This project builds on an established research programme in ancient genomics and represents a deliberate transition into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD in 2023 at the University of Iceland and has published on ancient Icelandic human genomics, with a companion study of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes currently in preparation. Co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) — a study encompassing 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes characterising the global genomic ancestry of modern horse breeds — marks her move into ancient horse genomics and establishes the international collaboration, the reference panel, and the analytical tools this project will build on.

**Scientific development.** Leading this project will develop her skills in three areas important for her research programme. First, horse-specific population genomic analysis, including selection scans, breed structure inference, and interpretation of functional variants, will expand her range of methods beyond human population genomics. Second, the temporal analysis of trait-associated variants will develop her expertise in connecting genomic data to phenotype, an increasingly central skill in ancient genomics. Third, leading an international collaboration with a large, active laboratory (Orlando/CAGT) will build her experience in managing multi-partner genomic projects.

**Conference participation and international visibility.** She will present results at international ancient DNA and population genomics conferences throughout the project, including the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE) annual meeting, as well as relevant Icelandic scientific meetings.

**Institutional development.** This project strengthens the host institution's position as an active contributor to ancient genomics, a field in which Iceland's unique archaeological and historical resources give it a natural comparative advantage. Training a Master's student creates a concrete local capacity-building outcome.

**Long-term trajectory.** The long-term goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. This project is the scientific foundation of that programme: it generates the core dataset, establishes the key international collaborations, and produces the publication record needed to compete for independent research funding at the European level. The results are designed to form the basis of a future European Research Council application (ERC Starting Grant), which would expand the ancient animal genomics programme to additional Icelandic archaeological species and time periods.
