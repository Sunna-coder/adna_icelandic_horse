## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016), it has remained distinct from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Medieval Icelandic law codes restricted the importation of horses (Grágás; Karlsson et al., 1992), and the breed's isolation continues today under Icelandic legislation prohibiting the importation of horses (Act No. 54/1990 on the Importation of Animals, Alþingi). Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. The origins of this restriction are often traced to the early Commonwealth period (from 930 CE onwards), although the precise date of its introduction remains uncertain (Stelter, 2014).

More than 1,100 years of unbroken isolation have produced a domestic animal of extraordinary scientific value: one whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone. Alongside this exceptional biological isolation, the Icelandic horse possesses one of the most complete genealogical archives of any domestic animal breed. The WorldFengur studbook, the official pedigree database of origin for the breed, was formally established in 1923 and today contains records for more than 300,000 registered horses across 31 countries; the oldest individual on record is a mare born in 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). The ancient genomic time series proposed here will extend this documented history back to the founding event itself, creating a genomic record reaching back to the first Norse settlers, which together with the WorldFengur archive will provide an almost unbroken record of the breed's history spanning more than 1,100 years.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is almost entirely unknown at the genomic level. Twenty-four Viking Age specimens have been sequenced, but all at very low depth of coverage; only two reached sufficient depth to be placed on a global phylogeny, and even then only tentatively (Fages et al., 2019; see Section B for details). We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in a single founding event or through multiple waves, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's distinctive traits, including its unique gaits, exceptional hardiness, and characteristic disease susceptibilities, evolved under more than a millennium of isolation. This project will potentially generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using more than 140 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, comprising 24 specimens already available from a previous sampling permission plus approximately 120 additional specimens now requested, and will address three specific scientific aims.

---

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions?

Norse settlers colonised Iceland in the late 9th century CE as farmers, not raiders. Their ships carried cattle, sheep, and horses not as trading goods but as the biological foundation of the farming communities they intended to establish (Stelter, 2014). This colonisation-driven transport meant that livestock were selected for hardiness, temperament, and working ability, and that multiple waves of settlers, departing from different regions of the Norse world, likely brought animals reflecting the varied horse populations of those regions. [**ACTION NEEDED: add 1–2 sentences drawing on a documentary or historical source about livestock transport in Norse colonisation — Stelter 2014 is the primary reference available; if a stronger source exists for the colonisation-specific context, please suggest it.**]

The founding Icelandic horse population was therefore likely a mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry, shaped by wherever the colonists had lived before crossing the Atlantic. The applicant's study of early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, *Science*; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows they were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry [**ACTION NEEDED: confirm the exact Norse:Gaelic ratio from the in-preparation manuscript before stating it here; if not yet stable, cite only Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018**], with the Norse component drawn disproportionately from southern and western Norway and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and Scotland. Many settlers did not depart directly from Norway but from Norse communities in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where Scandinavian and Celtic-speaking populations had lived alongside each other for a generation or more. A settler from the Hebrides or Ireland would have brought horses drawn from both Scandinavian and native British or Celtic pony stock.

The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds, including small stature, extreme hardiness, and dense coat, has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis" (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998). Molecular evidence supports it. Genotyping of the DMRT3 ambling allele (the gait-keeper mutation responsible for the tölt) in 90 ancient horses from across Europe, including 13 archaeological specimens from Icelandic sites (Wutke et al., 2016), found the allele in 10 of those 13 early Icelandic individuals (77%), yet absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses; it appears instead in two English horses from 850–900 CE (Jorvik), placing its likely origin in Viking Age Britain and indicating it arrived in Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Phylogenetic analysis places the two ancient Icelandic horse genomes closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain and Viking Age British specimens (Fages et al., 2019). Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock, or in what proportions. This project will answer that question for the first time, using genome-wide data and a reference panel that includes ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Viking Age itself.

---

**Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Modern genomic analysis of the contemporary Icelandic horse population has already detected two effective population size crashes in the genomes of living horses: one approximately 23 generations ago, consistent with the Laki/Skaftáreldar volcanic famine of 1783–1784 CE (which historical accounts estimate reduced the horse population by approximately 75%), and one during early 20th-century mechanisation; the current effective population size stands at 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). Pedigree analysis provides complementary estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that not all founders contributed equally to subsequent generations (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014).

The genomes of modern horses, however, cannot reveal what happened before the 18th century. The founding bottleneck, the medieval centuries, the catastrophic livestock mortality of the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE), and the Sturlung Age civil conflicts remain genomically invisible. This project provides what modern genomics cannot: a direct, time-resolved genomic record through the full post-settlement history, calibrated against Iceland's uniquely precise documentary record. Particularly significant are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki eruption. If specimens from both before and after the eruption are successfully recovered, this will allow direct genomic observation of the population through that demographic crisis, testing whether the historically documented 75% reduction leaves a detectable signal in the ancient genomes. Where the same archaeological contexts have yielded both horse and human remains, comparison with ancient human genomic data will provide additional context for interpreting the horse demographic signals.

---

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation?

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked through the temporal dataset.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). It is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses, forming a clear ROH island present in over 90% of individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and was already at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century founding horses, with statistical support for positive selection thereafter (Bayes Factor = 8.68; Wutke et al., 2016). The key question is when fixation was reached and how rapidly selection operated. Additional gait loci (STAU2, RELN) suggest the genetic architecture of tölt quality is more complex than a single variant (Sigurðardóttir, 2025); the temporal dataset will track all relevant loci simultaneously.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition present in Icelandic horses at clinically significant frequencies (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). Whether this allele was present from founding or arose later under drift or selection is entirely unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a later development, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), one of the most economically significant conditions affecting the breed, has GWAS-identified loci on ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, and ECA18 (Shrestha et al., 2015) and on ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012). [**ACTION NEEDED: verify these chromosome assignments against the original papers before submission.**] The temporal dataset will track allele frequencies at these loci, addressing whether disease-associated variants rose to high frequency during the founding bottleneck or under later drift or selection. Despite its severe bottleneck history, the Icelandic horse reportedly retains 79 distinct MHC haplotypes (Maccari et al., 2019). [**ACTION NEEDED: this figure could not be verified from available background files; please confirm against the primary source before submission.**] Whether this immune diversity was present from founding or has been maintained by balancing selection is directly addressable with the temporal dataset.

Aim 3 tracks pre-identified genetic variants rather than attempting discovery by GWAS. This is important for feasibility at this sample size and connects the findings directly to the breed's documented health and performance concerns.

**Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al., 2012), molecular markers on modern breeds (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), a candidate locus on ancient specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), sex chromosome read ratios for biological sex determination (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019), modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), or low-coverage shotgun sequencing of 22 Viking Age specimens, of which only two reached sufficient depth to be included in a global population survey (Fages et al., 2019, without imputation). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse population: the first to generate data sufficient for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and selection analysis, and the first to recover genomic data spanning the full post-settlement history of any horse population. Its originality operates at four levels.

*Geographically*, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic, including Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the broader Norse Atlantic world. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

*Temporally*, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient animal population genomics — the only comparable project from Iceland is the parallel human study currently in preparation (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), which covers the same period for the founding human population. The genomic time series can be calibrated against specific, dated historical events, a resolution that is rarely achievable anywhere in the ancient genomics literature. Crucially, the ancient genomic series complements the WorldFengur pedigree archive (established 1923), which traces individual lineages back 15 generations to horses born in the 1860s (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). Pedigree analysis has identified 4,547 founders for the Iceland-born population, yet the effective number of ancestors is only 42, reflecting the disproportionate genetic contribution of a small number of particularly prolific lines. Combined with modern SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), the result is a virtually unbroken record from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day. No other domestic animal population offers this combination of archaeological depth, genealogical continuity, modern genomic characterisation, and verified isolation.

*As a model system*, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions about the genomic consequences of long-term isolation: how founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how sustained inbreeding distributes deleterious variants, and what drives phenotypic selection in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case of this pattern, and this project will trace it empirically through more than a millennium.

*Through paired data*, the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation) is directly relevant for two reasons. First, Icelanders and their horses shared the same geographic and demographic isolation for over 1,100 years, making the human and horse populations natural parallels for studying the genomic consequences of long-term bottlenecks. Second, and more concretely, the human study sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, at least 17 of which [**ACTION NEEDED: verify this number against the cross-reference file before submission**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (*kuml*) documented in *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial contexts, at this temporal depth and sample size, is without parallel in the current ancient genomics literature. ## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse occupies a unique position in the genomics of domestic animals. It is not simply an ancient breed, since many breeds have long histories, but an ancient, isolated, and thoroughly documented one. The combination of genomic closure, environmental challenge, and historical precision makes it a rare natural experiment in how populations evolve under sustained isolation.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia, long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) used a large dataset of ancient and modern horse genomes [**ACTION NEEDED: verify the specific number of ancient genomes in this study against the published paper.**] to pinpoint the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, documenting rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. Most directly relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, and establishes that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data, confirmed that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds, a pattern fully consistent with over a millennium of gene pool closure. The most comprehensive modern genomic study to date (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024, *BMC Genomics*) used a 670K SNP array in 380 Icelandic horses to estimate a current effective population size of 125 individuals, a genomic inbreeding coefficient (F_ROH) of 0.08–0.20, and clear ROH islands over DMRT3 and several other genes associated with performance and adaptation. Pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014, *Icelandic Agricultural Sciences*) provides complementary estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that a small subset of founders has disproportionately shaped the modern gene pool. Y-chromosomal analysis of 12,896 Icelandic stallions confirmed that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed, with no evidence of recent external introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic stability across the 19th century using museum specimens. Together, these studies establish a picture of a breed carrying the imprint of its founding, sustained by only a fraction of its original founders, and shaped by a breeding programme that has steadily concentrated genetic material from a handful of highly influential lines.

**Genealogical and pedigree data.** Uniquely among ancient DNA study organisms, the Icelandic horse has an extensive and well-curated genealogical archive that directly complements the ancient genomic record proposed here. WorldFengur, the official studbook of origin for the Icelandic horse, was formally established in 1923 following the development of regional breeding societies in the late 19th century, and today contains pedigree records for more than 300,000 registered horses across 31 countries (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014; Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). The oldest individual on record is a mare born in 1860, and pedigree analysis can trace lineages back 15 generations from horses born today. Hreiðarsdóttir et al. (2014) used pedigree data from more than 300,000 individuals together with microsatellite genotyping of 4,822 horses and mitochondrial DNA sequencing of 443 horses to characterise the population's structure and founders: 4,547 founders are recorded for the Iceland-born population, but the effective number of founders is only 151 and the effective number of ancestors only 42, demonstrating that a small number of highly influential animals has disproportionately shaped the modern gene pool. Regional structure is present, with Skagafjörður and Hornafjörður maintaining very high within-area genetic material use, but overall population viability has been maintained despite more than a millennium of isolation and severe demographic crashes. The pedigree archive covers approximately 160 years. The present project extends the genomic record back to the founding event in 870 CE, which together with the WorldFengur genealogical data creates a nearly complete picture of the breed's history from settlement to the present day.

**Earlier research on the origins of the Icelandic horse.** Icelandic researchers have long suspected a Norwegian primary ancestry. Eldjárn (1991) proposed the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage on morphological grounds, and Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson (1998) confirmed striking physical resemblance between the two breeds, including the tölt gait, through field comparisons in northern Norway. Five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform the tölt; the physical similarity between the breeds is striking enough that they can be nearly impossible to distinguish by eye. The first molecular evidence for a close relationship between the Icelandic horse, the Shetland pony, and the Nordland/Lyngen horse comes from genetic marker studies. The question of whether British or Celtic ancestry also contributed cannot be resolved without ancient genomic data from Iceland itself.

**Ancient DNA work on Icelandic horses.** Four studies have generated molecular or genome-scale data from archaeological Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across multiple European sites, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses, with strong statistical evidence for positive selection (Bayes Factor = 8.68). This pattern is consistent with an origin of the ambling allele in Viking Age Britain and its transport to Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts using low-depth shotgun sequencing (range: 1,010–2,180,600 mapped reads; mean endogenous DNA 9.3%), confirming a male bias in burial horses and demonstrating that Icelandic horse skeletal material preserves ancient DNA at excellent levels. The zooarchaeological context for those same specimens is provided by Leifsson (2018), who documents riding-related skeletal lesions, butchery evidence, and the social protocols governing horse burial in Viking Age Iceland, providing the cultural framework within which the genomic data from this project will be interpreted. Of the 22 specimens, only two (VHR031 from Granastaðir and VHR102 from Berufjörður) had sufficient sequencing depth (0.08x and 0.18x autosomal coverage) to be included in the global ancient horse genome survey of Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*). In that global context, phylogenetic analysis placed these two early Icelandic horses closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse, consistent with a founding stock that included a British Isles component alongside the primary Norwegian ancestry. Campana et al. (2012) demonstrated DNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone specimens. Together, these studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is tractable for ancient DNA analysis. What they cannot provide is the depth or temporal range required for systematic population genomic inference.

**The gap and what makes it addressable now.** Two developments make this project possible at this moment. The first is methodological: the GLIMPSE2 imputation framework (Rubinacci et al., 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3x coverage, reducing sequencing cost per genome by an order of magnitude and making a project of this scale tractable within a postdoctoral fellowship budget. The second is the reference panel: Prof. Orlando's collection of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles from the Viking Age itself, provides the dense reference needed for imputation and the direct comparators needed for Aim 1. Five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries, yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA, confirm that the material is suitable and that the project is ready to begin.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; computing runs on the deCODE cluster or, if required, on the University of Iceland cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CEH Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse. The principal risk of low endogenous DNA in some specimens is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool of more than 140 specimens and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, sufficient for robust population genomic inference.

**Phase 1: Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10).** The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread. Teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics; cortical and petrous bone are used where teeth are unavailable. Silica-based DNA extraction is performed in the deCODE dedicated ancient DNA clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing (Meyer & Kircher, 2010). All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (approximately 1 million reads per library) for quality screening: endogenous DNA content is assessed against the EquCab3 horse reference genome using BWA (Li & Durbin, 2009) after adapter trimming with AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al., 2016), and characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends) are confirmed with mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al., 2013). Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5x target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. Duplicates are removed using Picard MarkDuplicates; base-quality rescaling is applied with mapDamage2. Biological sex is estimated from X- and Y-chromosome read ratios. Contamination control relies on unique dual indexing, damage authentication, and endogenous content thresholds; the standard ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse material.

**Phase 2: Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24).** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023) with Prof. Orlando's reference panel, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) characterise founding ancestry and test competing models of the founding event. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) tracks genealogical relationships across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC, Li & Durbin, 2011; SMC++, Terhorst et al., 2017) reconstructs effective population size through time. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE).

**Phase 3: Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36).** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, Sabeti et al., 2007; iHS, Voight et al., 2006; PBS, Yi et al., 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 and additional gait-associated loci (STAU2, RELN; Sigurðardóttir, 2025), coat colour and vision loci (ASIP, MC1R, PMEL17; Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012), and the MHC region (Maccari et al., 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics, a field in which no training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1: The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology). As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2: Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** (archaeology or biological anthropology). The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves, all four of which have been sequenced in the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), and two separate horse burials associated with warrior graves (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016). [**ACTION NEEDED: confirm the specific grave equipment details against *Kuml og haugfé* or the site report before submission — sword/axes/spearhead in one grave, bridle bit and iron fittings in another.**] This makes Sílastaðir one of the most precisely documented assemblages of co-deposited human and horse remains in Iceland. The student will examine whether the two horses are genetically related, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the human interred alongside it (the protocols governing who received horse burial in Viking Age Iceland are documented by Leifsson, 2018), and whether the Sílastaðir horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. If the student has a background in zooarchaeology, they will additionally assist in identifying horse remains within the broader osteological collection, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as "animal bones." This taxonomic screening work would directly expand the pool of candidate specimens for the project and provide valuable faunal data to the holding institutions.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Six months of salary support is included in the budget. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills directly applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.
