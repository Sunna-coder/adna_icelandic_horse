## 2. What is known about the origin of the Icelandic horse?

The honest answer is: **surprisingly little at the genomic level** — which is the central justification for the project. What is currently established:

- The **NORD ancestry component** (Ahmed et al., under review, co-authored by Sunna) emerged around **810 CE**, directly in the Viking Age. Modern Icelandic horses are among the purest carriers of this ancestry. This links the founding stock firmly to the Norse world but does not specify which part — Norway, Orkney, the Hebrides, or a mixture.

- **Modern Icelandic horses show very low genetic diversity** consistent with a small founding population and over 1,100 years of strict isolation (Petersen et al. 2013). This tells us the founding event was relatively small, but not where the founders came from.

- The **only ancient DNA study of Icelandic horses** (Nistelberger/Pálsdóttir et al. 2019) determined the sex of 22 Viking Age specimens but did not generate population genomic data. The founding ancestry is completely unknown at the genomic level.

- **The Landnámabók** (the medieval record of the Icelandic settlement) mentions specific settlers' horses — sometimes by name, sometimes by colour — providing documentary evidence that horses were deliberately transported to Iceland as part of the colonisation. This is a primary source that anchors the project in written history.

**Key open questions this project will answer for the first time:**
- Were the founding horses purely Scandinavian (western Norway) or did they include horses with Celtic/British ancestry from Norse settlements in the British Isles?
- Was there one founding event or multiple waves of horse introduction during the settlement period?
- How many horses actually founded the Icelandic population (effective founding size)?
- How quickly did the population lose diversity after the founding?

## 3. Were the horses in Orkney and Britain the same as in Norway?

**No — almost certainly not.** This is a scientifically important distinction.

By the Viking Age, Norse settlers had been living in Orkney, Shetland, and the Hebrides for several generations. The horses in these areas would have been a mixture of:

- **Norse imports** brought by the original Scandinavian settlers
- **Native British and Celtic ponies** already present in the British Isles — the ancestors of the modern Shetland pony, Highland pony, and Exmoor — which were small, hardy, and well-adapted to Atlantic island conditions

The Icelandic horse shares several physical characteristics with these native British island ponies: small stature, exceptional hardiness, a thick double coat, and tolerance for harsh winters. This similarity has long suggested to some researchers that **Celtic or British pony ancestry may form part of the Icelandic horse's founding stock** — a hypothesis sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis."

**This hypothesis has never been tested with ancient genomic data.** Your project can test it directly: if horses with Celtic/British pony ancestry were among those brought to Iceland by settlers departing from Orkney or the Hebrides, their genomic signal should be detectable in the earliest Icelandic horse genomes, distinguishable from horses brought directly from western Norway.

The Iceland2 manuscript also includes **thirteen Orcadian human genomes from the Iron and Viking Ages (600–1300 CE)**, providing a comparative human genomic dataset from the key Norse-Gaelic contact zone. There are no equivalent ancient horse genomic data from Orkney. This is a gap that future work could address, and it further contextualises why the Icelandic horse founding ancestry question is scientifically open.

## 4. The critical parallel: mixed human ancestry implies potentially mixed horse ancestry

This is perhaps the most important scientific framing for Aim 1.

Your own Science 2018 paper and the Iceland2 manuscript establish that **early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry** — many settlers came not directly from Norway, but via Orkney, the Hebrides, Shetland, and Ireland, where Norse populations had been living alongside Celtic-speaking people for a generation or more. This is confirmed genomically: Norse ancestry is highest among settlement-era Icelanders, but Gaelic ancestry is substantial and higher during the earliest settlement period.

**The logical implication:** a settler departing from the Hebrides or Orkney would have brought whatever horses they owned locally — which may already have included Celtic or British pony ancestry alongside any Scandinavian stock. **If the people were mixed Norse-Gaelic, their horses very plausibly were too.**

This makes the founding horse ancestry question directly analogous to the founding human ancestry question — and it gives Aim 1 a sharper, testable hypothesis:

> *The genomic composition of the founding Icelandic horse population will reflect the mixed Norse-Atlantic origins of the settlers, with ancestry contributions not only from Scandinavian horse populations but potentially also from horses of Celtic or British origin in the Norse-controlled Atlantic islands.*

This is added to Section A.2 of the Rannís draft. It is a much stronger framing than simply asking "where did they come from?" — it predicts a specific pattern and explains why that pattern would be scientifically interesting.

## 5. Did Vikings bring horses when raiding and trading?

This distinction is important and often misunderstood:

**Raiding: generally no.** The Irish annals and Anglo-Saxon chronicles consistently record that Viking raiding parties would land on shore and then **steal or commandeer horses locally** to conduct raids inland. Horses were not typically transported across the sea for raiding — they were acquired at the destination. This means that Viking raids, despite their geographic reach, did not significantly move horse genetics between regions.

**Colonisation: yes, deliberately.** Settlement was entirely different. Settlers brought livestock — horses, cattle, sheep, goats — from wherever they departed. This is documented in the Landnámabók, in zooarchaeological finds across Iceland, and in the prevalence of horse burials in settlement-era Iceland. Horses were essential agricultural animals; you could not farm or travel in Iceland without them.

**Trade: yes, selectively.** Horses were traded within the Norse world, particularly high-quality animals for wealth display and competitive horse fights (*hestamót*). Horse fights are extensively documented in the Icelandic sagas and were a major social institution in the Viking Age.

**Burial: yes.** As your own work documents, horses were buried with high-status individuals throughout the Norse world. The sex bias toward male horses in Icelandic burial contexts (Nistelberger/Pálsdóttir 2019) suggests stallions were particularly valued for burial. Given that the same practice occurs in Norway and Orkney, the horses buried in Iceland likely reflect the same cultural tradition brought from the settlers' places of origin.

**The implication for the proposal:** the founding Icelandic horse population derives specifically from deliberate colonisation events — not from the diffuse, incidental mixing associated with raiding. This means the founding horses trace to specific departure points (western Norway, Orkney, Hebrides, Ireland), which are identifiable in principle from ancient genomic data. This is why the Icelandic horse founding ancestry question is tractable with the methods proposed here.
