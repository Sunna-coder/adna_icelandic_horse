## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers beginning around 870 CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016), it has remained distinct from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Medieval Icelandic law codes restricted the importation of horses (Grágás; Karlsson et al., 1992), and the breed's isolation continues today under Icelandic legislation prohibiting their importation (Act No. 54/1990 on the Importation of Animals, Alþingi). Under this system, any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. The origins of this restriction are often traced to the early Commonwealth period (from 930 CE onwards), although the precise date of its introduction remains uncertain (Stelter, 2014).

More than 1,100 years of unbroken isolation have produced a domestic animal of extraordinary scientific value: one whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone. Alongside this exceptional biological isolation, the Icelandic horse possesses one of the most complete genealogical archives of any domestic animal breed. Formal pedigree registration dates to the early 20th century, when a studbook was established for horses meeting breeding standards; these records were progressively digitised from 1991 and consolidated into WorldFengur, the official international studbook of origin for the breed, opened formally in 2001 (Lorange, 2011). WorldFengur contained records for more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries as of 2010, of which approximately 220,000 were living at that time (Lorange, 2011), with the oldest individual in the pedigree archive a mare born in 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). The ancient genomic time series proposed here will extend this documented history back to the founding event itself, creating a genomic record reaching back to the first Norse settlers, which together with the WorldFengur archive will provide an almost unbroken record of the breed's history spanning more than 1,100 years.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is almost entirely unknown at the genomic level. Twenty-four Viking Age specimens have been sequenced, but all at very low depth of coverage; only two reached sufficient depth to be placed on a global phylogeny, and even then only tentatively (Fages et al., 2019; see Section B for details). We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in a single founding event or through multiple waves, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's distinctive traits — its unique gaits, exceptional hardiness, and characteristic disease susceptibilities — evolved under more than a millennium of isolation. This project will potentially generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using more than 140 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, comprising 24 specimens already available from a previous sampling permission plus approximately 120 additional specimens now requested, and will address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of populations from different parts of the Norse world?

According to Landnámabók (Benediktsson, 1968), Norse settlers colonised Iceland beginning around 870 CE. Their ships carried livestock, including horses, as the biological foundation of the farming communities they intended to establish (Stelter, 2014). Multiple waves of settlers departed from different parts of the Norse world, and would have brought animals from the varied horse populations of those regions.

The applicant's study of early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, *Science*; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows they had mixed Norse and Gaelic ancestry, including settlers who came from Norse communities established in the British Isles — Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland. The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds, including small stature, extreme hardiness, and dense coat, has long suggested a contribution from the British Isles, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis" (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998). Genomic evidence points in the same direction: genotyping of the DMRT3 ambling allele in 90 ancient horses found it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens, yet largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses, while it appears in two horses dated to 850–900 CE from Jorvik (York, England), suggesting the allele arrived in Iceland with settlers coming from or through Britain (Wutke et al., 2016). Phylogenetic analysis places the two ancient Icelandic horse genomes with sufficient coverage closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain and Viking Age British specimens (Fages et al., 2019). Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve whether horses with British Isles ancestry also contributed to the founding stock, or in what proportions.

This project will answer that question for the first time, using genome-wide data from more than 140 ancient Icelandic horse specimens and an imputation reference panel described in Section B. The panel includes modern representatives of all the breeds implicated by the historical and genomic evidence — Norwegian, Faroese, and British native breeds — alongside ancient specimens from Viking Age Scandinavia and Britain spanning the centuries immediately before Iceland's settlement.

**Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Modern genomic analysis of the contemporary Icelandic horse population has already detected two effective population size crashes in the genomes of living horses: one approximately 23 generations ago, consistent with the Laki/Skaftáreldar volcanic famine of 1783–1784 CE (which historical accounts estimate reduced the horse population by approximately 75%), and one during early 20th-century mechanisation; the current effective population size stands at 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). Pedigree analysis provides supporting estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that not all founders contributed equally to later generations (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014).

The genomes of modern horses, however, cannot reveal what happened before the 18th century. The founding bottleneck, the medieval centuries, the catastrophic livestock mortality of the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE), and the Sturlung Age civil conflicts remain genomically invisible. This project provides what modern genomics cannot: a direct, time-resolved genomic record through the full post-settlement history, anchored to Iceland's uniquely precise documentary record. Particularly significant are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki eruption. If specimens from both before and after the eruption are successfully recovered, this will allow direct genomic observation of the population through that demographic crisis, testing whether the historically documented 75% reduction leaves a detectable signal in the ancient genomes.

At several sites, the same archaeological contexts that yielded horse remains have also yielded human remains sequenced in the applicant's parallel ancient genomics study. Parallel demographic signals from two species sharing the same environment — but differing in how closely their population sizes track human management decisions — can help distinguish population-wide environmental catastrophe from horse-specific management changes.

The ancient genomes also enable inference from the sex chromosomes. Y-chromosomal haplotypes extracted from male specimens will trace paternal lineage diversity from the founding event to the 19th century, tracking how many distinct stallion lineages contributed to the breed and whether some disappeared during the demographic crises of the medieval and early modern periods. Y-chromosomal analysis of modern Icelandic stallions has shown that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed today (Weinzerl, 2024); the ancient time series will reveal when that distinctive paternal diversity was established and whether it contracted with the population crashes. The X chromosome, with its smaller effective population size and sensitivity to sex-biased breeding, will be analysed separately to detect any asymmetry between the number of breeding stallions and mares that may have accompanied the severe demographic events recorded in the historical record.

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation? And are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unknown or unexpected targets?

Unlike breeds developed for warfare, racing, or heavy draft — where intense, directional artificial selection for speed, size, or strength has left strong genome-wide signatures (Fages et al., 2019) — the Icelandic horse has historically been shaped by a different combination of forces. According to the historical record, horses in Iceland were valued primarily for hardiness, sure-footedness in volcanic terrain, good temperament, and the ability to perform the tölt and flying pace (Stelter, 2014; Leifsson, 2018). The genomic evidence is consistent with this: selection signals in modern Icelandic horses are concentrated on a small number of gait-associated loci, most prominently DMRT3, rather than distributed across the genome as in breeds under intense performance selection (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Wutke et al., 2016). The result is a breed where natural selection responding to climate, disease, and nutritional stress and relatively modest artificial selection for working ability and gaits have acted together across more than a millennium. Disentangling these two forces is one of the distinctive contributions of a temporal genomic dataset: where allele frequency change is gradual and correlates with environmental events such as volcanic famines, natural selection is the more likely driver; where change is rapid and concentrated on specific performance-associated loci, directed breeding is more probable.

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked through the temporal dataset.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). It is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses, forming a clear ROH island present in over 90% of individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and was already at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century founding horses with evidence for positive selection thereafter (Wutke et al., 2016). The key question is when fixation was reached and how rapidly selection operated. Additional gait loci (STAU2, RELN) suggest the genetic architecture of tölt quality is more complex than a single variant (Sigurðardóttir, 2025); the temporal dataset will track all relevant loci simultaneously.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition present in Icelandic horses at clinically significant frequencies (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). Whether this allele was present from founding or arose later under drift or selection is entirely unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a later development, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity, IBH) is an IgE-mediated allergic skin reaction triggered by *Culicoides* midge bites, and one of the most economically significant welfare problems in the breed. *Culicoides* midges are absent from Iceland, so IBH is not expressed there — but affects up to 50% of Icelandic horses exported to mainland Europe (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). GWAS studies have identified associated loci on multiple chromosomes including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012). The temporal dataset will address when these alleles arose, whether they were already present in the founding horses, and whether they accumulated under isolation. Despite its bottleneck history, the Icelandic horse retains 79 distinct MHC haplotypes exclusive to the breed, suggesting balancing selection across the demographic crises (Holmes et al., 2019).

*Metabolic disease susceptibility.* The Icelandic horse is prone to Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS), characterised by insulin dysregulation, obesity, and laminitis susceptibility. FOXO1 on ECA17, a prominent ROH island shared by Icelandic horses and Exmoor ponies, may reflect positive selection for metabolic thriftiness under Iceland's repeated famines (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). HMGA2 on ECA6, the most replicated EMS-associated locus across native pony breeds, is a further candidate (Norton et al., 2019). Tracking the frequency of these variants through the temporal dataset, and correlating changes with documented famine periods, provides a direct test of the thriftiness hypothesis.

The targeted tracking of pre-identified variants provides a feasible core for this aim. The temporal dataset will also support genome-wide selection scans across the full time series, searching for additional regions showing signatures of positive or purifying selection — potentially revealing unexpected targets of natural or human-imposed selection not previously identified in this breed.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* Ancient DNA extraction and sequencing library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well-established methodologies implemented in dedicated clean-room facilities worldwide (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007; Meyer & Kircher, 2010; Schubert et al., 2016; Jónsson et al., 2013; Li & Durbin, 2009). These protocols have been applied in studies of 27 (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, *Science*) and 201 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation). Horse skeletal material from Icelandic archaeological contexts preserves aDNA at excellent levels: Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) recovered a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA (range 0.4–39.8%) across 22 Viking Age specimens. Five Icelandic horse specimens processed using these same protocols yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA. There is no technical barrier to applying established aDNA workflows to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted or is being sought at the National Museum of Iceland for more than 140 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, comprising 24 specimens already available plus approximately 120 additional specimens now requested, covering burial mounds (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and open site assemblages from more than 50 archaeological sites across all major regions of Iceland. Particularly significant for Aim 2 are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki famine. Five libraries have been prepared from five quality-assessed specimens and are ready to enter Phase 2 immediately.

*Infrastructure.* All wet laboratory work will be conducted in the dedicated ancient DNA cleanroom at deCODE Genetics in Reykjavík. The applicant operates this facility under an existing collaboration agreement, with access to the clean-room, equipment, and dedicated laboratory space. Sequencing will be performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse available as fallback options. Bioinformatic computing will run on the deCODE high-performance computing cluster, with the University of Iceland computing cluster as an alternative if required. No new infrastructure investment is required.

*International collaboration.* The project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), whose laboratory has built a haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horses from more than 100 breeds globally, plus an associated dataset of 376 previously published ancient horse genomes imputed to high density using that panel. For Aim 1, the panel includes modern representatives of all the breeds most relevant to the Norse-Atlantic founding hypothesis; the ancient specimens include comparators from Viking Age Britain, Denmark (862 CE), and Estonia (832–858 CE). The applicant is a named co-author on Ahmed et al. (under review), which means the reference panel, the analytical pipeline, and the collaboration are already in place. The project is designed to proceed with approximately 60–70 high-quality imputed genomes if the full target of 140+ cannot be recovered.

**Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al., 2012), microsatellite markers and pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), a candidate locus on ancient specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), sex chromosome read ratios for biological sex determination (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019), modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), or low-coverage shotgun sequencing of 22 Viking Age specimens, of which only two reached sufficient depth to be included in a global population survey (Fages et al., 2019, without imputation). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse population: the first to generate data sufficient for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and selection analysis, and the first to recover genomic data spanning the full post-settlement history of any horse population. Its originality operates at four levels.

Geographically, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic, including Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the broader Norse Atlantic world. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient animal population genomics. The genomic time series can be checked against specific, dated historical events — a resolution that is rarely achievable anywhere in the ancient genomics literature. Combined with the WorldFengur pedigree archive, SNP array data from 380 modern horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and modern whole-genome data from Ahmed et al. (under review), the result is a virtually unbroken record from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day. No other domestic animal population offers this combination of archaeological depth, genealogical continuity, modern genomic characterisation, and verified isolation.

As a model system, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions about the genomic consequences of long-term isolation: how founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how sustained inbreeding distributes deleterious variants, and what drives phenotypic selection in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham, 1997), and the Icelandic horse is an extreme case; however, given that the breed became isolated before many dominant modern bloodlines took their current form, it may also retain ancestral variation that has since been reduced elsewhere.

Through paired data, the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation) sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, at least 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project. Paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial contexts at this combination of temporal depth and sample size has no equivalent in the current ancient genomics literature.

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including sheep, cattle, and dogs.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's role in settlement-era society was profound: a working mount, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and stallion contests, and a companion in death (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). Reconstructing the founding genetics of these animals offers direct insight into the human and environmental history of Iceland's early settlement period.

The Icelandic horse industry is substantial, with more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries (Lorange, 2011), and the genetic health of the breed is a practical concern. Sweet itch affects up to 50% of exported horses (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019) and ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will clarify when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current genetic uniformity reflects the original founding or later management decisions. Collaboration with FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations, founded 1969) and Landssamband hestamannafélaga (founded 1949), whose WorldFengur database integrates DNA parentage verification and health records across 31 countries, provides a direct channel to connect these findings to breeders.

For native breed conservation, Ahmed et al. (under review) identified a BRIT ancestry component — preserved Iron Age ancestry from the British Isles — concentrated in critically endangered British native breeds such as the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony. If horses from the British Isles contributed to the Icelandic founding stock, the Icelandic horse may have preserved genetic diversity that has since been severely reduced on the mainland. Both the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony are on the critical list of the Rare Breeds Survival Trust; identifying which British-origin genetic variants were carried to Iceland and tracking whether any are absent or rare in modern British native breeds is a direct contribution to their conservation genomics.

The long-term integrity of the Icelandic gene pool is not guaranteed. Ongoing discussions about Iceland's relationship with the EU raise the question of whether the current importation ban could come under pressure from EU animal movement regulations. The genomic baseline established here would provide an irreplaceable record of the breed's genetic heritage and a scientific foundation for evidence-based policy.

For global science, the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to evolutionary biology and conservation theory well beyond the Icelandic horse.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

Today approximately 60 million horses live on the planet (FAO, 2024), representing more than 500 recognised breeds worldwide (Petersen et al., 2013). Modern horse populations, however, are dominated by a handful of intensively bred bloodlines — Thoroughbred, Arabian, American Quarter Horse — whose genomic landscape is increasingly well characterised. The Icelandic horse became isolated around 870 CE, before many of these dominant modern breeds took their current form, and is therefore likely to retain ancestral genetic diversity that has been substantially reduced or lost in the most widely sequenced modern bloodlines (Ahmed et al., under review).

The Icelandic horse occupies a unique position among domestic animals. It is not simply an ancient breed — many breeds have long histories — but an ancient, isolated, and thoroughly documented one. The combination of genomic closure, environmental challenge, and historical precision makes it a rare natural experiment in how populations evolve under sustained isolation.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia, long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) used a dataset of 273 ancient and 10 modern horse genomes to identify the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes (in present-day southern Russia, north of the Caspian Sea) as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, documenting rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with the formation of modern breeds. Most relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern horse genomes worldwide, with 376 previously published ancient horse genomes imputed using this panel, establishing that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE. The modern genomic landscape is now well resolved; what has never been done is to generate population-level ancient genomic data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population. Comparison of ancient and modern Icelandic horses, and of ancient Icelandic horses against the Ahmed et al. reference dataset, will be central to this project.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** The most comprehensive modern genomic study to date (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024, *BMC Genomics*) genotyped 380 Icelandic horses on a 670K SNP array, estimating a current effective population size of 125 individuals, a genomic inbreeding coefficient (F_ROH) of 0.08–0.20, and clear regions of homozygosity over DMRT3 and several other genes associated with performance and adaptation. Notably, these 380 horses represent a rich resource — including SNP genotypes across 580,000 markers — that has not yet been analysed in the context of global horse variation or ancient horse genomes; doing so is a core component of the present project. Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data from 814 horses across 36 breeds, found that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds — a pattern fully consistent with over a millennium of gene pool closure. Intriguingly, however, and consistent with Agnar Helgason's observation that the Icelandic horses cluster close to ancient horses in global MDS space (Ahmed et al., under review), the breed may retain surprising levels of ancestral diversity given its bottleneck history, since its isolation predates the intensive selective breeding that has dramatically narrowed the genetic base of many dominant modern bloodlines.

Pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014) using records for more than 300,000 individuals, microsatellite genotypes from 4,822 horses, and mitochondrial DNA from 443 horses shows 4,547 recorded founders for the Iceland-born population, though not all of these were necessarily pure Icelandic in origin (some may reflect introductions before the modern ban). The effective number of founders is only 151 and the effective number of ancestors only 42, indicating that a small subset of horses has had an outsized influence on the modern gene pool. Y-chromosomal analysis of Icelandic stallions — with 72 stallions directly genotyped, and genealogical extrapolation to the broader population — confirmed that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed today, with no evidence of recent external introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated that mitochondrial DNA haplotype frequencies were stable across a sample of 19th-century museum specimens, suggesting the breed's maternal lineage composition was already well established by that point. What emerges from these studies is a picture of a breed carrying the genomic imprint of its founding, sustained by a small number of highly influential lines.

**WorldFengur and the pedigree archive.** WorldFengur, the official international studbook of origin for the Icelandic horse, was opened formally in 2001 (Lorange, 2011), bringing together pedigree records that date to the early 20th century. By 2010 it contained records for more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries, of which approximately 220,000 were living (Lorange, 2011), with the oldest individual on record a mare born in 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). The pedigree archive covers approximately 160 years. The present project extends the genomic record back to the founding event in 870 CE, which together with the WorldFengur data creates a nearly complete picture of the breed's history from settlement to the present day.

**Origins and ancient DNA.** Researchers have long suspected a Norwegian primary ancestry. Eldjárn (1991) proposed the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage on morphological grounds, and Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson (1998) noted striking morphological similarities between the Icelandic horse and the Nordland/Lyngen breed through field comparisons in northern Norway, including evidence that some individuals of both breeds perform the tölt. The first molecular evidence for a close genetic relationship between the Icelandic horse and Norwegian breeds comes from microsatellite analysis by Bjørnstad et al. (2003), in which the Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses cluster together with strong bootstrap support. A British Isles contribution has also long been considered plausible on historical grounds: Norse settlers were established in Shetland before moving on to Iceland, and horses bred in those islands would have carried ancestry from both Scandinavian and native British stocks. The Ahmed et al. (under review) reference panel includes a comprehensive set of modern horses relevant to these hypotheses: Nordlandshest/Lyngen, Norwegian Fjord, Faroese Pony, and Shetland Pony on the Scandinavian-Norse side, and Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh Pony, Eriskay, Fell, Dales, Highland Pony, Connemara, and Kerry Bog Pony representing the full range of British Isles native breeds. This makes it possible for the first time to test the proposed founding contributions with genome-wide data rather than relying on microsatellite markers and single-locus genotype data alone. Four studies have looked directly at ancient Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across Europe, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century, largely absent from ancient Scandinavian horses of the same period, while present in horses from Viking Age York — a pattern consistent with transport of the allele to Iceland from Britain, though the precise route remains unresolved. Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts using low-depth shotgun sequencing (mean endogenous DNA 9.3%), confirming a male bias in burial horses and demonstrating that Icelandic horse skeletal material preserves ancient DNA well. Of the 22 specimens, only two (VHR031 and VHR102) had sufficient sequencing depth (0.08x and 0.18x autosomal coverage) to enter the global ancient horse genome survey of Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*), where phylogenetic analysis placed both closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from Britain and Viking Age British specimens. Campana et al. (2012) demonstrated mtDNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone specimens. Leifsson (2018) provides the zooarchaeological context for the Viking Age specimens, documenting riding-related skeletal lesions, butchery evidence, and the social protocols governing horse burial in Viking Age Iceland. These studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is suitable for ancient DNA analysis and that a British Isles ancestry component was likely present in the founding stock. What they cannot provide is the depth or temporal range required for systematic population genomic inference.

**What makes this project possible now.** Two developments make a project of this scale feasible at this moment. The first is methodological: GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023, *Nature Genetics*) — a computational tool for genotype imputation that uses a large reference panel of known haplotypes to infer likely genotypes in low-coverage samples — enables population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3x coverage, reducing sequencing cost by an order of magnitude compared to earlier approaches. The second is the reference panel itself: Prof. Orlando's haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horses, against which the 376 previously published ancient horse genomes have already been imputed (Ahmed et al., under review), provides both the dense modern reference required for imputation and the temporal comparators required for Aim 1. Five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries, yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA, confirm that the material is suitable and that the project is ready to begin.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; computing runs on the deCODE cluster or, if required, on the University of Iceland cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse. The main risk — that some specimens will yield too little endogenous DNA — is reduced by the large initial sample pool of more than 140 specimens and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is enough to support the core population genomic analyses.

**Phase 1: Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10).** Work begins with specimen selection in collaboration with curators at the National Museum of Iceland, prioritising temporal coverage and geographic spread. Teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics; cortical and petrous bone are used where teeth are unavailable. Silica-based DNA extraction is performed in the deCODE dedicated ancient DNA clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing (Meyer & Kircher, 2010). All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (approximately 1 million reads per library) for quality screening. Endogenous DNA content is assessed against the EquCab3 horse reference genome using BWA (Li & Durbin, 2009) after adapter trimming with AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al., 2016), and characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends) are confirmed with mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al., 2013). Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5x target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. Duplicates are removed using Picard MarkDuplicates; base-quality rescaling is applied with mapDamage2. Biological sex is estimated from X- and Y-chromosome read ratios. Contamination control relies on unique dual indexing, damage authentication, and endogenous content thresholds.

**Phase 2: Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24).** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023) with Prof. Orlando's reference panel (901 modern horses), enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) characterise founding ancestry and test competing models of the founding event. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) tracks genealogical relationships across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC, Li & Durbin, 2011; SMC++, Terhorst et al., 2017) reconstructs effective population size through time. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (approximately 1402 CE). In addition, 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses will be whole-genome sequenced in collaboration with Prof. Sigríður Björnsdóttir (Agricultural University of Iceland): a subset at high coverage (≥15x) to expand the imputation reference panel with Icelandic-specific haplotypes, the remainder at low coverage (1–5x) and imputed using that expanded panel, providing a modern anchor for the demographic models at the same genomic resolution as the ancient dataset.

**Phase 3: Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36).** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, Sabeti et al., 2007; iHS, Voight et al., 2006; PBS, Yi et al., 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 and additional gait-associated loci (STAU2, RELN; Sigurðardóttir, 2025), coat colour and vision loci (ASIP, MC1R, PMEL17; Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012), and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a real research project for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics, a field in which no established training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1: The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology). The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2: Paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík** (archaeology or biological anthropology). The pre-Christian burial ground at Brimnes, Dalvík is one of the largest and most extensively studied Viking Age burial sites in Iceland, with thirteen human skeletal remains, eight horse burials, and the remains of three dogs. Six of the human individuals have been sequenced in the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), making Brimnes, Dalvík the Icelandic burial site with the richest combination of genomic human data and associated horse burials. The student will examine whether the horses are genetically related to one another, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the associated human burial (Leifsson, 2018), and whether the Dalvík horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. If the student has a background in zooarchaeology, they will additionally assist in identifying horse remains within the broader osteological collection at the Museum, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as animal bones — screening work that would expand the pool of candidate specimens.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript. Six months of salary support is included in the budget.

## G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT PLAN

This project builds on an established research programme in ancient genomics and represents a deliberate transition into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD in 2023 at the University of Iceland and has published on ancient Icelandic human genomics, with a companion study of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes currently in preparation. Co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) — a study encompassing 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes characterising the global genomic ancestry of modern horse breeds — marks her move into ancient horse genomics and establishes the international collaboration, the reference panel, and the analytical tools this project will build on.

**Scientific development.** Leading this project will develop her skills in three areas important for her research programme. First, horse-specific population genomic analysis, including selection scans, breed structure inference, and interpretation of functional variants, will expand her range of methods beyond human population genomics. Second, the temporal analysis of trait-associated variants will develop her expertise in connecting genomic data to phenotype. Third, leading an international collaboration with a large, active laboratory (Orlando/CAGT) will build her experience in managing multi-partner genomic projects.

**Conference participation and international visibility.** She will present results at international ancient DNA and population genomics conferences throughout the project, including the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE) annual meeting, as well as relevant Icelandic scientific meetings.

**Institutional development.** This project strengthens the host institution's position as an active contributor to ancient genomics, a field in which Iceland's unique archaeological and historical resources give it a natural comparative advantage. Training a Master's student creates a concrete local capacity-building outcome.

**Long-term trajectory.** The long-term goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. This project is the scientific foundation of that programme: it generates the core dataset, establishes the key international collaborations, and produces the publication record needed to compete for independent research funding at the European level. The results are designed to form the basis of a future European Research Council application (ERC Starting Grant), which would expand the ancient animal genomics programme to additional Icelandic archaeological species and time periods.
