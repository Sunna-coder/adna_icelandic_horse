# [OPEN ITEM 1 — DECISION: confirm title] Bred by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation

## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is one of the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world. Viking-Age settlers brought horses to Iceland in the late 9th century CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). The medieval law code Grágás (Karlsson et al., 1992) prohibited the import of horses, a restriction often traced to the early Commonwealth period from 930 CE, though the precise date is unknown (Stelter, 2014). The ban remains in force today (Act No. 54/1990 on the Importation of Animals, Alþingi) primarily as a biosecurity measure, since Icelandic horses are vulnerable to many diseases circulating among other breeds (Eriksson et al., 2008). Yet the genomic history of the breed across those 1,100 years of isolation remains largely uncharted. We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, how the breed responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how its distinctive traits evolved through isolation. Nor do we know when, or to what extent, genetic diversity was lost or how much remains relative to the founding stock and to other breeds. This project will generate the first dataset capable of answering these questions: approximately 150 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, together with whole-genome sequences from 100–150 present-day horses as a modern reference panel. The study is organised around four scientific aims.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the ancestry profile of the horses that arrived with the Viking-Age settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of horses from Scandinavia and the British Isles?

The genomic ancestry of early Icelanders shows that the settler population included individuals of Scandinavian and Gaelic ancestry, as well as admixed individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science). Genomics identifies these broad ancestry signals but cannot tell us about intermediate stops: a settler with Scandinavian ancestry may have spent years in Orkney or Shetland before arriving in Iceland. The horses they brought with them likely reflected the same diversity. Genetic evidence points to founding contributions from both Scandinavia and the British Isles (see Section B for full evidence), but the proportional contributions of these two source regions to the founding stock cannot be resolved from present-day data alone. This project will determine those proportions for the first time, modelling genome-wide ancient DNA from approximately 150 Icelandic specimens spanning 870–1900 CE against modern horses from more than 100 breeds and ancient specimens from Britain and Scandinavia spanning the Viking Age (Figure 1; Ahmed et al., under review).

Strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) will identify which horses in the earliest time points were physically transported to Iceland and which were born there. Tooth enamel records the geology of where an animal was raised; Iceland's young volcanic bedrock gives a distinctively low isotope ratio, clearly separable from the older continental geology of Norway and the British Isles. For settlement-era specimens, strontium data paired with genomic ancestry estimates will show which horses were physically transported to Iceland and which were bred there. This parallels the approach used to characterise the origins of early Icelanders in Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science), a study led by SSE (the co-proposer) with AH (the Project Leader) as senior author. *[OPEN ITEM 16 — PREPARE: get a formal quote from Durham for the horse strontium work.]*

**Aim 2: An ancestral recombination graph anchored to WorldFengur.** Can the genealogical history of the Icelandic horse genome be reconstructed across the post-settlement period, and what does that reconstruction reveal about the timing of key genomic events?

An ancestral recombination graph (ARG) reconstructs the genealogical history of every DNA fragment, tracing which ancestor each variant descended from and when. For a large outbred population, this would be out of reach, but the small effective population size of the Icelandic horse means that many genomic segments have their most recent common ancestor within the post-settlement period. WorldFengur's verified pedigree anchors the ARG to real individuals back to around 1860. The ancient imputed genomes from this project can be incorporated directly as time-stamped samples into ARG inference (using tools such as Relate and tsinfer; Speidel et al., 2019; Kelleher et al., 2019), thereby extending the well-resolved portion of the ARG back towards 870 CE. Confidence will be higher for the more recent centuries than for the settlement era, but even a partially resolved ARG covering the post-founding period would be the first for any domestic animal anchored to a verified pedigree database.

With the ARG in place, questions that standard methods can only estimate become directly answerable: when did *DMRT3* reach fixation in the breed? Which founder line first carried the *PMEL17* Silver allele? When did IBH susceptibility alleles become prevalent? The ARG also provides a framework for interpreting the demographic and trait analyses in Aims 3 and 4.

**Aim 3: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Whole-genome sequences will be generated for present-day Icelandic horses in collaboration with Prof. Sigríður Björnsdóttir (SB). Horses will be selected to represent the breed's geographic and genetic diversity, drawing from regional breeding populations across Iceland rather than from a single competition or breeding cohort. A subset will be sequenced at high coverage (15–30x) to expand the imputation reference panel with Icelandic-specific haplotypes; the remainder will be sequenced at low coverage (1–3x) and imputed using that panel, completing the temporal series from 870 CE to the present at the same genomic resolution as the ancient dataset. This dataset will allow reconstruction of effective population size, tracking of inbreeding over time, and testing of whether documented crises left detectable genomic signatures.

Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes, with no trace of outside male input in the modern record (Weinzerl, 2024). The ancient male specimens will show whether that was always the case or whether foreign stallions contributed early on and their lines later died out. The X chromosome adds a complementary signal. Because two out of every three X chromosomes in a population come from females, the X is more sensitive to the number of breeding mares than to the number of stallions. Periods when few mares contributed to the gene pool leave a stronger mark on the X than on the autosomes, and comparing X-chromosomal and autosomal patterns through time reveals whether breeding was male- or female-biased in different periods.

**Aim 4: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits emerge and change over more than a millennium? Are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unexpected targets not previously identified in this breed?

Iceland's historical record is unusually rich: volcanic chronology, livestock censuses, and written sources stretching back to the settlement provide dates against which genomic changes can be anchored. We plan to track six traits:

*Gait.* *DMRT3* on ECA23 controls the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). The allele was already present in 9th–11th-century Icelandic specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), but when it became fixed is unknown. *STAU2* and *RELN* (Sigurðardóttir, 2025) will be tracked alongside it.

*Coat colour — full palette.* The Icelandic horse carries more than 40 recognised colours and over 100 Icelandic-language names for colour variants, an exceptionally wide palette by any breed standard (Horses of Iceland, n.d.). The official Icelandic breeding goal explicitly preserves all naturally occurring colours; variety is actively encouraged. Colour diversity in domestic horses emerged through human selection following domestication around 2000 BCE (Librado et al., 2021, Nature). Before that, wild horses were predominantly bay or bay dun (Ludwig et al., 2009, Science). By the time horses arrived in Iceland in the 9th century, a wide palette already existed. Whether the exceptional breadth seen in the breed today reflects what the founding horses carried, or whether it expanded further under Iceland's distinctively inclusive breeding tradition, is a question this project will address for the first time. Colour-associated loci across the genome, including *MC1R* and *ASIP* (base coat), *KIT* (pinto and sabino patterns), *MATP* (cream dilute), *TYRP1*, *TRPM1* (leopard complex; Bellone et al., 2013), and *TBX3* (dun; Imsland et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2024 for review), will be tracked through the temporal dataset to reconstruct the founding colour palette and identify when specific variants entered or rose in frequency. This is the first time the colour history of the Icelandic horse will be addressed using genomic data.

*Coat colour and vision health.* *PMEL17* Arg618Cys causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). When this allele entered the breed is unknown; tracking its frequency from 870 CE will show whether MCOA was a founding-era liability, with direct consequences for breeding management.

*Body size.* The Icelandic horse is compact, usually standing between 133 and 143 cm at the withers (FEIF, 2025). Whether this size reflects the founding stock or was shaped by later conditions is itself one of the questions this project will address. *HMGA2* on ECA6 is the primary genetic locus for body size variation in ponies and small horse breeds (Norton et al., 2019), in addition to its role in Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS). To determine whether the breed's compact size stems from its founding population or from later selection under recurrent famine, *HMGA2* allele frequencies will be tracked through time and compared with osteological estimates of withers height. Standardised long-bone measurements, collected by Dr Joe Walser III (JWW), will serve as an independent phenotypic record to test these alternatives.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Up to half of Icelandic horses develop IBH on first exposure to *Culicoides* midges after export to mainland Europe. The midges do not occur in Iceland, so genetic susceptibility is widespread within the breed but the condition itself is never seen there (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). The temporal dataset will provide the first historical context for when IBH-associated alleles entered the breed and whether the 79 MHC haplotypes exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019) reflect founding diversity or later balancing selection.

*Metabolic disease susceptibility.* Icelandic horses have high rates of equine metabolic syndrome (EMS), with insulin dysregulation, weight gain, and pasture-related laminitis as the main clinical signs. Candidate loci include *FOXO1* on ECA17 and *HMGA2* on ECA6 (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Norton et al., 2019). Iceland's recurrent famines may have favoured alleles that help horses survive on very little, a selective pressure that would look quite different from what these alleles do in a well-fed horse today. Tracking their frequencies against the famine record through the temporal dataset is a direct way to test that idea.

Genome-wide selection scans across the full temporal dataset will also search for signatures of positive and purifying selection at loci not yet studied in the Icelandic horse.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* aDNA extraction and library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well established and have been applied to 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science) and more than 200 (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation). Horse skeletal remains from Icelandic archaeological sites preserve ancient DNA well: Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) reported a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA from 22 Viking Age specimens, and five pilot samples already processed by SSE yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA at a mean depth of 3.6×. Strontium isotope analysis is likewise established within the group: the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study includes strontium measurements for many individuals from the same sites, performed at the University of Durham, and the Icelandic baseline is already in hand. There is no technical barrier to applying either workflow to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland to sample approximately 150 specimens dating from 870 to 1900 CE. The material includes bones from burial mounds (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and open-site assemblages from locations across the country (Figure 2).

**Originality.** Studies of the Icelandic horse have so far relied on mitochondrial DNA and microsatellites (Campana et al., 2012), present-day SNP array genotypes from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), pedigree records (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), and two forms of single-locus ancient genotyping: sex chromosome read ratios (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019) and the DMRT3 variant (Wutke et al., 2016). None reaches the coverage needed for population-level inference. The only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses produced 22 low-depth genomes, of which only two entered a global population survey and neither was imputed (Fages et al., 2019). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse: the first capable of population structure inference, demographic reconstruction, and selection analysis across the full post-settlement history of the breed. The originality has four dimensions.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any North Atlantic horse population: not from Iceland, the Faroe Islands, or Greenland. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

A dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single isolated population with a well-documented historical record has almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics. The ancient genomes, WorldFengur pedigree records, SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and 100–150 new present-day whole-genome sequences together cover nearly the full history of the breed from 870 CE to the present. No other domestic animal offers the same combination: archaeology, genealogy, genomics, and a verified import ban, all for the same population.

Long-term isolation studies are usually hampered by the very kind of documentation gaps that the Icelandic case lacks. Elsewhere, founding events are poorly constrained, import histories are contested, and population records thin out rapidly. Here, the settlement is dated to within a generation, the prohibition on import is legally documented from the Commonwealth period, and written accounts of the population and its crises extend back to the 12th century. That means the genomic time series can be anchored to real events rather than estimated dates, a level of resolution that is genuinely rare in ancient animal genomics.

Island populations generally show reduced diversity relative to their mainland sources (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an acute case of this pattern. The most direct parallel is the human population that crossed the same ocean: Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science) showed how hard drift has worked on the Icelandic human gene pool across the same 1,100-year span.

The applicant's ongoing ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (kuml) documented in Kuml og haugfé (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from the same Viking Age burial contexts, at this combination of temporal depth and sample size, exists nowhere else.

**Impact.** This project will have impact across multiple fronts. The Icelandic horse is one of the most recognisable symbols of the country today, bred across 31 nations and central to Icelandic rural life and national identity. Establishing its genomic history gives that living tradition a scientific foundation, connecting the breed as it exists today to the animals that first crossed the North Atlantic with the Viking-Age settlers.

For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland. The same collections, laboratory infrastructure, and analytical workflows could, in principle, be extended to other species found at Icelandic archaeological sites, including sheep, cattle, and dogs, as the necessary reference data become available.

Studying the founding horses also sheds light on the settlers themselves. The horse was not just a working animal among the first generation — it was a marker of status, a participant in ritual practices including pagan sacrifice and stallion contests, and a creature that held a recognised place in Norse belief (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). Horses were buried alongside people. Knowing where those animals came from is therefore also a way of learning something about the people who brought them: where they had been, what they valued, and how they understood the relationship between humans and animals as they crossed the North Atlantic and how that relationship would evolve in the centuries that followed.

The genetic health of the breed is a practical concern for breeders and breed organisations across 31 countries. Sweet itch affects up to 50% of exported horses (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019) and ocular anomalies linked to *PMEL17* are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will clarify when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current genetic uniformity reflects the original founding or later management decisions. FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations, founded 1969) and Landssamband hestamannafélaga (founded 1949) jointly maintain WorldFengur, which integrates DNA parentage verification and health records for horses across 31 countries. Working with both puts these findings directly in front of breeders and creates a natural basis for follow-on funding in disease genomics and diversity management.

For native breed conservation, the findings carry a relevance beyond Iceland. Two independent lines of genomic evidence point to a British Isles component in the founding Icelandic horse population. Wutke et al. (2016) found the DMRT3 ambling allele in 77% of 9th–11th century Icelandic specimens and in horses buried at Jorvik (York) around 850–900 CE, yet largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses in the same dataset, a pattern that points to settlers arriving in Iceland from or through the British Isles, bringing horses with them. The two ancient Icelandic genomes with sufficient coverage for global placement cluster with pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain and Viking Age horses from the British Isles (Fages et al., 2019). Since the Icelandic horse has been isolated for 1,100 years, it may preserve haplotypes of British Isles origin that have since been diluted or lost in modern British native breeds through crossbreeding and severe population crashes. British native pony breeds such as Dartmoor and Exmoor have lost substantial genetic diversity since the 19th century; both are on the RBST Watchlist as priority conservation breeds (RBST, 2024), and Exmoor ponies are now widely used in rewilding and conservation grazing projects across northern Europe (Rewilding Europe, n.d.). Identifying whether the Icelandic horse carries British-origin haplotypes that are now absent or rare on the mainland is a direct contribution to the conservation genomics of these endangered populations.

Debates about Iceland's relationship with the European Union have raised questions about whether the current importation ban could come under pressure from EU animal movement regulations. The genomic baseline established here would provide an irreplaceable record of the breed's genetic heritage before any such change, and a scientific foundation for evidence-based policy on managing the breed's diversity.

A central question in evolutionary biology and conservation is how small, isolated populations evolve over time. Icelandic horses offer a useful case study because their long-term isolation makes patterns of inbreeding, admixture, and selection easier to interpret. These findings should be relevant to other insular or managed populations. The GLIMPSE2 imputation approach used here is also broadly applicable, and the workflow shows how population genomic analyses can be performed using ancient DNA.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

Horses are among the most globally significant domestic animals, with approximately 60 million individuals worldwide and over 500 recognised breeds (Petersen et al., 2013; FAO FAOSTAT, 2024). Today the genomic landscape of the species is shaped largely by a small number of intensively selected bloodlines. Thoroughbred, Arabian, and American Quarter Horse genetics are well characterised, and all three carry clear signatures of centuries of performance-focused breeding (Fages et al., 2019). The Icelandic horse became isolated before many of these dominant modern breeds took their current form and may retain genetic diversity that has since been lost or reduced elsewhere. Few domestic animal populations combine its age with verified isolation, a well-documented historical record, and material amenable to population genomic inference.

Ancient DNA research has greatly expanded knowledge of horse evolution over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, Science) showed that the Botai horses of Central Asia, previously thought to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact ancestral to Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. The lower Volga-Don region has since been established as the origin of modern domestic horse ancestry, with a rapid expansion across Eurasia from around 2000 BCE (Librado et al., 2021, Nature). A study spanning five millennia of horse genomes revealed two now-extinct lineages and a marked drop in diversity at the point when modern breeds were formed during the 17th to 19th centuries (Fages et al., 2019, Cell). Ahmed et al. (under review), on which AH is a corresponding author and SSE a co-author, characterise seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, establishing that Icelandic horses are defined by the NORD ancestry component that emerged at approximately 810 CE, close to the time horses and humans first arrived in Iceland. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

The question of where the founding Icelandic horses came from has been debated for a long time, and while evidence has accumulated from several directions, the key questions remain unresolved. Norway has usually been seen as the most likely main source. Morphological comparisons drew attention early to the Nordland/Lyngen horse as a probable founding type (Eldjárn, 1991). Later fieldwork added an intriguing functional parallel: several Nordland/Lyngen individuals could perform gaits related to the tölt (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998), though this does not tell us whether the underlying genetic basis was shared through common ancestry or arose independently. Microsatellite data later grouped Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses in the same phylogenetic cluster, which fits the historical accounts of horses being transported from Norway during the settlement period (Bjørnstad et al., 2003).

At the same time, a British Isles contribution has long been considered. It was first proposed on the basis of shared physical traits with pony breeds from the Atlantic fringe, sometimes described as the Celtic pony hypothesis (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998). More recently, several lines of genomic evidence have pointed in the same direction. The DMRT3 ambling allele was present in 77% of early Icelandic specimens from the 9th to 11th century but largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses, yet found in horses from 850 to 900 CE at Jorvik (York), pointing to its arrival in Iceland via settlers coming from or through Britain (Wutke et al., 2016). The only two ancient Icelandic genomes with sufficient depth for global placement cluster closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th to 7th century Britain and Viking Age horses from the British Isles (Fages et al., 2019). The native British breeds that cluster most closely with the Icelandic horse, Dartmoor and Exmoor, are characterised by the BRIT ancestry component representing Iron Age ancestry preserved in Britain, and SNP-based clustering places the Icelandic horse in a shared clade with these breeds (Petersen et al., 2013). Some Norse settlers came to Iceland from communities in the British Isles, as the Gaelic ancestry detected among early Icelanders shows (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science), and the horses they brought may have reflected that same mixed background. What none of these studies can establish is the exact proportional contribution of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock, or whether horses of different origins arrived with different settler groups.

Modern genomic studies provide a detailed picture of the present-day breed, though largely limited to the past two centuries. Genome-wide SNP comparisons show Icelandic horses to be among the most genetically distinct of all horse breeds (Petersen et al., 2013). Notably, they cluster with Nordic breeds and occupy a position close to where most ancient horse samples are found in global genomic space, suggesting the breed may have retained more ancestral diversity than some of the most intensively managed modern breeds (Ahmed et al., under review). Sigurðardóttir et al. (2024) genotyped 380 Icelandic horses on the 670K array, placing current Ne at 125 individuals and genomic inbreeding (F_ROH) between 0.08 and 0.20. Two bottlenecks stand out in the past 60 generations: one around 23 generations ago, matching the Laki eruption of 1783 that killed roughly 75% of Iceland's horses, and a more recent one around 8 generations ago linked to the shift away from horse-powered farming in the early 20th century. ROH islands are strongest over *DMRT3*, with additional signals near *FOXO1* on ECA17 and *HMGA2* on ECA6. The same *FOXO1* signal has been found in Exmoor ponies (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). Two breeds on opposite sides of the North Atlantic sharing an ROH island over the same locus points more to selection for metabolic thrift than to chance drift. A similar pattern holds for *HMGA2*, which recurs across association studies of equine metabolic syndrome in native pony breeds (Norton et al., 2019). Up to half of Icelandic horses develop IBH when exported to mainland Europe, where they encounter *Culicoides* midges for the first time. The midges are absent from Iceland, so susceptibility is widespread in the breed but the condition is never seen there (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). Associated loci have been identified on multiple chromosomes, including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012), and 79 MHC haplotypes are exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019). The 670K array was developed from whole-genome sequences of 153 individuals representing 24 horse breeds, mainly North American and European performance lines (Schaefer et al., 2017); the Icelandic horse was not among the design breeds, and the array may therefore not optimally tag Icelandic-specific variation. On the paternal side, all Icelandic stallions in the modern record carry Y-chromosomal haplotypes unique to the breed, with no detectable trace of outside male input (Weinzerl, 2024), consistent with the import ban having held from the Commonwealth period onward.

Pedigree analysis puts Ne at approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, with an effective number of founders of 151 and an effective number of ancestors of only 42, meaning a handful of animals have disproportionately shaped the gene pool (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). Together, these studies show a breed that still carries the imprint of its founding and of subsequent demographic crises, but the temporal structure of that history, and everything that happened between 870 CE and the mid-19th century pedigree record, remains largely unknown.

Earlier work on ancient Icelandic horse DNA has confirmed both the quality of the material and the limits of what single-locus or very low-depth approaches can achieve. Wutke et al. (2016) genotyped 90 ancient European horses for the DMRT3 variant; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age specimens from Icelandic sites, reporting good preservation with a mean of 9.3% endogenous DNA; Fages et al. (2019) included two Icelandic samples in a global genomic survey, the only ancient whole-genome sequencing of Icelandic horses carried out to date. None of this comes close to what population-level inference requires: no imputed genomes, no admixture estimates, no temporal tracking of allele frequencies, and no reconstruction of effective population size before the 19th century.

Two things have recently changed that make it possible to fill this gap. The first is methodological: GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023, Nature Genetics) has dramatically reduced the sequencing depth needed for population genomic work, making ancient genomes at 1 to 3x coverage usable for analyses that previously required much higher coverage. The second is the availability of reference data: the haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes developed by Orlando's laboratory, together with 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles spanning the Viking Age, provides both the reference needed for imputation and the temporal comparators needed for Aim 1. The panel includes the Nordlandshest/Lyngen alongside a wide range of Atlantic-fringe and Scandinavian breeds, which means founding contributions can now be tested genomically at a resolution no microsatellite or single-locus study could reach.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; sequencing at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse as a fallback; computing on the deCODE cluster or the University of Iceland cluster if needed. The main risk is poor DNA preservation in some specimens. However, the large initial sample set and SSE's preliminary results (five libraries with 10–40% endogenous DNA) reduce that concern. We aim to obtain at least 100 imputed genomes at 0.1x coverage, which is adequate for population-genomic analyses using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023).

Phase 1 begins with specimen selection, osteological assessment, and sampling under the coordination of JWW. Standardised long-bone measurements are taken from all specimens to build the osteometric body size record for Aim 4. Silica-based DNA extraction proceeds in the deCODE ancient DNA clean-room (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007); second or third molars from the same specimens are retained for strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) at the University of Durham, which is performing the strontium work for the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study. Shallow shotgun sequencing is used to screen each library for ancient damage patterns and endogenous DNA content, using AdapterRemoval2, BWA, and mapDamage2 (Schubert et al., 2016; Li & Durbin, 2009; Jónsson et al., 2013). Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA go forward to deep sequencing at 2–5× coverage.

In Phase 2, the ancient genomes will be imputed with GLIMPSE2 using Prof. Orlando's reference panel. Aim 1 focuses on the founding event and tests alternative origin models using wIBS, PCA, ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and admixture graph modelling with ADMIXTOOLS2 (Patterson et al., 2012; Maier et al., 2023). Aim 2 reconstructs the ancestral recombination graph with Relate and tsinfer (Speidel et al., 2019; Kelleher et al., 2019), using WorldFengur pedigree records as a recent framework and then extending that history back to around 870 CE with the imputed ancient genomes. Aim 3 examines how the gene pool changed through time by analysing identity by descent with hap-IBD (Zhou et al., 2020) and estimating inbreeding from runs of homozygosity. Effective population size will be reconstructed with PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++ (Terhorst et al., 2017), and the results interpreted in light of historically documented livestock crises in Iceland.

Phase 3 turns to selection and trait evolution. Genome-wide selection scans, including XP-EHH (Sabeti et al., 2007), iHS (Voight et al., 2006), and PBS (Yi et al., 2010), will be combined with direct tracking of allele-frequency changes over time at loci linked to key traits: gait (*DMRT3*, *STAU2*, *RELN*); coat colour (*MC1R*, *ASIP*, *KIT*, *MATP*, *TYRP1*, *TRPM1*, *TBX3*); the *PMEL17* vision locus; body size and equine metabolic syndrome (*HMGA2*, *FOXO1*); loci associated with sweet itch (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012); and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019). Osteometric estimates of withers height from Phase 1 will also be compared with changes in *HMGA2* allele frequencies, providing an independent phenotypic check on the genomic signal for body size.

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

One Master's student will be recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology at the University of Iceland or the Agricultural University of Iceland. Six project options are defined so that the student can be matched to a supervisor and background that fits. Options 1, 4, 5, and 6 are purely analytical, working from the Phase 2 imputed genomic dataset with no additional laboratory work required. Option 3 is osteological, requiring no bioinformatics. Option 2 combines both. All are designed to result in a publishable manuscript within a 12-month research period; six months of salary support is included in the budget.

**Option 1** (biology or biological anthropology): the temporal history of the Silver/*PMEL17* mutation. The *PMEL17* Arg618Cys variant causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome; when it entered the Icelandic population is unknown. The student will track its frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal dataset.

**Option 2** (archaeology or biological anthropology): paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík, where six of thirteen humans have been sequenced for the in-preparation human study, and eight associated horse burials exist. The student will examine genetic relationships among the horses and their connections to other Icelandic sites. A zooarchaeology background would also allow screening of the National Museum's uncatalogued animal bone boxes, directly expanding the candidate specimen pool. JWW will co-supervise.

**Option 3** (archaeology or biology): osteometric body size through time. The long-bone measurements taken in Phase 1 form the basis for this project. The student will compile the first systematic withers-height record for Icelandic horses across 870–1900 CE, test whether body size shifted in association with documented historical events, and compare the results with *HMGA2* allele-frequency data from the genomic dataset. No bioinformatics experience is needed. JWW will co-supervise.

**Option 4** (biology or biological anthropology): sex-biased founding. The X chromosome passes through females more often than males, making it sensitive to imbalances in the numbers of founding stallions and mares. The student will compare X-chromosomal and autosomal diversity and inbreeding trajectories through the temporal dataset to test whether the founding was sex-biased and whether that imbalance persisted over time.

**Option 5** (biology): the temporal history of IBH susceptibility alleles. Known loci (MHC region on ECA20, X chromosome, and the 79 Icelandic-exclusive MHC haplotypes) will be tracked through the temporal dataset to establish when these variants became breed-exclusive.

**Option 6** (biology or biological anthropology): the coat colours of the settlement horses. Using the earliest ancient genomes (870–1100 CE), the student will reconstruct the founding colour palette from known variants at *MC1R*, *ASIP*, *KIT*, *MATP*, *TYRP1*, *TRPM1*, and *TBX3*, and compare it with later periods and the present day.

Options 1, 4, 5, and 6 will be co-supervised by SSE and AH. Options 2 and 3 will include JWW as an additional co-supervisor. A student based at the Agricultural University of Iceland could be co-supervised by SB or Prof. Jón Hallsteinn Hallsson (Professor of Genetics, Agricultural University of Iceland), particularly for Options 3, 5, and 6.
