## Title page

**Issue: No project title.**

The application needs a formal title. The agreed title is:

> *Brought by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation*

This should appear on the title page of the submission. The subtitle "through isolation" is deliberate — it signals that the project covers not only origins but also the post-founding trajectory under 1,100 years of gene pool closure, which is the main scientific contribution. The Viking angle is a genuine selling point for both Rannís and any future Marie Curie application: it connects the science to a nationally and internationally resonant historical narrative.

## Section A — Specific Aims

### Opening paragraph

> "WorldFengur today contains records for more than 300,000 horses across 31 countries (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024)"

The figure "300,000" is outdated and the citation is not ideal for this specific number. Lorange (2011), which documents the WorldFengur archive directly, gives 354,035 total registered horses, of which 220,168 were living as of 2010. This is the appropriate citation and the more precise figure. Replace with:

> "WorldFengur today contains records for 354,035 registered horses across 31 countries, of which 220,168 were living as of 2010 (Lorange, 2011)"

The same figure appears twice more in the document (once in the Impact subsection and once in Section B under *Genealogical and pedigree data*). All three instances need updating.

---

### Aim 1 question

> "Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of multiple Norse and British-Celtic populations?"

Remove "Celtic." The accumulated evidence does not distinguish specifically Celtic-speaking populations from British Isles horse populations more broadly. The Wutke et al. DMRT3 finding points to Jorvik/English horses; the Fages et al. phylogeny points to Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain; Ahmed et al. identifies the BRIT ancestry component concentrated in British native breeds. None of these specifically implicate Celtic-speaking (Irish or Welsh) horse populations over Pictish or English ones. Using "Celtic" overstates what the genomic evidence can actually resolve, and the user has explicitly decided to remove the term from the application.

Replace with:

> "Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of Scandinavian and British Isles populations?"

---

### Aim 1 founding paragraph

> "The founding Icelandic horse population was therefore likely a mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry, shaped by wherever the colonists had lived before crossing the North Atlantic."

Change "British-Celtic ancestry" to "Scandinavian and British Isles ancestry." Same reasoning as above.

> "A settler from the Hebrides or Ireland would have brought horses drawn from both Scandinavian and native British or Celtic pony stock."

Change "British or Celtic pony stock" to "native British pony stock." The Hebrides/Ireland qualification already implies the relevant geography.

---

### Aim 1 — "Celtic pony hypothesis" and subsequent sentence

> "...has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the 'Celtic pony hypothesis' (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998)."

The term "Celtic pony hypothesis" is used in the literature and the citation is correct. Keeping it in quotation marks as a named concept from the literature is defensible. However, immediately after this sentence, consider adding a note that the genomic evidence now points more specifically to horses from Britain (Pictish and Viking Age English specimens) than to "Celtic" populations in the Irish Sea region specifically.

> "Ahmed et al. (under review)...does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock, or in what proportions."

Change "Celtic or British ancestry" to "British Isles ancestry." The sentence is about what the NORD component analysis cannot resolve — whether the British Isles contributed to the founding stock — and "Celtic or British" implies a distinction between Celtic and British that the genomic data does not make.

---

### Aim 1 — missing Petersen et al. and Ahmed BRIT sentence

After the sentence about Fages et al. placing the two ancient Icelandic genomes closest to Pictish horses, add a sentence on the shared ancestry finding from Petersen et al. and Ahmed et al.:

> "Genome-wide SNP data group the Icelandic horse with the Shetland Pony and related British native breeds in a shared pony clade (Petersen et al., 2013), and the BRIT ancestry component identified by Ahmed et al. (under review) — preserved Iron Age British ancestry concentrated in Dartmoor, Exmoor, and Shetland Pony — is also detectable in the Icelandic horse, providing the clearest genomic signal yet of a British Isles contribution to the founding stock."

This is important: it establishes a specific genomic finding (not just morphological resemblance) linking the Icelandic horse to British native breeds, and it sets up the question Aim 1 will answer — how much British ancestry, from which populations, and when?

---

### Aim 2 — missing modern horse sequencing plan

After the sentence:

> "...the current effective population size stands at 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024)."

Add:

> "To complement the ancient time series with a high-quality modern endpoint, the project will generate whole-genome sequence data from 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses in collaboration with Prof. Sigríður Björnsdóttir (Agricultural University of Iceland). A subset will be sequenced at high coverage (15–20x) to serve as an imputation reference for the ancient genomes; the remainder will be sequenced at low coverage (~1x) and imputed. These modern genomes will be drawn from pedigree-registered individuals spanning diverse regional breeding populations, providing a modern baseline that is directly comparable to the ancient temporal series and immediately relevant to current breeding management."

This addition is important for three reasons: (1) it makes the demographic models substantially more powerful by anchoring the Ne trajectory at the modern end with genome-wide data rather than SNP arrays alone; (2) it strengthens the proposal's practical relevance — breeders care about modern genetic diversity; (3) it signals to reviewers that the project generates new data rather than solely reanalysing existing resources.

### Originality — Temporally paragraph

> "Crucially, the ancient genomic series complements the WorldFengur pedigree archive...Combined with modern SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), the result is a virtually unbroken record from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day."

Replace the reference to the SNP array data with a reference to the new whole-genome data this project will generate:

> "Crucially, the ancient genomic series complements the WorldFengur pedigree archive...Combined with whole-genome sequence data from 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses to be generated as part of this project (Aim 2), the result is a virtually unbroken record from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day — the first time any horse population will have been characterised at genome scale across its entire post-founding history."

---

### Originality — geographic gap sentence

> "Geographically, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic, including Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the broader Norse Atlantic world."

Change "Norse Atlantic world" to "island communities settled during the Viking Age." The phrase "Norse Atlantic world" is a specific historiographical term that some specialists may contest; the more neutral phrasing is cleaner and avoids the Greenland framing which is slightly off-topic (Greenlandic horse populations, if any existed, are not the focus of this project). Also note: Viking Age horse specimens from Orkney ARE included in the Ahmed reference panel (three Pictish/Viking Age specimens dated ~600 CE), so the claim should be carefully worded. A possible revision:

> "Geographically, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from Iceland, the Faroe Islands, Greenland, or Norway — the core regions of Norse Atlantic settlement. The Orkney specimens in the Ahmed et al. panel are the nearest available comparators, from pre-Viking Age Scotland, not from the Norse colonisation zone itself."

### Impact — Industry section

> "The Icelandic horse industry is substantial, with approximately 300,000 registered horses across 31 countries worldwide (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024)"

Update to 354,035 / 220,168 (Lorange, 2011) — same as opening paragraph (see above).

> "FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations), which manages WorldFengur and sets international breeding standards for the breed"

Add the founding year: "FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations, founded 1969)". This is a small detail but signals familiarity with the organisation.

> "Collaboration with FEIF and its Icelandic member association (Landssamband hestamannafélaga)"

The name is incorrect. The correct name is **Landssamband íslenskra hestamanna** (LÍH), founded 1949. "Landssamband hestamannafélaga" is not the current name of the organisation. Add the founding year.

---

### Impact — missing EU importation ban paragraph

The current Impact section does not mention the ongoing debate about Iceland's potential EU membership and its implications for the importation ban. This is a significant policy angle that strengthens the practical relevance of the project. Add after the conservation/rewilding paragraph:

> "A further dimension of practical relevance concerns Iceland's relationship with European Union membership. The ban on importing horses to Iceland — the legal foundation of the breed's genetic isolation — is enforced under Icelandic national law (Act No. 54/1990). Should Iceland join the EU, this legislation would need to be reconciled with EU free-movement principles, potentially ending the isolation that has defined the breed for over a millennium. Understanding the current genomic state of the breed — its levels of genetic diversity, disease allele frequencies, and population structure — before any such policy change would provide a scientific baseline that could inform both legislative decisions and breeding management in a changed regulatory environment. The ancient genomic record proposed here provides the historical depth needed to assess which aspects of the breed's current genomic profile are irreversible consequences of the founding and which might respond to managed gene flow."

## Section C — Research Plan

### ANGSD note on contamination

The section notes that X-chromosome contamination estimation (the ANGSD method) is not applicable to horse material. This is correct — that method uses the human X chromosome. For horses, the appropriate approach is mitochondrial DNA consistency checking and comparison of X:autosome read ratios. Clarify that contamination will be assessed through mapDamage2 damage pattern confirmation and mtDNA consistency, not ANGSD.

### Björnsdóttir paragraph

> "Prof. Björnsdóttir's collaboration makes it possible to expand contemporary sampling further as the project requires."

This is vague. The project has already committed to generating 100–150 whole-genome sequences from present-day Icelandic horses (see Aim 2 above). The Björnsdóttir paragraph should reflect this concrete plan. Replace the current vague closing sentence with:

> "In collaboration with Prof. Björnsdóttir, the project will generate whole-genome sequence data from 100–150 pedigree-registered present-day Icelandic horses, drawn from diverse regional breeding populations. A subset will be sequenced at high coverage to serve as an imputation reference panel; the remainder will be low-coverage sequenced and imputed. This modern genomic baseline anchors the temporal series at its present-day endpoint and provides allele frequency estimates directly comparable to the ancient data."

## General — language and style

### "Will potentially generate"

> "This project will potentially generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse"

The word "potentially" weakens the opening considerably. This appears to be a cautious hedge, but at the opening of Section A it signals uncertainty about whether the project can deliver its core output. Replace with "aims to generate" or simply "will generate." The appropriate place for feasibility hedges is the Feasibility subsection, not the abstract-equivalent opening statement.

---

### Sentence length and structure — opening paragraph

The opening of Section A is strong in content, but several sentences carry heavy subordinate clauses. For example:

> "Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016), it has remained distinct from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years."

Consider breaking this:

> "The horse arrived in Iceland with Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016). In the millennium that followed, it remained entirely distinct from all other horse breeds."

Not a mandatory change, but the second version moves more cleanly.
