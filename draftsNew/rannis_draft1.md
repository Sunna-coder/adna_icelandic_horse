## A. Specific Aims, Research Questions, Feasibility, Originality, and Impact

### A.1 Overview

The Icelandic horse is one of the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Horses were first brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, and their descendants have remained separated from all other horse breeds ever since — protected by one of the world's oldest livestock import bans, established around the 10th century and continuously enforced to the present day. Despite this uniquely continuous and well-documented lineage, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is virtually unknown. No genome-wide study has yet investigated how the founding population was established, how it changed over more than a millennium of isolation, or how that isolation shaped the breed's distinctive traits and health profile.

This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using archaeological specimens held at the National Museum of Iceland spanning the settlement period (ca. 870 CE) to the late 19th century. We will apply advanced ancient DNA (aDNA) methods — established and proven on Icelandic archaeological material by the applicant — to address three specific research questions of major scientific and cultural significance.

### A.2 Research Questions and Specific Aims

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding**: What was the genomic composition of the horses brought to Iceland by the Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock?

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation**: How did the Icelandic horse population change over 1,100 years of geographic isolation? What is the trajectory of inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size through time?

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution**: What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How do the genetic variants associated with the breed's distinctive traits — including the tölt gait, metabolic adaptation to Icelandic conditions, and known disease susceptibilities — change through time?

### A.3 Feasibility

The project is highly feasible, resting on three pillars of prior work:

**Proven aDNA methods for Icelandic material**: The applicant has developed and applied aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols specifically optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material. In a landmark study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., *Science* 2018), these methods successfully recovered genome-wide data from 88 ancient Icelandic human individuals, including specimens over 1,100 years old. An expanded dataset of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation) further establishes the reliability of this approach. Seven ancient Icelandic horse specimens have already been extracted and sequenced using the same protocol, with endogenous DNA yields of 10–40% — well above the threshold required for high-quality genomic analysis.

**Existing sample collection**: Sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for 22 settlement-era horse specimens. A formal request for permission to sample up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE has been submitted. The curator has indicated that this broader permission is expected to be granted.

**International collaboration and reference data**: The project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (Centre for Anthropobiology and Genomics of Toulouse, CAGT, University of Toulouse), the world's leading expert in ancient horse genomics. His laboratory has developed a reference panel of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, already used in a co-authored manuscript with the applicant currently under review (Ahmed et al.). The applicant and Prof. Orlando have an established working relationship and proven capacity to collaborate internationally on high-impact horse genomic research.

### A.4 Originality

This project is the first genome-wide investigation of the Icelandic horse through time. Despite recent major advances in ancient horse genomics — covering horses from Central Asia, the Pontic steppe, Scandinavia, and the Iberian Peninsula — Iceland and the entire North Atlantic region have been entirely absent from the global picture. The project fills this gap with a uniquely powerful dataset: 90 ancient genomes spanning 1,100 years from one of the most isolated and historically documented animal populations in the world.

A key discovery from the Ahmed et al. manuscript (under review), of which the applicant is a co-author, provides a compelling scientific entry point: the **NORD ancestry** component — the primary genetic ancestry of Icelandic horses — emerged around **810 CE, coincident with the Viking era and the Norse settlement of Iceland**. Our project will be the first to trace, from inside Iceland, how that founding stock arrived, established itself, and evolved over more than a millennium. Ahmed et al. provides the global backdrop; this project provides the Icelandic close-up.

### A.5 Scientific and Societal Impact

**For Icelandic science**: This project positions Iceland as a world leader in ancient animal genomics and demonstrates the scientific value of Iceland's unique historical and biological resources. The aDNA methods and analytical pipelines developed here are directly applicable to other Icelandic archaeological animal remains.

**For Icelandic cultural heritage**: The Icelandic horse is central to Iceland's national identity and history. Reconstructing the founding of the breed in the Viking Age and tracing its evolution through the medieval and modern periods will contribute directly to public understanding of Icelandic history and the Norse settlement.

**For the Icelandic horse industry**: With ~80,000–100,000 registered horses in Iceland and over 100,000 abroad, the Icelandic horse represents a significant economic and cultural resource. This project will provide the first population-level genomic baseline for assessing current genetic diversity and health, and will clarify the historical roots of heritable conditions including sweet itch (Culicoides hypersensitivity), equine metabolic syndrome-like profiles, and ocular conditions — all of practical relevance to breeders and veterinarians worldwide.

**For global science**: The Icelandic horse is an exceptional model system for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation in a domestic species — relevant to evolutionary biology, conservation genetics, and the study of other isolated populations worldwide.

## B. Present State of Knowledge in the Field

### B.1 The Icelandic horse: a unique biological and cultural heritage

The Icelandic horse occupies an exceptional position in both natural history and Icelandic culture. Descended from horses transported during the Norse settlement of Iceland in the late 9th century CE, the breed has remained isolated from all other horse populations for over 1,100 years, maintained by what is among the oldest continuously enforced livestock import regulations in the world. This makes the Icelandic horse not only a living cultural symbol but a remarkable natural experiment: a domestic animal population whose entire history since founding is, in principle, traceable and interpretable.

The breed is characterised by small stature, exceptional hardiness, and long lifespan. Most distinctively, it expresses two gaits found in no other breed: the tölt, a four-beat ambling gait, and the flying pace, a two-beat lateral gait. The genetic basis of these gaits has been elucidated: Andersson et al. (2012, *Nature*) demonstrated that a premature stop codon in the *DMRT3* gene — identified from genome-wide association analysis using Icelandic horses specifically — is both necessary and sufficient for the ability to perform alternate gaits. This mutation is now understood to have had major consequences for the diversification of the domestic horse globally, and its history in the Icelandic lineage — when it arose and how it was selected — remains entirely unknown.

The Icelandic horse also displays a characteristic disease profile, including elevated rates of sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), metabolic sensitivity, and ocular anomalies. Particularly relevant to this project, Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome — caused by the same *PMEL17* mutation responsible for the Silver coat colour phenotype (Brunberg et al. 2006, *BMC Genetics*) — has been documented in the Icelandic horse at clinically significant frequencies (Andersson et al. 2011, *BMC Veterinary Research*). These heritable conditions are consistent with elevated inbreeding and the fixation of deleterious variants under long-term isolation; their genomic history through time is a central objective of this project. The Icelandic horse also stands out in modern genomic surveys as a breed with characteristically low within-breed diversity, consistent with its closed population history (Petersen et al. 2013, *PLOS ONE*).

### B.2 Ancient horse genomics: recent advances and the North Atlantic gap

The last decade has produced transformative advances in ancient horse genomics, primarily through the work of the Orlando laboratory and collaborators. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia — previously considered the earliest domestic horses — are in fact the ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, not of modern domestic horses. This finding reset the field, showing that the domestic horse lineage had a separate and as yet unknown origin. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*), using 273 ancient horse genomes, pinpointed the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the homeland of modern domestic horses, with rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE — synchronous with the Sintashta spoke-wheeled chariots. This expansion was accompanied by strong selection at locomotor and behavioural loci, including *GSDMC* and *ZFPM1*.

Building on this foundation, Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled the largest ancient DNA time series for any non-human organism at the time — 278 ancient equid genomes — revealing two now-extinct horse lineages (Iberian and Siberian) that contributed little to modern diversity, and documenting a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. The influence of Oriental/Persian horse lineages was shown to increase substantially following the Islamic conquests, reshaping European breeds from the medieval period onwards.

The recent Ahmed et al. manuscript (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, builds on this body of work with a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes to identify seven major genetic ancestries shaping modern horse breeds and trace their emergence through time. Crucially, this study establishes that the **NORD ancestry component** — which defines Icelandic horses and Scandinavian breeds — emerged at approximately **810 CE**, directly contemporaneous with the Viking Age and Norse expansion. This is the most direct scientific context for the proposed project.

Despite this remarkable progress, the **entire North Atlantic region** — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, and the Norse Atlantic world — remains completely unsampled in the ancient horse genomic record. This is not a minor gap: the Norse settlement represents one of the most dramatic episodes of long-distance human and animal dispersal in the pre-modern world, and the Icelandic horse is its most direct and best-preserved biological legacy. Pre-genomic work on Scandinavian horse breeds was limited to microsatellite-based studies (Bjørnstad et al. 2000, *Journal of Animal Breeding and Genetics*), which lack the resolution to address the questions of this project. The only ancient DNA study of Icelandic horses, Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*), provided the first molecular sex-typing of 22 Viking Age specimens — confirming a strong male bias in burial horses — but at sequencing depths far below those required for population-genomic analysis. No genome-wide study of the Icelandic horse through time has ever been conducted.

### B.3 The applicant's methodological foundation

The applicant's landmark study of 88 ancient Icelandic human genomes (Ebenesersdóttir et al., *Science* 2018) directly establishes the feasibility of the proposed horse project. The same aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols — developed and optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material in the applicant's laboratory — that enabled genome-wide analysis of 1,100-year-old human remains are directly transferable to horse specimens from the same archaeological contexts and time periods. Preliminary work has already confirmed this: seven Icelandic horse specimens yielded 10–40% endogenous horse DNA using the same extraction protocol, far exceeding the threshold required for downstream analysis. Moreover, the Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) study — which includes Orlando as a co-author — demonstrates that the very specimens held at the National Museum of Iceland yield aDNA of sufficient quality for molecular analysis.

### B.4 Imputation-based analysis of low-coverage ancient genomes

A key methodological innovation enabling this project is the use of genotype imputation to increase the density of analysable variants from low-coverage sequencing data. The GLIMPSE framework (Rubinacci et al. 2021, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 0.75–1× coverage, dramatically increasing the number of individuals that can be analysed per sequencing budget. This approach has been validated for ancient horse genomes in Ahmed et al. (under review) using Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, which provides the imputation backbone for this project. The applicant has direct experience applying this framework in the Iceland2 human genomics manuscript (manuscript in preparation).

## C. Research Plan

### C.1 Overview

The project is structured in three phases corresponding to the three aims, with substantial overlap between phases. All laboratory work (Phase 1) is conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík, using specimens from the National Museum of Iceland. Specimens remain in Iceland throughout the project. Computing infrastructure for bioinformatic and population genomic analyses (Phases 2–3) is provided by the deCODE Genetics high-performance cluster under the applicant's collaboration agreement. Sequencing will be performed on the Illumina NovaSeq platform at deCODE Genetics; if sequencing capacity is unavailable at deCODE, sequencing will be conducted at the CAGT facility (Centre for Anthropobiology and Genomics of Toulouse, University of Toulouse) under the established collaboration with Prof. Orlando.

### C.2 Work Plan and Methodology

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing** (Months 1–8)

*Specimen assessment and selection* (Month 1): The applicant will assess available bone and tooth material at the National Museum of Iceland and select optimal specimens for aDNA analysis (up to 90). All specimens remain at the Museum until sampled; sampling takes place under the existing and pending permissions from the National Museum of Iceland. Seven existing sequencing libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) will be incorporated directly into the sequencing phase.

*DNA extraction and library preparation* (Months 2–6): Sampled material will be transferred to the deCODE Genetics aDNA laboratory, Reykjavík. Using silica-based extraction protocols optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material — developed by the applicant in this same facility — DNA will be extracted from each specimen. Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries will be prepared with unique dual indexing.

*Quality screening* (Months 3–6): All libraries will undergo shallow shotgun sequencing to determine endogenous DNA content and confirm characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA will proceed to deep sequencing.

*Deep sequencing* (Months 5–8): Libraries passing quality screening will be sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq platform (deCODE Genetics facility, or CAGT, Toulouse if unavailable) to a target depth of 2–5× per individual. Raw reads will be processed using the applicant's validated aDNA pipeline: adapter trimming (AdapterRemoval2), mapping to the EquCab3 horse reference genome (BWA), duplicate removal (Picard MarkDuplicates), and base quality rescaling (mapDamage2).

**Phase 2 — Population structure and ancestry analysis** (Months 7–18)

*Imputation* (Months 7–12): Low-coverage ancient genomes will be imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 and Prof. Orlando's reference panel (901 modern horse genomes). This step is critical for enabling robust population-level inference from low-coverage data. Computation will run on the deCODE Genetics high-performance cluster, and the analysis will be conducted in close collaboration with the Orlando laboratory.

*Aim 1 — Ancestry and founding* (Months 10–18): Principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE, and f-statistics (ADMIXTOOLS2) will characterise the genomic composition of ancient Icelandic horses and identify the Scandinavian and European source populations of the founding stock. Admixture graph modelling will test whether the founding involved one or multiple introduction events and quantify source population contributions.

*Aim 2 — Demography* (Months 10–18): Identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD), runs of homozygosity (ROH), and demographic modelling (PSMC, SMC++) will reconstruct the trajectory of inbreeding and effective population size across the 1,100-year temporal transect. Results will be compared to other isolated breeds and to the broader global horse dataset from Ahmed et al.

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution** (Months 14–[end])

*Aim 3 — Selection and traits* (Months 14–[end]): Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, iHS, PBS) will identify regions of the genome showing signatures of positive selection specific to the Icelandic lineage. Targeted genotyping of known functional variants — including DMRT3 (tölt and pace), coat colour loci (ASIP, MC1R), and variants associated with sweet itch, metabolic traits, and ocular conditions — will characterise the evolution of these traits under isolation. ROH-based analysis will identify potentially deleterious recessive variants that may have risen to elevated frequency under long-term inbreeding.

## F. Contribution of Doctoral and Master's Students

This project will provide a substantive research component for **one Master's student** from the Faculty of Social and Human Sciences (or Faculty of Natural Sciences), University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology or archaeology. The involvement of a Master's student serves a dual purpose: it directly strengthens the research capacity of the project and provides high-quality, supervised research training within Iceland in a cutting-edge field.

**Scope of the Master's project**: The student will contribute to the specimen assessment and sampling component of Phase 1, developing practical experience in archaeological bone identification, sample documentation, and ancient DNA sampling protocols. In Phase 2, the student will be introduced to basic bioinformatic processing — including read mapping, quality filtering, and visualisation of ancient DNA damage patterns — providing a foundation in computational genomics methods. The student's thesis will address a focused analytical question within the project, such as characterising endogenous DNA preservation across specimen types and archaeological sites, or comparing pre-modern and post-modern Icelandic horse samples at a regional scale.

**Supervision**: The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (University of Iceland / deCODE Genetics, co-supervisor), ensuring both methodological mentorship and institutional support within Iceland. The Master's project is specifically scoped to be achievable as an independent thesis component within a 12-month period, drawing on the broader project dataset without placing unreasonable demands on the student.

**Capacity building**: Embedding a Master's student in this project directly advances the University of Iceland's capacity in ancient genomics. The trained student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with hands-on experience in ancient DNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material — a skill set with broad future application across Icelandic bioarchaeological research.

## G. Career Development Plan

This project represents a pivotal stage in the applicant's development as an independent researcher in ancient genomics. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD in Biological Anthropology at the University of Iceland in 2023 and has an established track record in ancient human population genomics, including a landmark publication in *Science* (2018) and an ongoing manuscript on 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes. This project marks a deliberate extension of her expertise into ancient animal genomics — a field of growing importance for archaeology, conservation biology, and evolutionary science — while retaining her unique comparative advantage in Icelandic ancient DNA methodology.

**Scientific development**: By leading a project at the intersection of ancient genomics, population genetics, and Norse archaeology, the applicant will develop new skills in horse-specific population genomic analysis, selection scanning, and trait genetics, while deepening her methodological expertise in imputation-based analysis of degraded ancient material. Collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, Toulouse) will deepen her integration into the global network of ancient horse genomics researchers and expand her expertise in animal population genomics more broadly.

**Institutional development**: This project strengthens the University of Iceland's position as a leading centre for ancient genomics research in Iceland and the North Atlantic. The applicant's return to the Icelandic research community with new international experience and an established collaboration network will enhance the University of Iceland's capacity in this field.

**Long-term trajectory**: The applicant aims to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, with this project forming the scientific foundation of that programme. The proposed research will generate the core dataset and international partnerships needed to compete for European Research Council (ERC) and other major international grants in the subsequent career stage.
