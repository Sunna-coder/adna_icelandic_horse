## A. Specific Aims, Research Questions, Feasibility, Originality, and Impact

### A.1 Overview

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Horses were brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record — and their descendants have remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted what is one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of horses to Iceland. Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. This legislation, maintained without interruption from the Viking Age to the present day, has produced a population of extraordinary scientific value: a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown at the genomic level. We do not know which Scandinavian populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in one event or many, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines, livestock epidemics, and periods of severe contraction, or how the breed's most distinctive traits — its gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under isolation. These are not merely academic questions. The Icelandic horse is a living part of Iceland's national identity, a significant economic resource, and an exceptional scientific model for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation in a domestic species.

This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning the settlement period (ca. 870 CE) to the late 19th century. Using ancient DNA (aDNA) methods developed and validated by the applicant on Icelandic skeletal material, and the most advanced genotype imputation framework available for ancient horse genomes, we will address three specific scientific aims of major significance for Icelandic science, cultural heritage, and the global study of domestic animal evolution.

### A.2 Research Questions and Specific Aims

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding**: What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves of immigration? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions? Did the composition of the founding population change across the settlement period (870–1000 CE)?

The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock — cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, and horses — across the North Atlantic to newly settled islands. The Icelandic horse descended from this founding event, but its precise genetic origins remain unknown. Ahmed et al. (under review) has established that the NORD ancestry component defining modern Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE in the context of the Viking Age — but what Scandinavian populations carried this ancestry to Iceland, and how the founding population was structured, is a question that only ancient genomic data from Iceland itself can answer.

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation**: How did the Icelandic horse population change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? What are the trajectories of inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size through time? Did Iceland's major historical crises — the Black Death (~1402 CE), the catastrophic volcanic eruption and famine of Laki (1783–1784 CE), and periodic livestock epidemics — leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

The Icelandic horse population experienced a series of severe demographic bottlenecks documented in historical sources. The Laki eruption of 1783–1784 caused catastrophic livestock losses across Iceland; the horse population is estimated to have declined by over 70% within two years. Whether such events are visible in the genomic record — as reductions in effective population size, increases in ROH, or shifts in allele frequencies — is a question this project is uniquely positioned to answer, because we can compare the genomic data directly against the known historical timeline.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution**: What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How do the genetic variants associated with the breed's distinctive traits — the tölt gait (*DMRT3*), coat colour, metabolic adaptation, and disease susceptibility — change in frequency through time?

The Icelandic horse's most celebrated trait, the tölt, depends on a single mutation in *DMRT3* (Andersson et al. 2012). When did this mutation reach fixation in the Icelandic population? Was it selected for from the beginning of settlement, or did it rise to dominance gradually? Similarly, the heritable disease conditions that affect the breed — ocular anomalies linked to the *PMEL17* (Silver) mutation, sweet itch, and metabolic sensitivity — may have risen to their current frequencies through the stochastic processes of long-term inbreeding, or through active selection. Ancient genomic data spanning 1,100 years will allow us to distinguish between these scenarios for the first time.

### A.4 Originality

This project is the first genome-wide investigation of the Icelandic horse across time. Its originality rests on three dimensions that together make it unlike any previously published study.

**Geographic and biological uniqueness**: No ancient genomic data exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the Norse Atlantic world. This absence is remarkable given the pace of progress in ancient horse genomics over the past decade. The Icelandic horse is the direct biological descendant of Viking Age Norse horse husbandry, preserved in genomic amber by the livestock import ban. As Ahmed et al. (under review) has demonstrated, the NORD ancestry component that defines Icelandic horses emerged in the Viking Age specifically — our project will be the first to examine what happened to that founding stock after it arrived in Iceland, and how it evolved over the following millennium.

**Temporal depth and historical resolution**: A dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single isolated population is exceptionally rare in ancient genomics. Most ancient DNA studies reconstruct population history across broad geographic regions over millennia; our study examines one population across a fully documented historical period where major demographic events — the founding, the livestock import ban, the Black Death, the Laki famine, the introduction of modern breeding practices — are precisely dated in the historical record. This allows a level of calibration between genomics and history that is almost never achievable.

**Scientific model system**: The Icelandic horse's combination of genetic isolation, demographic documentation, and phenotypic distinctiveness makes it a uniquely powerful model for questions that are central to evolutionary biology and conservation genetics: How do founder effects shape a population's long-term genetic architecture? How does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants across a genome? What is the relationship between phenotypic selection and underlying genomic change in a closed breeding population? The answers this project generates will have implications far beyond the Icelandic horse.

**Paired human and horse genomic data from individual burial events**: A dimension of originality unique to this project within the ancient genomics literature is the opportunity to combine horse and human genomic data from the same burial contexts. The companion study of ancient Icelandic human genomics (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) has generated genome-wide sequence data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals recovered from 58 sites across Iceland, spanning the settlement period to the late medieval era. A number of these sites — documented in *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) and the National Museum's catalogue — contain both human skeletal remains, now genomically characterised, and horse skeletal remains that are among the specimens targeted in the present project. Combining these datasets opens the prospect of examining, for the first time, whether horses selected for burial were related to horses at other sites, and whether patterns of ancestry and kinship reconstructed for the human population are reflected in the animals buried alongside them. Paired human and horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events represents a scientific resource that does not exist anywhere else in the world.

## B. Present State of Knowledge in the Field

### B.1 The Icelandic horse: a unique biological and cultural heritage

The Icelandic horse occupies an exceptional position in both natural history and Icelandic culture. Descended from horses transported during the Norse settlement of Iceland in the late 9th century CE, the breed has remained isolated from all other horse populations for over 1,100 years, maintained by what is among the oldest continuously enforced livestock import regulations in the world. This makes the Icelandic horse not only a living cultural symbol but a remarkable natural experiment: a domestic animal population whose entire history since founding is, in principle, traceable and interpretable.

The breed is characterised by small stature, exceptional hardiness, and long lifespan. Most distinctively, it expresses two gaits found in no other breed: the tölt, a four-beat ambling gait, and the flying pace, a two-beat lateral gait. The genetic basis of these gaits has been elucidated: Andersson et al. (2012, *Nature*) demonstrated that a premature stop codon in the *DMRT3* gene — identified from genome-wide association analysis using Icelandic horses specifically — is both necessary and sufficient for the ability to perform alternate gaits. This mutation is now understood to have had major consequences for the diversification of the domestic horse globally, and its history in the Icelandic lineage — when it arose and how it was selected — remains entirely unknown.

The Icelandic horse also displays a characteristic disease profile, including elevated rates of sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), metabolic sensitivity, and ocular anomalies. Particularly relevant to this project, Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome — caused by the same *PMEL17* mutation responsible for the Silver coat colour phenotype (Brunberg et al. 2006, *BMC Genetics*) — has been documented in the Icelandic horse at clinically significant frequencies (Andersson et al. 2011, *BMC Veterinary Research*). These heritable conditions are consistent with elevated inbreeding and the fixation of deleterious variants under long-term isolation; their genomic history through time is a central objective of this project. The Icelandic horse also stands out in modern genomic surveys as a breed with characteristically low within-breed diversity, consistent with its closed population history (Petersen et al. 2013, *PLOS ONE*).

### B.2 Ancient horse genomics: recent advances and the North Atlantic gap

The last decade has produced transformative advances in ancient horse genomics, primarily through the work of the Orlando laboratory and collaborators. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia — previously considered the earliest domestic horses — are in fact the ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, not of modern domestic horses. This finding reset the field, showing that the domestic horse lineage had a separate and as yet unknown origin. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*), using 273 ancient horse genomes, pinpointed the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the homeland of modern domestic horses, with rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE — synchronous with the Sintashta spoke-wheeled chariots. This expansion was accompanied by strong selection at locomotor and behavioural loci, including *GSDMC* and *ZFPM1*.

Building on this foundation, Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled the largest ancient DNA time series for any non-human organism at the time — 278 ancient equid genomes — revealing two now-extinct horse lineages (Iberian and Siberian) that contributed little to modern diversity, and documenting a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. The influence of Oriental/Persian horse lineages was shown to increase substantially following the Islamic conquests, reshaping European breeds from the medieval period onwards.

The recent Ahmed et al. manuscript (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, builds on this body of work with a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes to identify seven major genetic ancestries shaping modern horse breeds and trace their emergence through time. Crucially, this study establishes that the **NORD ancestry component** — which defines Icelandic horses and Scandinavian breeds — emerged at approximately **810 CE**, directly contemporaneous with the Viking Age and Norse expansion. This is the most direct scientific context for the proposed project.

Despite this remarkable progress, the **entire North Atlantic region** — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, and the Norse Atlantic world — remains completely unsampled in the ancient horse genomic record. This is not a minor gap: the Norse settlement represents one of the most dramatic episodes of long-distance human and animal dispersal in the pre-modern world, and the Icelandic horse is its most direct and best-preserved biological legacy. Pre-genomic work on Scandinavian horse breeds was limited to microsatellite-based studies (Bjørnstad et al. 2000, *Journal of Animal Breeding and Genetics*), which lack the resolution to address the questions of this project. The only ancient DNA study of Icelandic horses, Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*), provided the first molecular sex-typing of 22 Viking Age specimens — confirming a strong male bias in burial horses and demonstrating that the specimens yield recoverable aDNA — but at sequencing depths far below those required for population-genomic analysis. No genome-wide study of the Icelandic horse through time has ever been conducted.

### B.3 The applicant's methodological foundation

The applicant's landmark study of 88 ancient Icelandic human genomes (Ebenesersdóttir et al., *Science* 2018) directly establishes the feasibility of the proposed horse project. The same aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols — developed and optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material in the applicant's laboratory at deCODE Genetics — that enabled genome-wide analysis of 1,100-year-old human remains are directly transferable to horse specimens from the same archaeological contexts and time periods. These protocols have subsequently been applied to 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation), further validating their reliability and scalability. Preliminary work has already confirmed their applicability to horse material: seven Icelandic horse specimens yielded 10–40% endogenous horse DNA, far exceeding the threshold required for downstream analysis.

### B.4 Imputation-based analysis of low-coverage ancient genomes

A key methodological innovation enabling this project is the use of genotype imputation to increase the density of analysable variants from low-coverage sequencing data. The GLIMPSE framework (Rubinacci et al. 2021, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 0.75–1× coverage, dramatically increasing the number of individuals that can be analysed per sequencing budget. This approach has been validated for ancient horse genomes in Ahmed et al. (under review) using Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, which provides the imputation backbone for this project. The applicant has direct experience applying this framework in the Iceland2 human genomics manuscript (manuscript in preparation).

## C. Research Plan

### C.1 Overview

The project is structured in three phases corresponding to the three aims, with substantial overlap between phases. All laboratory work (Phase 1) is conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík, using specimens from the National Museum of Iceland. Specimens remain in Iceland throughout the project. Computing infrastructure for bioinformatic and population genomic analyses (Phases 2–3) is provided by the deCODE Genetics high-performance cluster under the applicant's collaboration agreement. Sequencing will be performed on the Illumina NovaSeq platform at deCODE Genetics; if sequencing capacity is unavailable at deCODE, sequencing will be conducted at the CAGT facility (Centre for Anthropobiology and Genomics of Toulouse, University of Toulouse) under the established collaboration with Prof. Orlando.

The principal risk to the project — low endogenous DNA yield from some specimens — is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool (up to 90 specimens) and the preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in seven Icelandic horse specimens processed to date. Even if a subset of specimens yields insufficient DNA, the project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is sufficient for robust population genomic inference using the imputation approach described below. The seven existing libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) provide a guaranteed baseline that would allow Phase 2 to begin even in a worst-case sample processing scenario.

### C.2 Work Plan and Methodology

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing** (Months 1–8)

*Specimen assessment and selection* (Month 1): The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to assess the available skeletal material — tooth roots, cortical bone, and petrous bone fragments where available — and select specimens that maximise temporal coverage and geographic spread across Iceland. Up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE will be selected. Teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics; cortical bone and petrous bone are used where teeth are unavailable. All specimens remain at the Museum until sampled under the existing and pending National Museum permissions.

*DNA extraction and library preparation* (Months 2–6): Sampled material is transferred to the deCODE Genetics aDNA laboratory. Silica-based extraction — the protocol developed by the applicant and optimised for Icelandic skeletal material — is used to recover endogenous DNA from each specimen. Extractions are performed in a dedicated ancient DNA clean-room facility that is physically isolated from areas where modern DNA is handled, using standard contamination-control procedures (UV decontamination, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing for multiplexed sequencing, following established protocols for ancient DNA library preparation.

*Quality screening* (Months 3–6): All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (~1 million reads per library) to assess endogenous DNA content and confirm characteristic ancient DNA authentication markers: cytosine deamination at read ends (C→T substitutions), characteristic fragmentation patterns, and fragment length distributions consistent with ancient material. Authentication is evaluated using mapDamage2. Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing; libraries below threshold are documented and archived.

*Deep sequencing* (Months 5–8): Qualifying libraries are sequenced to a target average depth of 2–5× per individual on the Illumina NovaSeq platform. Raw reads are processed through the applicant's validated aDNA pipeline: adapter trimming (AdapterRemoval2, minimum read length 25 bp), mapping to the EquCab3 horse reference genome (BWA aln), duplicate removal (Picard MarkDuplicates), and post-mapping base quality rescaling (mapDamage2). Sex is estimated from the ratio of reads mapping to the X and Y chromosomes.

**Phase 2 — Population structure and ancestry analysis** (Months 7–18)

*Imputation* (Months 7–12): Low-coverage ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE and Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes. This step is critical for enabling robust population-level inference from low-coverage data: imputation recovers the vast majority of variant sites from genomes sequenced at 1–5× coverage, making the dataset analytically comparable to high-coverage modern genomic datasets. Computation runs on the deCODE Genetics high-performance cluster, and the imputation pipeline is run in close collaboration with the Orlando laboratory, who developed and validated this approach for ancient horse genomes in Ahmed et al.

*Aim 1 — Ancestry and founding* (Months 10–18): The genomic composition of ancient Icelandic horses is characterised and their relationship to other horse populations established using a hierarchical analytical strategy. Principal components analysis (PCA) and ADMIXTURE provide a global overview of ancestry structure. f-statistics and formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2) quantify the contributions of specific Scandinavian and European source populations to the Icelandic founding stock, and test competing models of the founding event — single introduction versus multiple waves. Temporal changes in ancestry across the settlement period are examined to detect whether the founding population's composition shifted during the settlement phase itself.

*Aim 2 — Demography* (Months 10–18): The trajectory of the Icelandic horse population across 1,100 years is reconstructed using complementary methods. Identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD) estimates genealogical relationships between individuals and tracks the progressive accumulation of shared ancestry through time. Runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level, with the temporal profile of ROH lengths informing the timing and severity of population bottlenecks. Demographic modelling (PSMC, SMC++) reconstructs changes in effective population size. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Icelandic livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (~1402 CE), to test whether major historical events are detectable in the genomic record.

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution** (Months 14–[end])

*Aim 3 — Selection and traits* (Months 14–[end]): Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, iHS, population branch statistic PBS) identify regions of the genome showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted analysis of known functional variants tracks the frequency trajectory of specific alleles through time: the *DMRT3* stop codon (gait), *ASIP* and *MC1R* (coat colour), the *PMEL17* Arg618Cys mutation (Silver coat colour and MCOA), and loci associated with sweet itch susceptibility, metabolic sensitivity, and immune function where genomic positions are known. ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency, providing a population-level explanation for the breed's known disease profile.

## E. Proposed Publication of Results in Open Access

Two primary research articles are planned as outputs of this project:

## F. Contribution of Doctoral and Master's Students

This project will provide a substantive research component for **one Master's student** from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on research experience in a field — ancient animal genomics — that is currently represented by no trained Icelandic researchers, contributing directly to the development of national research capacity in this area.

**Scope of the Master's project**: In Phase 1, the student will participate in specimen assessment at the National Museum of Iceland, developing practical knowledge of archaeological bone and dental morphology, sample documentation procedures, and the destructive sampling protocols used in aDNA work. This introduces the student to the intersection of archaeology and molecular biology in a supervised, structured setting. In Phase 2, the student will learn basic bioinformatic processing of ancient DNA data — read quality filtering, alignment to a reference genome, and visualisation of ancient DNA damage patterns using established tools — providing a foundation in computational genomics that is increasingly central to biological anthropology and archaeology. The student's Master's thesis will address a self-contained analytical question drawing on the broader project dataset: for example, characterising endogenous DNA preservation patterns across specimen types and chronological periods, or examining the geographic distribution of specific coat colour or gait-associated alleles across Icelandic archaeological sites.

**Supervision**: The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor), ensuring continuous methodological and scientific mentorship throughout the project. The Master's project is specifically scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period, drawing on the broader project dataset without placing demands on the student that would be inappropriate for a Master's-level thesis.

**Capacity building**: Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit for Icelandic science beyond the outputs of this project. The student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in this methodological area, with skills applicable to future bioarchaeological projects on Icelandic animal remains — cattle, sheep, and dogs from the same archaeological contexts as the horse specimens studied here.
