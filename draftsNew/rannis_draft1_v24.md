# Bred by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation

## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is one of the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world, and a defining cultural symbol of the country. Brought to Iceland by Viking-age settlers in the late 9th century CE (870 CE), as documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016), it has been isolated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. The medieval law code Grágás (Karlsson et al., 1992) prohibited the import of horses, a restriction often traced to the early Commonwealth period (from 930 CE onwards), though the precise date remains uncertain (Stelter, 2014). This ban has never been lifted. Under current Icelandic legislation (Act No. 54/1990 on the Importation of Animals, Alþingi), no horse may enter Iceland, and any horse that leaves may not return. The current prohibition is grounded in biosecurity: having been isolated for over a millennium, Icelandic horses have limited immunity to many diseases common in other horse populations.

More than 1,100 years of geographic and genetic separation make the Icelandic horse an intriguing case for ancient genomics, offering a window into population history that texts and archaeology alone cannot provide. The breed is also unusually well documented. Formal pedigree recording began in the early 20th century with the establishment of a studbook for approved breeding stock. From 1991 onwards, breeders and registries digitised the records and consolidated them into WorldFengur, the breed's official international studbook, which formally opened in 2001 (Lorange, 2011). WorldFengur contained records for more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries as of 2010, of which approximately 220,000 were living at that time (Lorange, 2011), and the oldest individual in the archive was born around 1860 (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014).

While the modern pedigree record is exceptionally detailed, it only reaches back to the mid-19th century. Before that, the population-level genomic history of the Icelandic horse is largely unknown. Twenty-four Icelandic Viking Age specimens have been sequenced, but all at very low depth of coverage (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019); only two reached sufficient depth to be tentatively placed on a global phylogeny (Fages et al., 2019, Cell). We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, how the breed responded to the country's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how its distinctive traits — unique gaits, exceptional hardiness, characteristic disease susceptibilities — evolved over the past 1,100 years. Nor do we know when and to what extent its current genetic composition took shape: whether patterns of inbreeding reflect a narrow founding or accumulated change over centuries of isolation, famine, and epidemic disease. This project will produce the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, drawing on approximately 150 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, complemented by whole-genome sequence data from 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses. Together, these data will address these questions through three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the ancestry profile of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of distinct breeds from different regions in Scandinavia and the British Isles?

According to the Book of Settlements (Landnámabók — the medieval Icelandic record of the settlement, Benediktsson, 1968), Norse settlers colonised Iceland in the late 9th century CE. Their ships carried livestock, including horses (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). A proportion of these settlers came not directly from Scandinavia but from Norse communities already established in the British Isles, as indicated by the mixed Norse and Gaelic genomic ancestry found among early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science), and horses brought from those communities may have included animals of mixed Norwegian and British pony ancestry. The Icelandic horse's small stature, hardiness, and dense coat are shared with native breeds along the Atlantic fringe of Europe, which has long been taken to suggest a contribution from the British Isles, the so-called 'Celtic pony hypothesis' (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998). Multiple lines of genomic evidence now support this: the DMRT3 ambling allele was present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century, largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses, yet found in two horses from 850–900 CE at Jorvik (York) (Wutke et al., 2016); genome-wide phylogenetic analysis places the two ancient Icelandic genomes of sufficient depth closest to pre-Viking Pictish and Viking Age horses from Britain (Fages et al., 2019); and genomic analysis identifies a shared Iron Age ancestry component between Icelandic horses and native British breeds, with clustering grouping the Icelandic horse with native pony breeds in a shared clade (Ahmed et al., under review; Petersen et al., 2013). A distinct Scandinavian ancestry profile (NORD), characteristic of Nordic breeds including the Icelandic horse, emerged at approximately 810 CE (Ahmed et al., under review) — but the proportional contributions of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock remain unknown.

This project will determine those proportions for the first time, using genome-wide ancient DNA data from approximately 150 Icelandic specimens spanning 870–1900 CE. The imputation reference panel comprises 901 modern horses from more than 100 breeds; 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel — including specimens from Britain, Denmark, and Sweden spanning 495–956 CE — provide the temporal comparators needed to resolve founding ancestry (Ahmed et al., under review; Figure 1).

**Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Genomic analysis of the present-day Icelandic horse has detected two effective population size crashes: one consistent with the Laki volcanic famine of 1783–1784, which reduced the horse population by approximately 75%, and one during early 20th-century mechanisation. The current effective population size stands at 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and pedigree analysis identifies 4,547 recorded founders but an effective number of ancestors of only 42 — the genetic contribution to the present-day breed is concentrated in a handful of animals (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). This project will generate whole-genome sequence data for 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses in collaboration with Prof. Björnsdóttir: a subset at high coverage (15–30x) to expand the imputation reference panel with Icelandic-specific haplotypes, the remainder at low coverage (1–3x) and imputed using that panel, providing a present-day anchor for demographic models at the same genomic resolution as the ancient dataset.

The ancient genomes also carry information that autosomal data alone cannot provide. Male specimens will yield Y-chromosomal haplotypes, allowing us to track how many distinct stallion lineages contributed to the breed and whether any were lost or became dominant during historical crises. Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes (Weinzerl, 2024), but when that distinctiveness emerged remains unknown. The X chromosome, with its smaller effective population size and sensitivity to unequal sex ratios in breeding, will be analysed separately to detect sex-biased bottlenecks invisible in autosomal data.

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits emerge and change over more than a millennium? Moreover, are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unexpected targets — traits not previously identified as candidates for selection in this breed?

Unlike breeds developed for warfare, racing, or heavy draft — where intense artificial selection has left strong genome-wide signatures (Fages et al., 2019) — the Icelandic horse was shaped by a different combination of forces: natural selection driven by climate, disease, and nutritional stress alongside modest artificial selection for hardiness, sure-footedness, and gait (Stelter, 2014; Leifsson, 2018). Consistent with this, selection signals in present-day Icelandic horses cluster at a handful of gait-associated loci rather than being spread genome-wide (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Wutke et al., 2016). A temporal genomic dataset can separate these forces: genome-wide diversity shifts coinciding with documented environmental crises point to natural selection, while rapid change concentrated on specific loci suggests directed breeding. Iceland's unusually precise historical record makes this an exceptionally good system for asking these questions.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012), forming an ROH island present in over 90% of present-day Icelandic horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024). The locus was already at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), but when fixation was reached remains unknown. Additional gait loci including STAU2 and RELN (Sigurðardóttir, 2025) will be tracked simultaneously.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, present in Icelandic horses at clinically significant frequencies (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). Whether this allele was present from founding or arose later under drift or selection is unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a later development, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity, IBH) is an IgE-mediated allergic skin reaction triggered by Culicoides midge bites and one of the most economically significant welfare problems in the breed. Culicoides midges are absent from Iceland, so IBH is not expressed there — but affects up to 50% of exported horses on first exposure to midges in mainland Europe (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019), meaning susceptibility is widespread but phenotypically hidden. Associated loci have been identified on multiple chromosomes including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012), but present-day data cannot resolve when these alleles arose or whether they came from the Scandinavian founding stock, the British Isles component, or accumulated through drift. The temporal dataset will provide the first historical context for this question, including whether the 79 MHC haplotypes exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019) reflect diversity present from founding or maintained by balancing selection across the demographic crises.

*Metabolic disease susceptibility.* The Icelandic horse is prone to Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS), characterised by insulin dysregulation, obesity, and laminitis susceptibility. FOXO1 on ECA17, a prominent ROH island shared by Icelandic horses and Exmoor ponies (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and HMGA2 on ECA6, the most replicated EMS-associated locus across native pony breeds (Norton et al., 2019), are the primary candidates. The leading hypothesis is that selection for metabolic thriftiness — advantageous under Iceland's repeated famines — produced allele frequency changes that now predispose the breed to EMS on modern rich pasture. Tracking these variants through the temporal dataset and correlating changes with documented famine periods, most notably the Laki eruption of 1783, provides a direct test of this hypothesis.

Beyond these targeted variants, the temporal dataset will be used to run genome-wide selection scans searching for additional signatures of positive or purifying selection, potentially revealing unexpected targets not previously identified in this breed.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* aDNA extraction and library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well established and have been applied to 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science) and more than 200 (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation). Horse skeletal material from Icelandic archaeological contexts preserves aDNA well: Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) recovered a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA across 22 Viking Age specimens, and five pilot specimens processed by the applicant yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA at a mean depth of 3.6x. There is no technical barrier to applying these workflows to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted at the National Museum of Iceland for approximately 150 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE. These come from burial mounds (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and open-site assemblages distributed across the country (Figure 2).

Sequencing will be performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse available as fallback options. The working relationship already exists through co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) — so the reference panel, analytical pipeline, and collaboration are in place from day one. Prof. Orlando hosts one research visit per year at CAGT Toulouse and can provide sequencing as a fallback if needed. A letter of support is included.

**Originality.** No previous study has generated ancient genomic data from the Icelandic horse at the depth or scale required for population inference. Prior work relied on mitochondrial DNA and microsatellites (Campana et al., 2012), present-day SNP array data (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), or single-locus ancient genotyping — sex chromosome read ratios (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019) and the DMRT3 gait variant (Wutke et al., 2016). The only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses produced 22 low-depth genomes, of which only two entered a global population survey and neither was imputed (Fages et al., 2019). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse — the first capable of population structure inference, demographic reconstruction, and selection analysis across the full post-settlement history of the breed. The originality has four dimensions.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the Viking Age settlements of the North Atlantic — not from Iceland, the Faroe Islands, or Greenland. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record has almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics. The genomic time series can be checked against specific, dated historical events — a resolution that is rarely achievable in the ancient genomics literature. Combined with the WorldFengur pedigree archive, existing SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and 100–150 present-day whole-genome sequences generated in this project, this creates a record running almost unbroken from the founding event in 870 CE to the present day. No other domestic animal comes close: we have the archaeology, the genealogy, the genomics, and a verified import ban — all for the same population.

The Icelandic horse is close to an ideal model system for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation: how does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants through a population, and what drives trait selection when a breeding population is completely closed? These are fundamental questions in evolutionary genetics, and this system addresses them with empirical data spanning more than a millennium. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland sources (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case, and the human population that settled Iceland provides a direct parallel — the ancient genomic study of Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science) demonstrated how profoundly drift has shaped the Icelandic human gene pool across the same period.

The applicant's ongoing ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (kuml) documented in Kuml og haugfé (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from the same Viking Age burial contexts, at this combination of temporal depth and sample size, exists nowhere else.

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including sheep, cattle, and dogs, and the work creates a template for future projects on those species.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse was at the heart of settlement-era society — a working mount, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and stallion contests (hestamót), a companion in death (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). In Norse tradition horses carried deep mythological weight, and Icelandic burial archaeology reflects this: they were interred alongside their owners as companions rather than property. Where the founding horses came from is therefore also a question about the settlers themselves — their origins, their routes, and the livestock populations they had access to before crossing the North Atlantic.

The Icelandic horse industry is substantial, with more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries as of 2010 (Lorange, 2011), and the genetic health of the breed is a practical concern. Sweet itch affects up to 50% of exported horses (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019) and ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will clarify when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current genetic uniformity reflects the original founding or later management decisions. Collaboration with FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations, founded 1969) and Landssamband hestamannafélaga (founded 1949), whose WorldFengur database integrates DNA parentage verification and health records across 31 countries, provides a direct channel to connect these findings to breeders and to explore follow-on funding for disease genomics and diversity management.

For native breed conservation, the findings carry a relevance beyond Iceland. Ahmed et al. (under review) identified a BRIT ancestry component — preserved Iron Age ancestry from the British Isles — concentrated in critically endangered British native breeds such as the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony. If horses from the British Isles contributed to the Icelandic founding stock, the Icelandic horse may have preserved genetic diversity that has since been severely reduced on the mainland through modern breed formation and population bottlenecks. Both breeds are on the critical list of the Rare Breeds Survival Trust; Exmoor ponies are now widely used in rewilding and conservation grazing projects across northern Europe. Identifying which British-origin variants were carried to Iceland, and whether any are absent or rare in modern British native breeds, is a direct contribution to the conservation genomics of these endangered populations.

The long-term integrity of the Icelandic gene pool is not guaranteed. Ongoing discussions about Iceland's relationship with the European Union (EU) raise the question of whether the current importation ban could come under pressure from EU animal movement regulations. The genomic baseline established here would provide an irreplaceable record of the breed's genetic heritage before any such change, and a scientific foundation for evidence-based policy on managing the breed's diversity in the future.

For global science, the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to evolutionary biology and conservation theory well beyond the Icelandic horse. The methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

With approximately 60 million horses distributed worldwide and more than 500 recognised breeds (Petersen et al., 2013; FAO FAOSTAT, 2024), the horse is among the most globally significant domestic animals, yet modern horse populations are dominated by a handful of intensively bred bloodlines — Thoroughbred, Arabian, American Quarter Horse — whose genomic landscape is increasingly well characterised. The Icelandic horse became isolated before many of these dominant modern breeds took their current form, and may retain genetic diversity that has since been lost or reduced on the mainland. Few domestic animal populations combine its age with verified isolation, a well-documented historical record, and material amenable to population genomic inference.

Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, Science) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia, long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. The lower Volga-Don region (present-day southern Russia and Ukraine) has since been established as the origin of modern domestic horse ancestry, with a rapid expansion across Eurasia from around 2000 BCE (Librado et al., 2021, Nature). A study spanning five millennia of horse genomes revealed two now-extinct lineages and a marked drop in diversity at the point when modern breeds were formed during the 17th to 19th centuries (Fages et al., 2019, Cell). Much has also been gained from analyses of modern horses alone and through comparisons of ancient and modern genomes: Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, characterise seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from 901 modern horse genomes and 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that reference panel. They establish that Icelandic horses are characterised by the NORD ancestry component that emerged at approximately 810 CE — close to the time horses and humans first arrived in Iceland. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

The question of where the founding Icelandic horses came from has a long research history, and a coherent picture is emerging — though critical gaps remain. On origins, Norway has been the primary candidate from the start. Morphological comparisons pointed early to the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage (Eldjárn, 1991), and subsequent field comparisons confirmed that five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform gaits related to the tölt (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998) — though tölt performance in Nordland/Lyngen horses does not resolve whether the DMRT3 allele controlling it was shared through a common ancestral population or arrived independently. Microsatellite analysis later placed Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses in the same phylogenetic cluster with strong bootstrap support (Bjørnstad et al., 2003), consistent with the historical record of Viking Age introduction from Norway. A British Isles contribution has also attracted sustained attention, supported initially by shared morphological features with Atlantic-fringe breeds and now by several converging lines of genomic evidence. The DMRT3 ambling allele was present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses in the dataset — yet found in two horses from 850–900 CE at Jorvik (York), pointing to its arrival in Iceland via settlers coming from or through Britain (Wutke et al., 2016). The only two ancient Icelandic genomes with sufficient depth for global placement cluster closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses from Britain and Scandinavia, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse (Fages et al., 2019). Genomic analysis further identifies a shared Iron Age ancestry component (BRIT) between Icelandic horses and native British breeds including Dartmoor and Exmoor ponies, with SNP-based clustering placing the Icelandic horse in a shared pony clade with these breeds (Ahmed et al., under review; Petersen et al., 2013). Some Norse settlers came not directly from Scandinavia but from established communities in the British Isles, as indicated by the presence of Gaelic ancestry among early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science), and horses carried on those voyages may have reflected mixed Norwegian and British pony ancestry. What none of these studies can establish is the exact proportional contribution of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock, or whether horses of different origins arrived with different settler groups.

On diversity and population history, modern genomic and pedigree studies provide a detailed picture of the present-day breed, though largely limited to the past two centuries. Genome-wide SNP comparisons show Icelandic horses to be among the most genetically distinct of all horse breeds (Petersen et al., 2013). Notably, however, Icelandic horses cluster with Nordic breeds and occupy a position close to where most ancient horse samples are found in global genomic space — suggesting the breed may have retained more ancestral diversity than some of the most intensively managed modern breeds, rather than simply being low in diversity relative to all horses (Ahmed et al., under review). The most comprehensive recent study (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024) genotyped 380 Icelandic horses on a 670K SNP array, estimating a current effective population size of 125 individuals, a genomic inbreeding coefficient (F_ROH) of 0.08–0.20, and clear ROH islands over DMRT3 and several other loci linked to performance and adaptation. Importantly, these chip data have not yet been analysed in the context of global horse variation or ancient horse genomes — a gap this project will close. Pedigree analysis puts Ne at approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, with an effective number of founders of 151 and an effective number of ancestors of only 42 — a handful of animals have disproportionately shaped the gene pool (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). WorldFengur, the official international studbook formally opened in 2001 (Lorange, 2011), contains records for more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries as of 2010, with documentation reaching back to individuals born around 1860. Y-chromosomal analysis of Icelandic stallions, based on direct genotyping of 72 individuals and extrapolation using genealogical records, found only Icelandic-specific haplotypes with no sign of recent outside introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Together, these studies show a breed that still carries the imprint of its founding and of subsequent demographic crises — but the temporal structure of that history, and everything that happened between 870 CE and the mid-19th century pedigree record, remains largely unknown.

Previous attempts to study ancient Icelandic horse DNA have confirmed the potential of the material and the limits of what single-locus or very low-depth approaches can achieve. Wutke et al. (2016) genotyped the DMRT3 variant across 90 ancient European specimens; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age specimens, confirming good preservation (mean 9.3% endogenous DNA); and Fages et al. (2019) included two Icelandic genomes in a global survey — the only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses to date. None of these studies produces the depth or sample size needed for population-level inference: no imputed genomes, no admixture quantification, no temporal tracking of allele frequencies, no estimate of effective population size before the 19th century.

The central gap is therefore the complete absence of a temporal genomic dataset from the Icelandic horse. Without it, we cannot quantify the proportional contributions of founding populations, determine when current patterns of inbreeding emerged, or test the relationship between documented historical events and genomic change. Two developments now make it possible to close this gap. The first is methodological: GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023, Nature Genetics), a computational framework for low-coverage genotype imputation, enables population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3x coverage, reducing costs by an order of magnitude. The second is the availability of the reference data: Prof. Orlando's haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, together with 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel — including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles spanning the Viking Age — provides both the reference needed for imputation and the temporal comparators needed for Aim 1. With genomic data from many ancient and modern Icelandic horses and from relevant breeds and ancient samples across Europe, it will for the first time be possible to quantify founding admixture proportions, track allele frequencies through 1,100 years, and explore population structure using PCA, IBD sharing, wIBS, and admixture graph modelling. Five quality-assessed pilot libraries yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA confirm the material is ready and the project can begin immediately. Both the Nordlandshest/Lyngen and a broad range of Atlantic-fringe and Scandinavian breeds — including Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh, Connemara, Kerry Bog Pony, Fjord, Eriskay, Fell, and Highland ponies — are represented in the reference panel, making it possible to test founding contributions genomically with a resolution unavailable to earlier microsatellite and single-locus studies.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; sequencing at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse as fallback; computing on the deCODE cluster or the University of Iceland cluster if needed. The main risk is that some specimens will yield too little endogenous DNA, but the large initial sample pool and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries reduce this considerably. The project is designed to generate at least 100 high-quality imputed genomes at approximately 0.1x coverage, sufficient for population genomic inference using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023).

Phase 1 begins with specimen selection, sampling, and silica-based DNA extraction in the deCODE ancient DNA clean-room (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007). Libraries are screened by shallow shotgun sequencing using a standard aDNA pipeline (AdapterRemoval2, BWA, mapDamage2; Schubert et al., 2016; Li & Durbin, 2009; Jónsson et al., 2013), confirming ancient damage profiles and endogenous content. Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA proceed to deep sequencing at 2–5x target depth.

Phase 2 imputes ancient genomes using GLIMPSE2 with Prof. Orlando's reference panel. Aim 1 uses wIBS, PCA, ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) to test competing models of the founding event. Aim 2 tracks changes in the gene pool through identity-by-descent analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) and ROH-based inbreeding coefficients, with effective population size reconstructed using PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++ (Terhorst et al., 2017) and interpreted against Iceland's documented livestock crises.

Phase 3 runs genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, iHS, PBS) and tracks allele frequencies through time at gait loci (DMRT3, STAU2, RELN), coat colour and vision loci (PMEL17, ASIP, MC1R), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012), and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019).

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a real research project for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology — a field where no established training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland. Two project options are available.

**Option 1:** The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation (biology or biological anthropology). As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2** (archaeology or biological anthropology): paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík, one of the largest Viking Age burial sites in Iceland, where six of the thirteen humans have been sequenced at sufficient depth for population-level analysis as part of the ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), and eight horse burials are associated. The student will examine genetic relationships among the horses and their connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. A student with a background in zooarchaeology could also help identify horse remains within the broader osteological collection at the Museum, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as animal bones — screening work that would directly expand the pool of candidate specimens.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir and Prof. Agnar Helgason. Both projects are designed to result in a publishable manuscript within a 12-month research period. Six months of salary support is included in the budget.

## G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT PLAN

This project builds on an established research programme in ancient genomics and represents a deliberate step into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. The PI completed her PhD at the University of Iceland in 2023 and has since developed a research programme in ancient genomics, with multiple publications in the field and a companion study of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes currently in preparation. Co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) — a study encompassing 901 modern horse genomes and 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel, characterising the global genomic ancestry of modern horse breeds — established the international collaboration, the reference panel, and the analytical tools this project will build on.

**Scientific development.** Leading this project will develop the PI's skills in horse-specific population genomic analysis, temporal tracking of trait-associated variants, and leading a multi-partner genomic project — expanding her methodological range beyond human population genomics and building her experience in multi-partner genomic projects.

**Conference participation and international visibility.** The PI will present results at the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE) annual meeting — the two primary international venues for ancient DNA and population genomics — with Aim 1 results targeted for presentation at ISBA in Year 2, coinciding with first manuscript submission. Results will also be presented at relevant Icelandic scientific meetings to ensure visibility within the domestic research community.

**Institutional development.** This project strengthens the host institution's position as an active contributor to ancient genomics, a field in which Iceland's unique archaeological and historical resources give it a natural comparative advantage. Training a Master's student creates a concrete local capacity-building outcome.

**Long-term trajectory.** The long-term goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. This project is the scientific foundation of that programme: it generates the core dataset, establishes the key international collaborations, and produces the publication record needed to compete for independent research funding at the European level. The results are designed to form the basis of a future European Research Council application (ERC Starting Grant), which would expand the ancient animal genomics programme to additional Icelandic archaeological species and time periods.
