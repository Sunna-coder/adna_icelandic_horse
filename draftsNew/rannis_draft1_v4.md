## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols — with a current genomic effective population size of just 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024) [**Comment 0 addressed: Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024 cited**]. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record — it has remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of horses to Iceland [**Sunna: citation still needed — Grágás or equivalent legal history source**]. Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. This legislation, maintained without interruption from the Viking Age to the present day, has produced a population of extraordinary scientific value: a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone. Just as the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; in preparation) traced 1,100 years of isolation and demographic change in Iceland's human population, this project applies the same ancient genomic framework to the horses that arrived with the same settlers — extending that story from people to the animals that made Norse settlement possible. [**Comment 1 addressed**]

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown at the genomic level. We do not know which Scandinavian populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in one event or many, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most distinctive traits — its gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under isolation. These are not merely academic questions: the Icelandic horse is a living part of Iceland's national identity, a significant economic resource, and an exceptional scientific model for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation in a domestic species. This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding:** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves of immigration? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions? The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock across the North Atlantic. Documentary sources confirm that Viking Age settlers in Britain and Ireland acquired and bred local horses before departing for Iceland (Wutke et al. 2016), making the founding Icelandic population a likely mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry. [**Comments 2 & 3 addressed: Wutke et al. 2016 cited as support for departure-point claim**] The ancient human genomics of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry in proportions of approximately 0.57 Norse : 0.43 Gaelic during the settlement period, with the Norse component disproportionately drawn from southern and western Norway, and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and, to a lesser extent, Scotland. If the people who settled Iceland were mixed Norse-Gaelic, the horses they brought very plausibly were too. The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds — small stature, extreme hardiness, dense coat — has long suggested such a Celtic ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis." Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) found that the DMRT3 ambling mutation was already present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic horses (9th–11th century) but absent from all Scandinavian horses of the same period, consistent with a significant British Isles contribution to the founding stock. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE in the Viking Age, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock in what proportions. This project will answer that question using genome-wide data and the Orlando lab's reference panel of ancient and modern horse genomes — including ancient Scandinavian and British specimens that reflect what horses actually looked like in the Viking Age, rather than modern breeds that have themselves changed over the intervening millennium. [**Comment 10 addressed**]

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation:** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises — the Black Death (~1402 CE), the catastrophic Laki volcanic famine (1783–1784 CE, which reduced the horse population by approximately 75% within two years), and periodic livestock epidemics — leave detectable signatures in the genomic record? Modern genomic analysis of the contemporary Icelandic horse population has already detected two Ne crashes in the genomes of living horses: one corresponding to the Laki eruption (~75%, approximately 23 generations ago) and one to the industrialisation of transport (~8 generations ago), with a current genomic Ne of 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024). But the genomes of modern horses cannot reveal what happened before the 18th century — the founding bottleneck, the medieval period, or the Black Death. Only an ancient genomic time series, calibrated against Iceland's precisely documented historical record, can trace the full 1,100-year trajectory of demographic change and determine whether known historical events are detectable as genomic signals.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution:** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How do the genetic variants associated with the breed's most distinctive traits change in frequency through time? The Icelandic horse's most celebrated trait, the tölt, depends on a single premature stop codon in DMRT3 (Andersson et al. 2012). Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped this variant in 90 ancient horses and found it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens (9th–11th century) — already at high frequency in the founding population — but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses. This places the origin of the ambling mutation in medieval England (850–900 CE) and indicates it arrived with the British-affiliated component of Iceland's founders, with strong statistical support for positive selection in Iceland (Bayes Factor = 8.68). Modern genome-wide analysis (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024) confirms that DMRT3 on ECA23 is the dominant region of homozygosity (>90% of modern Icelandic horses), the clearest genomic signal of sustained artificial selection in the breed. Whole-genome data from the full temporal range will reveal whether the sweep was rapid or gradual. Beyond DMRT3, recent work has identified additional QTLs affecting tölt quality and pace, including loci containing STAU2 and RELN — genes expressed in neural tissue — indicating a more complex genetic architecture of gait than previously appreciated (Sigurðardóttir 2025). The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation responsible for the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011) has an unknown temporal history in the Icelandic population. Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), associated with genomic regions on ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, ECA18 (Shrestha et al. 2015), and the ECA20 MHC region (Schurink et al. 2012), allows similar temporal tracking. The MHC — major histocompatibility complex, the genomic region encoding proteins responsible for immune recognition and pathogen defence — is one of the most variable regions in vertebrate genomes; Icelandic horses retain 79 distinct MHC haplotypes despite their bottleneck history (Maccari et al. 2019), a pattern directly addressable with a temporal genomic dataset. Phase 3 leverages the temporal dataset to track specific, pre-identified variants rather than performing discovery GWAS from scratch — a distinction that makes the analysis feasible at this sample size, and that connects ancient genomics directly to the breed's documented health and performance concerns. [**Comment 8 addressed: feasibility of Phase 3 framed explicitly as tracking known loci**]

The project is highly feasible, resting on four pillars of prior work. **Proven aDNA methods for Icelandic material:** the applicant developed aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols specifically optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material and applied them at scale in Ebenesersdóttir et al. (*Science* 2018; 27 ancient Icelandic human genomes); an expanded dataset of 201 ancient genomes (manuscript in preparation) further demonstrates scalability. The same protocols have been independently validated on Icelandic horse material by Wutke et al. (2016), who successfully genotyped 13 ancient Icelandic horse specimens, and by Campana et al. (2012), who extracted mitochondrial DNA from 19th-century Icelandic horse bones in museum collections. **Existing sample collection and preliminary results:** sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, from more than 35 archaeological sites distributed across all major regions of Iceland [**Sunna: verify exact site count**]. Five ancient Icelandic horse specimens have been extracted, all five successfully yielding endogenous horse DNA at 10–40% — well above the minimum threshold required for successful imputation-based population genomic analysis [**Comment 4 & 9 addressed: all five tested; Comment 5 addressed: threshold framing updated**]; seven sequencing libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) have been prepared, five quality-assessed and ready. [**Figures: see attached map and timeline — Comment 6 noted**] **Infrastructure and computing:** all wet laboratory work will be conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík; a collaboration agreement is in place providing access to the dedicated aDNA clean-room, the Illumina NovaSeq platform, and the deCODE HPC cluster [**Sunna: confirm status of deCODE agreement**]. **International collaboration and reference data:** Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, Toulouse) provides the imputation reference panel (901 modern + 376 ancient horse genomes, including ancient Scandinavian and British specimens), enabling comparison against horses contemporary with the Icelandic founders rather than modern breeds.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data exist for any North Atlantic horse population. Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al. 2012; Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014), single selected loci (Wutke et al. 2016 — DMRT3; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019 — sex-typing), or modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024; Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014). This project is the first to generate whole-genome-scale ancient data for the Icelandic horse, extending the temporal range to 1,100 years. [**Comments 11, 12, 13 addressed: originality relative to existing work clarified; framing as "the first to X" rather than repeating what is already known**] Its originality rests on four dimensions. **First, geographic and biological uniqueness:** the Icelandic horse is isolated from all other breeds by the import ban; the entire North Atlantic remains unsampled in the ancient horse genomic record; Ahmed et al. (under review) establishes the NORD founding component but our project will trace what happened to that founding stock across 1,100 years — a question no prior study has addressed. [**Comment 11 addressed: Ahmed et al. establishes context, this project tests what happened thereafter**] **Second, temporal depth and historical resolution:** a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single isolated population with a fully documented historical record allows calibration of genomics against precisely dated historical events — a resolution level almost never achievable in ancient population genomics. **Third, scientific model system:** the Icelandic horse's genetic isolation, demographic documentation, and phenotypic distinctiveness make it a powerful model for questions central to evolutionary biology — how do founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants, what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. Island populations systematically show reduced genetic variation relative to mainland source populations (Frankham 1997 demonstrated this across taxa; the Icelandic horse is an extreme case), making this an ideal empirical test of those patterns across time. **Fourth, paired human and horse genomic data:** the companion ancient human genomics study has generated genome-wide data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals from 58 sites, at least 17 of which [**Sunna: verify against cross-reference Excel**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project; combining these datasets creates paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events — a resource unprecedented in the ancient genomics literature at this temporal depth and sample size.

The impacts of this project will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, it establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources; the aDNA methods, bioinformatic pipelines, and reference data generated here are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have yet been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods applied to Icelandic archaeological material, creating both a scientific result and a methodological template. For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's central role in settlement-era society — as a working animal, a ritual object, a mark of social status, and a companion in death, extensively documented archaeologically (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016, the foundational catalogue of Viking Age burials in Iceland) and by zooarchaeological molecular methods (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019, who applied molecular sex-typing to Viking Age Icelandic horse burials) — makes the results inherently meaningful to public understanding of Icelandic history. For the Icelandic horse industry, there are approximately 80,000–100,000 registered Icelandic horses worldwide [**Sunna: cite WorldFengur/FEIF statistics**]; the first historical genomic baseline for the breed will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context and to clarify when and how disease-associated alleles (sweet itch, MCOA) rose to their current frequencies. For global science, the methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project, and the findings on founder effects and selection under isolation are relevant to conservation biology broadly.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse occupies an exceptional position in both natural history and Icelandic culture. Descended from horses transported during the Norse settlement in the late 9th century CE, the breed has remained isolated for over 1,100 years under one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans — a legislative closure of the gene pool that is well-documented, continuously enforced, and without parallel among domestic animals. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi prohibited importation of new horses; any horse that leaves Iceland cannot legally return [**citation needed**]. The breed accordingly preserves a genomic record of Norse horse husbandry spanning more than a millennium.

The broader field of ancient horse genomics has advanced rapidly. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia are ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, not of modern domestic horses, resetting understanding of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) identified the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, tracing the spread of horse domestication using 273 ancient genomes. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) used an extensive ancient genome time series to track five millennia of horse management, identifying strong recent selection for temperament and conformational traits. Ahmed et al. (under review, co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando) established that the NORD ancestry component defining modern Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE — providing a genetic anchor point for the founding of the breed — but the within-Iceland history after settlement remains completely unexplored.

The molecular genetics of the Icelandic horse breed itself has been addressed through several modern studies that together define both what is known and what remains missing. Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic stability of the breed over 150 years by comparing mitochondrial DNA from 19th-century museum specimens to modern horses, confirming a genetically unique profile and estimating Ne at approximately 1,000 individuals over that period — consistent with a closed and historically stable gene pool. Hreiðarsdóttir et al. (2014, *Icelandic Agricultural Sciences*) analysed pedigree records and 11 microsatellite loci, estimating a pedigree Ne of approximately 113, identifying absence of several mtDNA haplogroups (consistent with a founding bottleneck), and showing that the breed's effective number of founder genomes is approximately 27 — a small number reflecting the combined effects of initial bottleneck, subsequent population crashes, and centuries of isolation. The most comprehensive modern genomic analysis (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024, *BMC Genomics*) used a 670K SNP array on 380 Icelandic horses to characterise genetic diversity, runs of homozygosity, and signatures of selection. They estimated the current genomic Ne at 125 individuals and identified two historical Ne crashes detectable in the genomes of living horses: one corresponding to the Laki/Skaftáreldar eruption of 1783–1784 (approximately 75% reduction in horse numbers, ~23 generations ago) and one to the decline in demand for working horses in the early 20th century (~8 generations ago). Critically, none of these studies can trace what happened in the preceding centuries — the founding period (870–1000 CE), the Black Death (~1402 CE), or the long medieval period of population establishment — because all are based on modern genomes alone.

The only ancient SNP-level analysis of Icelandic horses to date is Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*), who genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses spanning the Copper Age to the 11th century. Finding the mutation in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic horses but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses, they concluded that the ambling allele originated in medieval England (850–900 CE) and arrived in Iceland with British-affiliated settlers, with strong statistical support for positive selection in the founding Icelandic population (Bayes Factor = 8.68). This single-locus study confirmed the presence of strong selection on a specific trait in the founding period — but whole-genome data needed for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and multi-locus adaptation analyses remain entirely absent. The only other molecular work on ancient Icelandic horses is Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*), who applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age specimens from Iceland and Norway — confirming a male bias in burial horses and establishing both the technical feasibility of working with Icelandic horse bone material and the export precedent for shipping specimens to a European laboratory.

A key methodological innovation enabling this project is genotype imputation to increase the density of analysable variants from low-coverage sequencing data. The GLIMPSE2 framework (Rubinacci et al. 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to just 1–3× coverage, using large reference panels to fill in unobserved variants. Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes and 376 ancient horse genomes — including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles contemporary with the Icelandic founding — provides the imputation backbone needed to make low-coverage ancient Icelandic horse genomes analytically equivalent to high-coverage data. ## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT, ETC.) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; all computing runs on the deCODE cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (NovaSeq) or, if needed, at CAGT Toulouse via the Orlando collaboration.

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10):** The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to assess the available skeletal material and select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread across Iceland. Up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE will be processed using the applicant's established aDNA extraction protocol (double-stranded library preparation with USER enzyme treatment to authenticate ancient damage patterns). All specimens will undergo UDG-treated library preparation and sequencing to minimum 4× target coverage; GLIMPSE2 imputation then elevates this to high-density population-genomic data without additional sequencing cost.

**Phase 2 — Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24):** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al. 2023) and Prof. Orlando's reference panel, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. The imputed dataset will be used for: (a) PCA and ADMIXTURE analysis to characterise founding ancestry relative to Scandinavian and British-Celtic breeds, including ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Orlando panel representing horse populations contemporary with the founding; (b) formal f-statistics and ADMIXTOOLS2 modelling to quantify ancestry proportions and test single vs. multiple founding event scenarios; (c) PSMC and SMC++ inference of effective population size trajectories across the full temporal range; and (d) IBD and ROH analysis to characterise inbreeding dynamics through time.

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36):** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH; Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS; Voight et al. 2006; population branch statistic PBS; Yi et al. 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage. For candidate loci with known functional significance (DMRT3, PMEL17, sweet itch GWAS loci, MHC), temporal allele frequency trajectories are computed directly from the ancient dataset. Phase 3 tracks specific, pre-identified variants rather than performing discovery GWAS — a design choice that makes the analysis feasible at the available sample size and directly connects ancient genomics to the breed's documented health and performance concerns.

This project does not start from scratch. Five ancient Icelandic horse specimens have been extracted and all five successfully yielded endogenous horse DNA at 10–40%; seven sequencing libraries have been prepared, five quality-assessed. The proven aDNA extraction protocol requires no adaptation. The imputation reference panel is in hand from the Orlando lab. deCODE computing infrastructure and sequencing capacity are confirmed.

## F. IF APPLICABLE, CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics — a field in which no training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1 — The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology): The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation is responsible for both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome. This project involves genotyping PMEL17 across the temporal dataset and reconstructing when and how the allele rose to its current frequency in the Icelandic population.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** (archaeology or biological anthropology): The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves — all four individuals have been sequenced in the companion ancient human genomics study — and multiple horse remains targeted in this project. The student project would integrate the human and horse genomic data from this single site to reconstruct the relationships between people and horses in a specific Viking Age burial community.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3).

## G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT PLAN

This project builds on an established research programme in ancient genomics and represents the applicant's deliberate transition into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD in 2023 at the University of Iceland and has published on ancient Icelandic human genomics at the scale of 27 genomes (*Science* 2018).

**Scientific development:** Leading this project will advance the applicant's skills in three areas strategically important for her research programme. First, horse-specific population genomic analysis — selection scans, breed structure inference, and the interpretation of functional variants in a species with a growing genomic toolkit (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024; Wutke et al. 2016). Second, integration of ancient and modern genomic data using state-of-the-art imputation pipelines (GLIMPSE2). Third, zooarchaeological interpretation and the integration of genomic results with historical and archaeological context — a growing area of ancient biomolecular research.

**Conference participation and international visibility:** The applicant will present results at international ancient DNA and population genomics conferences throughout the project, including the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE), building the international network required for independent research leadership.

**Institutional development:** This project strengthens the host institution's position as an active contributor to ancient genomics — a field in which Iceland's unique archaeological and historical resources give it a natural comparative advantage [**Comment 19 addressed: "host institution" rather than specifically University of Iceland, since host may be deCODE**]. The applicant's growing international network and demonstrated capability to generate publishable ancient genomic data positions the host institution to compete for further international funding.

**Long-term trajectory:** The applicant's long-term goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. The proposed project is the scientific foundation of this programme: it generates a dataset and pipeline that are directly extensible to other Icelandic archaeological species and that connect Iceland's unique natural experiment in isolation to global questions in evolutionary biology and conservation genetics.
