## A. Specific Aims, Research Questions, Feasibility, Originality, and Impact

### A.1 Overview and Research Questions

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE, it has remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years — maintained by one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, enacted by the Alþingi around 982 CE. This has produced a population of exceptional scientific value: a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record. Yet the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown. We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most distinctive traits evolved under isolation.

This project will generate the first genome-wide ancient DNA dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and address three specific aims.

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding**: What was the genomic composition of the founding horse population? Were horses introduced in a single event or multiple waves? The ancient human genomics of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry — many departing from Norse settlements in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland rather than directly from western Norway. Horses transported for colonisation (as distinct from raiding, where horses were acquired locally) would have reflected these departure points. A settler from the Hebrides would have brought horses carrying a mixture of Scandinavian and native Celtic or British pony ancestry. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve whether Celtic or British pony ancestry also contributed to the founding stock — the "Celtic pony hypothesis," which this project will test directly for the first time.

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation**: How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years? Did Iceland's major historical crises — the Black Death (~1402 CE), the Laki volcanic famine (1783–1784 CE), which reduced the horse population by an estimated 70% within two years — leave detectable genomic signatures? The known historical timeline allows a level of calibration between genomics and history that is almost never achievable.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution**: When did the *DMRT3* stop codon enabling the tölt reach fixation — from founding or gradually? How did the frequency of the *PMEL17* Silver/MCOA allele change through time? What genomic signatures of selection are detectable at loci associated with sweet itch susceptibility (ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, ECA18, ECA20/MHC; Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), metabolic adaptation, and immune diversity? Icelandic horses are also notable for exceptional longevity (routinely 30–35 years), connected in part to unexpectedly high MHC heterozygosity maintained under isolation (79 distinct MHC haplotypes despite bottleneck history; Maccari et al. 2019) — whether this diversity was present from founding or sustained by balancing selection is directly addressable here.

### A.2 Feasibility

The project rests on four pillars of prior work.

**Proven methods**: aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols developed by the applicant at deCODE Genetics were applied to recover genome-wide data from 88 ancient Icelandic human genomes (*Science* 2018) and subsequently 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation). Seven ancient Icelandic horse specimens processed using the same protocol yielded 10–40% endogenous horse DNA — well above the ≥1% threshold required for genomic analysis — confirming that Icelandic horse material preserves aDNA at excellent levels.

**Sample collection**: Sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, covering burial contexts (*kuml*), animal bone deposits (*dys*), and assemblages from sites across Iceland. Seven libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) are already prepared and ready for Phase 2.

**Infrastructure**: All wet laboratory work will be conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík, under the applicant's collaboration agreement, which provides access to the dedicated aDNA clean-room laboratory, Illumina NovaSeq sequencing platform, and high-performance computing cluster. No new infrastructure investment is required.

**International collaboration**: The project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), co-author on Ahmed et al. (under review), whose laboratory provides the horse imputation reference panel (901 modern + 376 ancient genomes) and validated imputation pipeline on which Phase 2 depends.

### A.3 Originality

No ancient genomic data exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic. The Icelandic horse is the biological descendant of Viking Age Norse horse husbandry, preserved by the import ban. Four dimensions make this project unlike any previously published study: (1) it is the first genome-wide investigation of an North Atlantic horse population; (2) a 1,100-year dataset from a single population with a fully documented historical record allows calibration of genomics against precisely dated events; (3) the Icelandic horse is a model system for evolutionary questions about founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and selection in a closed population (Frankham 1997); and (4) the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) provides human genomic data from the same burial sites — including sites documented in *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) — creating paired human-horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events that does not exist anywhere else in the world.

### A.4 Impact

**For Icelandic science**: This project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods and pipelines generated here are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have been studied at the genomic level.

**For cultural heritage**: The horse's role in settlement-era Icelandic society — as working animal, ritual object, and companion in death (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019) — is well documented. Tracing its genetic founding will contribute directly to public understanding of Icelandic history, disseminated through the National Museum of Iceland.

**For the horse industry**: There are approximately 80,000–100,000 registered Icelandic horses in Iceland and over 100,000 abroad in more than 30 countries. Sweet itch affects an estimated 30–40% of exported horses; ocular anomalies linked to *PMEL17* are a recognised breeding concern. The first historical genomic baseline for the breed will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context.

**For global science**: The methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project. The findings on founder effects, sustained inbreeding, and selection under isolation are relevant to conservation biology broadly.

## B. Present State of Knowledge in the Field

The Icelandic horse is genetically distinctive: low within-breed diversity consistent with its closed history (Petersen et al. 2013), two unique gaits controlled by a *DMRT3* stop codon (Andersson et al. 2012), a heritable disease profile including MCOA from the *PMEL17* Silver mutation (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011), and unexpectedly high MHC diversity despite its bottleneck history (Maccari et al. 2019). None of its genomic history through time has been investigated.

The broader field has advanced rapidly. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) separated the Botai horse lineage from domestic horse ancestry. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) localised horse domestication to the lower Volga-Don steppe ca. 2000 BCE (see also Warmuth et al. 2012). Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) documented diversity loss during breed formation across 278 ancient equid genomes. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic and Scandinavian breeds as emerging around 810 CE — the most direct context for this project.

Despite this progress, the entire North Atlantic remains unsampled. The only ancient DNA study of Icelandic horses (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019) sex-typed 22 specimens by molecular methods but generated no population-genomic data. The applicant's methods (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018) directly establish feasibility: the same protocols that recovered genome-wide data from 1,100-year-old Icelandic human remains will be applied to horse specimens from the same contexts. Genotype imputation using the GLIMPSE framework (Rubinacci et al. 2021) with Prof. Orlando's reference panel enables robust population-genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced at 1–5× coverage, and has been validated for ancient horse genomes in Ahmed et al.

## C. Research Plan

### C.1 Overview

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; all computing runs on the deCODE cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CAGT Toulouse. The principal risk — low endogenous DNA in some specimens — is mitigated by a large sample pool (up to 90) and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in seven existing Icelandic horse libraries.

### C.2 Methodology

**Phase 1 — Sample processing** (Months 1–10): Skeletal material (tooth roots prioritised; cortical and petrous bone where teeth are unavailable) is selected with curators at the National Museum to maximise temporal and geographic coverage. Silica-based DNA extraction is performed in the deCODE aDNA clean-room (UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure). Double-stranded Illumina libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing. Shallow shotgun sequencing (~1M reads/library) assesses endogenous content and confirms aDNA damage patterns (mapDamage2; Jónsson et al. 2013). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA proceed to deep sequencing (2–5× target) on the Illumina NovaSeq. The bioinformatic pipeline applies AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al. 2016), BWA mapping to EquCab3 (Li and Durbin 2009), Picard MarkDuplicates, and mapDamage2 base-quality rescaling. Biological sex is estimated from X/Y read ratios.

**Phase 2 — Ancestry and demography** (Months 9–24): Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE (Rubinacci et al. 2021) and Prof. Orlando's 901-genome modern horse reference panel, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. *Aim 1* (Months 12–22): PCA, ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al. 2009), and f-statistics (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al. 2012) characterise founding ancestry and test the Celtic pony hypothesis — whether horses with British or Celtic pony ancestry contributed alongside Scandinavian stock. *Aim 2* (Months 12–24): IBD analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al. 2020), ROH, and demographic modelling (PSMC; Li and Durbin 2011; SMC++; Terhorst et al. 2017) reconstruct inbreeding and effective population size through time, interpreted against the historical record of the Laki famine, Black Death, and other crises.

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and traits** (Months 20–36): *Aim 3*: Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH; Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS; Voight et al. 2006; PBS; Yi et al. 2010) identify selected regions in the Icelandic lineage. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at *DMRT3* (tölt), *ASIP*/*MC1R*/*PMEL17* (coat colour and MCOA), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), and MHC/immune regions. ROH identifies regions where inbreeding elevated deleterious variant frequency.

## F. Contribution of Doctoral and Master's Students

One Master's student from the University of Iceland (biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology) will be integrated into the project. Two defined project options are available, with the final choice based on the recruited student's background.

**Option 1 — History of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** *(biology or biological anthropology)*: The *PMEL17* Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and MCOA syndrome (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). The student will extract genotype calls at *PMEL17* and related coat-colour loci from the imputed ancient genomes and track allele frequencies across 870–1900 CE — a fully analytical project requiring no additional laboratory work, completable within 12 months.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** *(archaeology or biological anthropology)*: The burial ground at Sílastaðir contains four sequenced human individuals (companion human genomics study) alongside two horse burials — one accompanying a warrior burial with sword, axes, and spearhead; one accompanying a second warrior burial. The student will examine whether the two horses are related, whether their genomic profiles (sex, ancestry, inbreeding) correlate with the human assemblages, and whether they connect genetically to horses at other Icelandic sites. The student will participate in laboratory processing of the Sílastaðir specimens in Phase 1 and carry out population genomic analysis in Phase 2.

Both students will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary) and Prof. Helgason (co-supervisor), training the first Icelandic researchers in ancient animal genomics methods.

## G. Career Development Plan

This project represents the applicant's transition from ancient human genomics into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD at the University of Iceland and has published on ancient Icelandic human genomics (*Science* 2018; manuscript in preparation with 201 genomes). Co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) marks her deliberate extension into the animal genomics field that this project is designed to establish as her primary programme.

**Scientific development**: The project will expand the applicant's expertise into horse-specific population genomics, temporal trait variant analysis, and the management of multi-partner international genomic projects. These skills are strategically important for establishing an independent research group.

**Conference participation**: Results will be presented at the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE) annual meeting, as well as relevant Icelandic scientific meetings, building international visibility throughout the grant period.

**Long-term trajectory**: The applicant's goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. This project generates the core dataset, establishes the key international collaborations, and produces the publication record required to compete for independent European-level funding — specifically, the results are designed to form the scientific basis of a future ERC Starting Grant application.
