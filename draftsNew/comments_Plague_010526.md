## Abstract

**Ab1 [MINOR] Final sentence is misleading.** "our results indicate notable changes in the patterns of long-distance mobility into Lund, Trondheim and Vilnius before the second plague pandemic" — this reads as if the finding concerns the period *before* the pandemic, when the main finding is the decline in diversity *after* the arrival of the Black Death. The sentence should read something like: "our results indicate a marked reduction in long-distance immigration coinciding with the arrival of the second plague pandemic, such that populations became notably more homogeneous in ancestry in the post-BD period."

**Ab2 [MINOR] "Arthur, 2010" — author name unclear.** In the first sentence, the citation for 40–60% mortality estimates is listed as "Arthur, 2010." This is an unusual citation for plague mortality. Verify this is correct — it may be a mis-spelled name (Aberth 2010?). Confirm full reference.

## Discussion — detailed review

### General assessment

The Discussion is clearly written and engages seriously with the two main findings: (1) no evidence of strong selection and (2) a consistent decline in the diversity of ancestry. The strongest paragraphs are [157] (cosmopolitan pre-BD cities, Christianity, Iceland/Trondheim connection) and [161] (Vilnius specifics). The weakest is the closing paragraph [163], which relies on vague language and misses the opportunity to synthesise the findings into a single interpretive statement. Specific comments follow.

---

**D1 [MAJOR] Alternative hypothesis not addressed: did plague disproportionately kill the outliers?** Para [155] interprets the decline in ancestry diversity as evidence of "reduced long-distance immigration." However, the null hypothesis — that it reflects differential mortality, i.e., that individuals of non-local ancestry died at higher rates during plague outbreaks — is not explicitly discussed or ruled out. A reviewer may raise this, even though the paper's own data effectively refute it. In Lund, outliers drop from ~52% pre-BD to ~9% post-BD. For differential mortality to account for a shift of that magnitude, outliers would need to have died at ~90%+ while locals died at close to 0% — implausible given observed overall plague mortality rates. Moreover, the selection scan found no genetic variants significantly affecting survival, making it internally contradictory to invoke ancestry-based differential mortality at the scale required. The Discussion should pre-empt this with one sentence, for example: "While it is conceivable that individuals of non-local ancestry suffered higher mortality through socioeconomic disadvantage or differential access to care, the magnitude of the shift (52% to 9% outliers in Lund) implies a degree of differential mortality that is mathematically implausible given observed overall plague mortality rates, and is further contradicted by our finding that no genetic variants significantly affected survival."

**D2 [MAJOR] No discussion of what specifically changed: trade networks, ecclesiastical networks, or general population collapse?** Para [159] mentions merchants, clerics, and craftsmen, and para [157] discusses Christianity as a pull factor, but the Discussion never synthesises these into a single explanatory model. Was the collapse in diversity driven primarily by the disruption of ecclesiastical networks (which would explain the particularly strong effect in Lund, the archiepiscopal centre, and the Icelandic connection to Trondheim)? Or by the collapse of the Hanseatic trade network? Or both? The data may not resolve this, but the Discussion should stake out a position or at minimum present the alternatives and what further data would be needed.

**D3 [MODERATE] "Coincidental" is the wrong word.** Para [163]: "It was coincidental that our study, designed to identify such variants, revealed a fairly drastic decline in the diversity of ancestry." "Coincidental" implies the finding was accidental or fortuitous rather than scientifically meaningful. Replace with something like "While our study was primarily designed to identify such variants, it serendipitously revealed..." or, better, simply remove the framing: "Our analysis of more than 500 ancient genomes revealed a drastic decline in the diversity of ancestry..."

**D4 [MODERATE] Closing paragraph is too diffuse.** Para [163] serves as the conclusion of the Discussion but covers several different points in loose succession: evolutionary timescale argument for why selection was not found, the ancestry finding, a brief synthesis, and a final statement about sample size needs. The paragraph should be restructured as a genuine conclusion: restate both key findings, state what they mean together for understanding the social and genetic impact of the second plague pandemic, and end with a forward-looking sentence about what future data would resolve. The current ending — "much larger numbers of ancient genomes will be needed to determine whether it altered the frequency of genetic variants" — is technically accurate but undersells the positive finding (the ancestry collapse), which is the paper's most important result.

**D5 [MODERATE] The Iceland/Trondheim connection deserves more discussion.** Para [157] notes that seven of the 15 pre-BD outliers in Trondheim show Icelandic ancestry, supported by strontium isotopes for three individuals. This is a striking and specific result — nearly half of all non-Scandinavian individuals in pre-BD Trondheim appear to be Icelandic. The explanation offered (archbishopric, king's residence, Icelandic texts describing travel to Trondheim) is correct but compressed. This finding merits its own short paragraph or at least an expanded treatment. In particular: does this provide independent genetic corroboration of specific historical accounts? Does it tell us something about the scale of Icelandic travel to the Norwegian mainland? And why does no analogous signal appear in Lund?

**D6 [MINOR] "It seems unlikely" — epistemic hedge could be stronger.** Para [157]: "It seems unlikely that the extent of long-range immigration required to account for such large proportions of outliers among the pre-BD period could have been longstanding." This is an important interpretive claim — that the pre-BD cosmopolitanism was not a steady-state but a temporally specific phenomenon. It deserves more than an "it seems unlikely." Rephrase: "The scale of long-range immigration implied by the proportion of pre-BD outliers — 52% in Lund — is implausible as a longstanding steady state. These patterns most plausibly reflect a historically bounded window of intense mobility..."

**D7 [MINOR] Little Ice Age citation.** Para [159]: "Collet et al. 2025" — verify this is published or in press and add DOI. As a very recent paper it may not be widely known to reviewers and a brief parenthetical description of the finding would help (e.g., "which dendrochronological evidence associates with crop failure and famine in 14th-century Scandinavia (Collet et al., 2025)").

**D8 [MINOR] No polygenic selection discussion.** Para [153] concludes that "the effect was likely modest or spread across many loci rather than concentrated in large-effect variants." This opens the door to polygenic selection as an alternative, but the Discussion does not pursue it. At minimum add one sentence acknowledging that polygenic selection — distributed across thousands of common variants with individually tiny effects — is beyond the resolution of current ancient DNA studies, and cite one relevant methodological paper (e.g., Mathieson 2021 or equivalent).

**D9 [MINOR] Comparison with Beneker et al. 2025 and Hui et al. 2024 is brief.** Para [153] cites whole-genome studies that also found no compelling evidence for selection, but does not explain where this study adds over them — what makes 500+ genomes from three sites more informative than what Beneker and Hui provided? One sentence explaining the added value (geographic spread, sample size, temporal resolution) would pre-empt a reviewer asking why this study was needed.

**D10 [MINOR] "It is important to bear in mind that in evolutionary terms, the majority of the mortality occurred during a very brief period."** Para [163]. This is the key argument for why strong selection was unlikely to be detectable, and it is correct — but it appears late, as a throw-away clause, rather than as the lead argument in the first selection paragraph. Consider moving it to Para [153] as the opening or second sentence of the selection discussion, since it frames the entire null result.
