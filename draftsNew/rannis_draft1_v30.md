# [OPEN ITEM 1 — DECISION: confirm title] Brought/Bred by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation

## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse has been isolated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Viking-Age settlers brought horses to Iceland in the late ninth century CE (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). Since at least the Commonwealth era, importation has been forbidden, a ban still enforced under Act No. 54/1990. WorldFengur, the breed's international studbook (Lorange, 2011), documents parentage for more than 354,000 registered horses with records back to individuals born around 1860. Before that, the breed's genomic history was largely unknown: no ancient horse genomes from Iceland existed at the depth needed for population-level inference. This project will generate the first such dataset: approximately 150 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, together with whole-genome sequences from 100–150 present-day horses as a modern reference panel. The study is organised around three scientific aims.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the ancestry profile of the horses that arrived with the Viking-Age settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of distinct breeds from different regions in Scandinavia and the British Isles?

The proportional contributions of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock cannot be resolved from present-day data alone. This project will determine those proportions for the first time, using genome-wide ancient DNA from approximately 150 Icelandic specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, modelled against modern horses from more than 100 breeds and ancient specimens from Britain and Scandinavia spanning the Viking Age (Ahmed et al., under review; Figure 1).

Strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) will add an individual-level perspective that complements the genomic data. Because tooth enamel reflects the geology of the region where an animal was raised, this approach can distinguish horses born in Iceland from those brought from abroad. Combined with genomic ancestry estimates, this allows us to test whether Icelandic horses derive from a single founding event or multiple introductions over time. While genomics reveals population ancestry, strontium identifies where individual animals spent their early life. This work builds on established strontium frameworks developed for Icelandic archaeological sites and will be carried out in collaboration with established isotope laboratories. *[OPEN ITEM 16 — PREPARE: get a formal quote from Durham for the horse strontium work. **Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Whole-genome sequences will be generated for 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses in collaboration with Prof. Björnsdóttir: a subset at high coverage (15–30x) to expand the imputation reference panel with Icelandic-specific haplotypes, the remainder at low coverage (1–3x) and imputed using that panel, providing a present-day anchor for demographic modelling at the same genomic resolution as the ancient dataset. The temporal series of ancient genomes will then allow the reconstruction of effective population size, the tracking of inbreeding over time, and the testing of whether documented crises left detectable genomic signatures.

Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes; no outside stallion has contributed to the breed in the modern record (Weinzerl, 2024). How far back that exclusivity goes will be revealed by the ancient male specimens. The X chromosome carries a different kind of signal: transmitted more often through females than males, its effective population size is three-quarters that of the autosomes, and it amplifies the signal left by unequal use of stallions and mares. Bottlenecks driven by uneven stallion use can appear in X-chromosomal data even when the autosomal signal is unremarkable.

WorldFengur's genealogical depth creates a further opportunity: an ancestral recombination graph (ARG) for the breed. An ARG is a map of the genealogical pathways each variant took on its way to living horses; when that map is tied to WorldFengur's pedigree records, questions like when DMRT3 reached fixation or which founder line first carried the PMEL17 Silver allele become answerable at nucleotide resolution. Combined with the present-day whole-genome sequences and ancient imputed genomes from this project, this would be the first ARG for any domestic animal anchored to a verified pedigree database. The Icelandic horse is the natural candidate: a small population, a closed gene pool, a complete studbook, and now ancient DNA spanning the full breeding history.

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits emerge and change over more than a millennium? Are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unexpected targets not previously identified in this breed?

Iceland's historical record is unusually precise: volcanic chronology, livestock censuses, and written sources from the settlement era provide fixed dates against which genomic changes can be anchored. Six trait systems will be tracked:

*Gait.* DMRT3 on ECA23 controls the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). The allele was already present in 9th–11th-century Icelandic specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), but when it became fixed is unknown. STAU2 and RELN (Sigurðardóttir, 2025) will be tracked alongside it.

*Coat colour — full palette.* The Icelandic horse carries more than 40 recognised colours with over 100 Icelandic-language names for colour variants — an exceptionally wide palette by any breed standard. Unlike most breeds where only a narrow range of colours is permitted, the official Icelandic breeding goal explicitly preserves all naturally occurring colours; variety is actively encouraged. This breadth is itself a scientific question: was it present from the founding, or did it accumulate under deliberate Icelandic breeding selection across the post-settlement centuries? Wild horses before domestication showed virtually no colour variation, carrying predominantly bay or bay-dun coats; coat colour diversity increased sharply during domestication, driven by human selection (Ludwig et al., 2009, Science). Colour-associated loci across the genome — MC1R and ASIP (base coat), KIT (pinto and sabino patterns), MATP (cream dilute), TYRP1, TRPM1 (leopard complex), TBX3 (dun) — will be tracked through the temporal dataset to reconstruct the founding colour palette and identify when specific variants entered or rose in frequency. This is the first time the colour history of the Icelandic horse will be addressed using genomic data.

*Coat colour and vision health.* PMEL17 Arg618Cys causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). When this allele entered the breed is unknown; tracking its frequency from 870 CE will show whether MCOA was a founding-era liability, with direct consequences for breeding management.

*Body size.* The Icelandic horse is compact — 125–145 cm at the withers. Whether this size reflects the founding stock or was shaped by later conditions is itself one of the questions this project will address. HMGA2 on ECA6 is the primary genetic locus for body size variation in ponies and small horse breeds (Norton et al., 2019), in addition to its role in Equine Metabolic Syndrome (EMS). To determine whether the breed's compact size stems from its founding population or from later selection under recurrent famine, HMGA2 allele frequencies will be tracked through time and compared with osteological estimates of withers height. *[FLAG — unverified claim: "Breed experts have noted that the Icelandic horse may have been getting larger in recent decades, though this has not been formally documented." — include only if you can cite a source or attribute this to a named person. Otherwise remove.]* Standardised long-bone measurements, collected by Dr Joe Walser III, will serve as an independent phenotypic record to test these alternatives. *[OPEN ITEM 18 — VERIFY: confirm the 125–145 cm height range is correct.]*

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Up to half of Icelandic horses develop IBH on first exposure to *Culicoides* midges after export to mainland Europe. The midges do not occur in Iceland, so genetic susceptibility is widespread within the breed but the condition itself is never seen there (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). The temporal dataset will provide the first historical context for when IBH-associated alleles entered the breed and whether the 79 MHC haplotypes exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019) reflect founding diversity or later balancing selection.

*Metabolic disease susceptibility.* In Icelandic horses, equine metabolic syndrome (EMS) is marked by insulin dysregulation, weight gain, and pasture-related laminitis. Candidate loci include FOXO1 on ECA17 and HMGA2 on ECA6 (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Norton et al., 2019). The hypothesis is that repeated episodes of famine favoured alleles promoting metabolic thrift, and that these same variants now predispose the breed to EMS under abundant nutrition. Comparing temporal allele-frequency patterns with known famine events offers a direct test.

Genome-wide selection scans across the full temporal dataset will also search for signatures of positive and purifying selection at loci not yet studied in the Icelandic horse.

**Originality.** Studies of the Icelandic horse have so far relied on mitochondrial DNA and microsatellites (Campana et al., 2012), present-day SNP array genotypes from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), pedigree records (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), and two forms of single-locus ancient genotyping: sex chromosome read ratios (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019) and the DMRT3 variant (Wutke et al., 2016). None reaches the coverage needed for population-level inference. The only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses produced 22 low-depth genomes, of which only two entered a global population survey and neither was imputed (Fages et al., 2019). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse: the first capable of population structure inference, demographic reconstruction, and selection analysis across the full post-settlement history of the breed. The originality has four dimensions.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any North Atlantic horse population: not from Iceland, the Faroe Islands, or Greenland. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record has almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics. The ancient genomes, WorldFengur pedigree records, SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and 100–150 new present-day whole-genome sequences together span nearly the full history of the breed from 870 CE to the present. No other domestic animal comes close: we have the archaeology, the genealogy, the genomics, and a verified import ban, all for the same population.

Long-term isolation studies are usually hampered by the very kind of documentation gaps that the Icelandic case lacks. Elsewhere, founding events are poorly constrained, import histories are contested, and population records thin out rapidly. Here, the settlement is dated to within a generation, the prohibition on import is legally documented from the Commonwealth period, and written accounts of the population and its crises extend back to the 12th century. That means the genomic time series can be anchored to real events rather than estimated dates — a level of resolution that is genuinely rare in ancient animal genomics.

Island populations generally show reduced diversity relative to their mainland sources (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an acute case of this pattern. The most direct parallel is the human population that crossed the same ocean: Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science) showed how hard drift has worked on the Icelandic human gene pool across the same 1,100-year span.

The applicant's ongoing ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (kuml) documented in Kuml og haugfé (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from the same Viking Age burial contexts, at this combination of temporal depth and sample size, exists nowhere else.

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including sheep, cattle, and dogs, and the work creates a template for future projects on those species.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse was central to life in the settlement period. It served as a working animal, a marker of status, and played a role in ritual practices such as pagan sacrifice and stallion contests (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). Horses held a strong place in Norse belief, reflected in Icelandic burials where they were often placed alongside people. Understanding where the founding horses came from therefore also speaks to the settlers themselves — their origins, the routes they travelled, and the animals they brought with them across the North Atlantic.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

Horses are one of the most widespread domestic animals, with about 60 million individuals and more than 500 recognised breeds worldwide (Petersen et al., 2013; FAO FAOSTAT, 2024). Much of today's genetic landscape is dominated by a small number of intensively managed lineages: well-studied breeds such as the Thoroughbred, Arabian, and American Quarter Horse carry clear signals of sustained selection for performance traits (Fages et al., 2019). The Icelandic horse became isolated before many of these dominant modern breeds took their current form, and may retain genetic diversity that has since been lost or reduced on the mainland. Few domestic animal populations combine their age with verified isolation, a well-documented historical record, and material amenable to population genomic inference.

Ancient DNA research has greatly expanded knowledge of horse evolution over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, Science) showed that the Botai horses of Central Asia, previously thought to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact ancestral to Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. The lower Volga-Don region (present-day southern Russia and Ukraine) has since been established as the origin of modern domestic horse ancestry, with a rapid expansion across Eurasia from around 2000 BCE (Librado et al., 2021, Nature). A study spanning five millennia of horse genomes revealed two now-extinct lineages and a marked drop in diversity at the point when modern breeds were formed during the 17th to 19th centuries (Fages et al., 2019, Cell). Ahmed et al. (under review), on which Agnar is a corresponding author and Sunna a co-author, characterise seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from 901 modern horse genomes and 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that reference panel. They establish that Icelandic horses are characterised by the NORD ancestry component that emerged at approximately 810 CE — close to the time horses and humans first arrived in Iceland. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

The question of where the founding Icelandic horses came from has a long research history, and a coherent picture is emerging — though critical gaps remain. Norway has been the primary candidate from the start. Morphological comparisons pointed early to the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage (Eldjárn, 1991), and subsequent field comparisons confirmed that five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform gaits related to the tölt (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998) — though tölt performance in Nordland/Lyngen horses does not resolve whether the DMRT3 allele controlling it was shared through a common ancestral population or arrived independently. Microsatellite analyses later grouped the Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses in the same phylogenetic cluster with strong bootstrap support (Bjørnstad et al., 2003), in line with historical evidence for their introduction from Norway during the Viking Age. A British Isles contribution has also attracted sustained attention, supported initially by shared morphological features with Atlantic-fringe breeds — the so-called Celtic pony hypothesis (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998) — and now by several converging lines of genomic evidence. The DMRT3 ambling allele was present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses in the dataset, yet found in two horses from 850–900 CE at Jorvik (York), pointing to its arrival in Iceland via settlers coming from or through Britain (Wutke et al., 2016). The only two ancient Icelandic genomes with sufficient depth for global placement cluster closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses from Britain and Scandinavia, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse (Fages et al., 2019). Genomic analyses reveal a shared Iron Age ancestry component (BRIT) between Icelandic horses and native British breeds such as Dartmoor and Exmoor ponies, with clustering analyses placing the Icelandic horse in a shared pony clade with these breeds (Ahmed et al., under review; Petersen et al., 2013). Some Norse settlers reached Iceland from communities in the British Isles rather than directly from Scandinavia, as shown by the Gaelic ancestry detected among early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science), and the horses they brought likely combined Norwegian and British pony ancestry. The import of horses was prohibited in Iceland under the medieval law code Grágás (Karlsson et al., 1992), a restriction often traced to the early Commonwealth period from 930 CE, though the precise date is uncertain (Stelter, 2014); this ban has kept the gene pool closed ever since. What none of the founding studies can establish is the exact proportional contribution of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock, or whether horses of different origins arrived with different settler groups.

On diversity and population history, modern genomic and pedigree studies provide a detailed picture of the present-day breed, though largely limited to the past two centuries. Genome-wide SNP comparisons show Icelandic horses to be among the most genetically distinct of all horse breeds (Petersen et al., 2013). Notably, Icelandic horses cluster with Nordic breeds and occupy a position close to where most ancient horse samples are found in global genomic space, suggesting the breed may have retained more ancestral diversity than some of the most intensively managed modern breeds, rather than simply being low in diversity relative to all horses (Ahmed et al., under review). Sigurðardóttir et al. (2024) genotyped 380 Icelandic horses on the 670K array and put current Ne at 125 individuals, with genomic inbreeding (F_ROH) ranging 0.08–0.20. Effective population-size reconstruction across 60 generations reveals two clear declines: one approximately 23 generations ago, coinciding with the Laki eruption of 1783, which reduced Iceland's horse population by roughly 75%, and another approximately 8 generations ago, associated with early 20th-century mechanisation. Runs of homozygosity (ROH) islands are strongest over DMRT3, with additional signals near FOXO1 on ECA17 and HMGA2 on ECA6. The FOXO1 region is also elevated in Exmoor ponies (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024); that two isolated breeds share the same elevated region points toward selection for metabolic thriftiness rather than drift. Signals over HMGA2 recur across association studies of equine metabolic syndrome (EMS) in native ponies (Norton et al., 2019). Up to half of Icelandic horses develop IBH on first exposure to *Culicoides* midges after export to mainland Europe. The midges are absent from Iceland, so susceptibility remains unexpressed there but affects up to half of exported horses on first exposure (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). Associated loci have been identified on multiple chromosomes including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012), and 79 MHC haplotypes are exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019). The 670K array was developed from whole-genome sequences of 153 individuals representing 24 horse breeds, mainly North American and European performance lines (Schaefer et al., 2017); the Icelandic horse was not among the design breeds, and the array may therefore not optimally tag Icelandic-specific variation. These chip data have not yet been analysed in the context of global horse variation or ancient horse genomes; this is a gap the project will close directly. Pedigree analysis puts Ne at approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, with an effective number of founders of 151 and an effective number of ancestors of only 42, meaning a handful of animals have disproportionately shaped the gene pool (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). Y-chromosomal analysis of Icelandic stallions, based on direct genotyping of 72 individuals and extrapolation using genealogical records, found only Icelandic-specific haplotypes with no sign of recent outside introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Together, these studies show a breed that still carries the imprint of its founding and of subsequent demographic crises — but the temporal structure of that history, and everything that happened between 870 CE and the mid-19th century pedigree record, remains largely unknown.

Previous attempts to study ancient Icelandic horse DNA have confirmed the quality of the material and the limits of what single-locus or very low-depth approaches can achieve. Wutke et al. (2016) genotyped the DMRT3 variant across 90 ancient European specimens; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age specimens, confirming good preservation (mean 9.3% endogenous DNA); Fages et al. (2019) included two Icelandic genomes in a global survey — the only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses to date. None provides data at the depth or sample size required for population-level inference: there are no imputed genomes, no admixture estimates, no temporal tracking of allele frequencies, and no reconstruction of effective population size before the 19th century.

The central gap is the absence of a chronological genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse. Without one, the proportional contributions of founding populations cannot be quantified, the timing of current inbreeding patterns cannot be determined, and documented historical events cannot be connected to genomic change. Two developments now make this gap closeable. GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023, Nature Genetics) has lowered the sequencing threshold for population genomic inference: ancient genomes at 1–3x coverage now support full analyses at a fraction of the previous cost. The reference data are also now in place: the haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes developed by Orlando's laboratory, together with 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel — including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles spanning the Viking Age — provides both the reference needed for imputation and the temporal comparators needed for Aim 1. With genomic data from many ancient and modern Icelandic horses and from relevant breeds and ancient samples across Europe, it will for the first time be possible to quantify founding admixture proportions, track allele frequencies through 1,100 years, and explore population structure using PCA, IBD sharing, wIBS, and admixture graph modelling. Five quality-assessed pilot libraries yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA confirm the material is ready and the project can begin immediately. Both the Nordlandshest/Lyngen and a broad range of Atlantic-fringe and Scandinavian breeds — including Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh, Connemara, Kerry Bog Pony, Fjord, Eriskay, Fell, and Highland ponies — are represented in the reference panel, making it possible to test founding contributions genomically with a resolution unavailable to earlier microsatellite and single-locus studies.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; sequencing at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse as fallback; computing on the deCODE cluster or the University of Iceland cluster if needed. The main risk is that some specimens will yield too little endogenous DNA, but the large initial sample pool and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries reduce this considerably. The project is designed to generate at least 100 high-quality imputed genomes at approximately 0.1x coverage, sufficient for population genomic inference using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023).

Phase 1 begins with specimen selection, osteological assessment, and sampling under the coordination of Dr Joe Walser III. Standardised long-bone measurements are taken from all specimens to build the osteometric body size record for Aim 3. Silica-based DNA extraction proceeds in the deCODE ancient DNA clean-room (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007); second or third molars from the same specimens are retained for strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) at the University of Durham, which is performing the strontium work for the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study. Libraries are screened by shallow shotgun sequencing to confirm characteristic ancient damage patterns and estimate endogenous content, using AdapterRemoval2, BWA, and mapDamage2 (Schubert et al., 2016; Li & Durbin, 2009; Jónsson et al., 2013). Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA proceed to deep sequencing at 2–5x target depth.

Phase 2 imputes ancient genomes using GLIMPSE2 with Prof. Orlando's reference panel. Aim 1 uses wIBS, PCA, ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) to test competing models of the founding event. Aim 2 tracks temporal change in the gene pool through identity-by-descent analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) and inbreeding coefficients derived from runs of homozygosity. Effective population size is reconstructed using PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++ (Terhorst et al., 2017) and interpreted against Iceland's documented livestock crises.

Phase 3 applies genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, iHS, PBS) and tracks allele frequencies through time at key loci: gait (DMRT3, STAU2, RELN); coat colour across the full palette (MC1R, ASIP, KIT, MATP, TYRP1, TRPM1, TBX3); the PMEL17 vision locus; body size and EMS (HMGA2, FOXO1); sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012); and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019). Osteometric withers-height estimates from Phase 1 are compared with HMGA2 allele-frequency trajectories as an independent phenotypic check on the genomic body size signal.

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a real research project for one Master's student, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology at the University of Iceland or the Agricultural University of Iceland (Landbúnaðarháskóli Íslands). Six project options are available, spanning analytical genomics, osteology, and archaeology, so that the student can be matched to a supervisor and background that fits.

**Option 1** (biology or biological anthropology): the history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation. As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2** (archaeology or biological anthropology): paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík, one of the largest Viking Age burial sites in Iceland, where six of the thirteen humans have been sequenced at sufficient depth for population-level analysis as part of the ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), and eight horse burials are associated. The student will examine genetic relationships among the horses and their connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. A student with a background in zooarchaeology could also help identify horse remains within the broader osteological collection at the National Museum, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as animal bones — screening work that would directly expand the pool of candidate specimens. JWW will co-supervise.

**Option 3** (archaeology, biology, or biological anthropology): osteometric body size through time. JWW will take standardised long-bone measurements from all ~150 archaeological specimens as part of Phase 1; a student with an osteological or zooarchaeological background would take primary responsibility for this dataset. The project involves estimating withers heights, compiling the first systematic osteometric record for Icelandic horses across 870–1900 CE, and testing whether the compact conformation of the breed has been stable across that span or shows change associated with documented historical events. The osteometric record will be paired with allele-frequency data from the HMGA2 size locus from Aim 3, providing an independent phenotypic check on the genomic signal. The project requires no bioinformatics and is well suited to an archaeology or biology student. JWW will co-supervise.

**Option 4** (biology or biological anthropology): sex-biased founding — X-chromosome versus autosomal diversity. As described in Aim 2, the X chromosome is transmitted more often through females than males and amplifies the signal left by unequal numbers of founding stallions and mares. This is a fully analytical project using the Phase 2 imputed ancient genomes: the student will compare X-chromosomal and autosomal diversity and inbreeding trajectories through the 870–1900 CE time series, testing whether the founding event was sex-biased and whether that imbalance changed over time. The question of how many mares and stallions actually crossed the ocean, and how their relative contributions to the gene pool have shifted over the centuries, has a clear methodology and a publishable result.

**Option 5** (biology): the temporal history of sweet itch (IBH) susceptibility alleles. IBH affects up to 50% of Icelandic horses exported to mainland Europe, yet the breed never encounters the trigger (*Culicoides* midges) at home; genetic susceptibility is widespread but entirely hidden in Iceland. Known IBH-associated loci — in the MHC region on ECA20 (Schurink et al., 2012), on the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015), and at the 79 MHC haplotypes exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019) — will be tracked through the Phase 2 temporal dataset. The student will determine when these haplotypes became breed-exclusive and whether their frequency changed across the 1,100-year span, providing the first historical context for a trait of direct concern to breeders and breed organisations. The project is entirely analytical.

**Option 6** (biology or biological anthropology): the coat colours of the settlement horses. The Icelandic horse carries one of the widest colour palettes of any breed — more than 40 recognised colours with over 100 Icelandic-language names — yet what the founding horses actually looked like is entirely unknown. Wild horses before domestication showed virtually no colour variation; the diversity seen in the breed today emerged through human selection, but whether it was already present at the settlement or accumulated over subsequent centuries has never been tested. Using the ancient genomes from the earliest time points in the dataset (870–1100 CE), the student will reconstruct the probable coat colours of individual settlement-era horses by imputing known colour-associated variants at MC1R and ASIP (base coat), KIT (pinto and sabino patterns), MATP (cream dilute), TYRP1, TRPM1 (leopard complex), and TBX3 (dun), and compare the founding colour palette with the distribution seen in later historical periods and in the present-day breed. The result will be the first genomically based reconstruction of what Iceland's earliest horses looked like — a question of broad cultural as well as scientific interest. The project is entirely analytical, using the Phase 2 imputed genomes, and requires no additional laboratory work.

The student will be co-supervised by Prof. Agnar Helgason and Dr Ebenesersdóttir (Options 1, 2, 4, 5, 6), or by Prof. Agnar Helgason and Dr Ebenesersdóttir, with the addition of Dr Joe Walser III (Option 3). *[FLAG: consider whether JWW should also co-supervise Option 2, since it involves the National Museum osteological collection — see Section D flag.]* A student based at the Agricultural University of Iceland could be co-supervised by Prof. Sigríður Björnsdóttir or Prof. Jón Hallsteinn Hallsson (Professor of Genetics, Agricultural University of Iceland), both of whom have direct expertise in Icelandic horse genetics and breeding; this would be particularly natural for Options 3, 5, and 6. All six projects are designed to result in a publishable manuscript within a 12-month research period. Six months of salary support is included in the budget.
