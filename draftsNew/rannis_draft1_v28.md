# [OPEN ITEM 1 — DECISION: confirm title] Brought/Bred by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation

## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse has been isolated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Norse settlers brought horses to Iceland in the late 9th century CE (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018), and import has been banned since at least the medieval Commonwealth period, a prohibition that remains in force under current Icelandic law (Act No. 54/1990). WorldFengur, the breed's international studbook (Lorange, 2011), documents parentage for more than 354,000 registered horses with records back to individuals born around 1860. Before that, the genomic history of the breed is largely unknown: no ancient horse genomes from Iceland exist at the depth needed for population-level inference. This project will generate the first such dataset: approximately 150 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, with whole-genome sequences from 100–150 present-day horses as a present-day reference. Three scientific aims structure the work.

**Aim 1: Origins and founding.** What was the ancestry profile of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Did the founding population reflect a single source region or a mixture of distinct breeds from different regions in Scandinavia and the British Isles?

The proportional contributions of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock cannot be resolved from present-day data alone. This project will determine those proportions for the first time, using genome-wide ancient DNA from approximately 150 Icelandic specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, modelled against modern horses from more than 100 breeds and ancient specimens from Britain and Scandinavia spanning the Viking Age (Ahmed et al., under review; Figure 1).

Strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) will add an individual-level dimension that genomics alone cannot provide. Strontium in tooth enamel records the geology of the region where an animal was raised; Iceland's young volcanic bedrock produces a distinctively low isotope ratio, clearly separable from the older continental geology of Norway and the British Isles. Sampling second or third molars from the same specimens processed for ancient DNA — no additional sampling required — will identify horses that were not born in Iceland: animals carried across the ocean rather than locally bred descendants of imported stock. Paired with the genomic ancestry estimates, this distinguishes two founding scenarios that genomics alone cannot resolve: a single crossing in which horses of mixed Norwegian and British origin arrived together in the settlement generation, and a multi-wave model in which horses of different origins arrived with different settler groups at different times. Where genomics reveals what population a horse descended from, strontium reveals where that individual animal spent its early life. Strontium isotope analysis is already an established method in the applicant's group: the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study (202 genomes) includes strontium measurements for many individuals from the same archaeological sites targeted here, with analysis carried out at the University of Durham. The Icelandic strontium baseline being built through that study is directly applicable to the horse specimens, and the 19 sites shared between the two datasets will yield paired human–horse strontium data — a combination that has no parallel elsewhere. Dr Joe Walser III (National Museum of Iceland) coordinates osteological assessment and strontium sampling; isotope analysis will be performed at the University of Durham, which is carrying out the strontium work for the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study.

**Aim 2: Demographic history under isolation.** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Whole-genome sequences will be generated for 100–150 present-day Icelandic horses in collaboration with Prof. Björnsdóttir: a subset at high coverage (15–30x) to expand the imputation reference panel with Icelandic-specific haplotypes, the remainder at low coverage (1–3x) and imputed using that panel, providing a present-day anchor for demographic modelling at the same genomic resolution as the ancient dataset. The temporal series of ancient genomes will then allow reconstruction of effective population size, tracking of inbreeding through time, and testing whether documented crises left detectable genomic signatures.

Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes; no outside stallion has contributed to the breed in the modern record (Weinzerl, 2024). How far back that exclusivity goes is what the ancient male specimens will reveal. The X chromosome carries a different kind of signal: transmitted more often through females than males, its effective population size is three-quarters that of the autosomes, and it amplifies the signal left by unequal use of stallions and mares. Bottlenecks driven by uneven use of stallions can appear in X-chromosomal data when the autosomal signal looks unremarkable.

WorldFengur's genealogical depth creates a further opportunity: an ancestral recombination graph (ARG) for the breed. An ARG is a map of the genealogical pathways each variant took on its way to living horses; when that map is tied to WorldFengur's pedigree records, questions like when DMRT3 reached fixation or which founder line first carried the PMEL17 Silver allele become answerable at nucleotide resolution. Combined with the present-day whole-genome sequences and ancient imputed genomes from this project, this would be the first ARG for any domestic animal anchored to a verified pedigree database. The Icelandic horse is the natural candidate: a small population, a closed gene pool, a complete studbook, and now ancient DNA spanning the full breeding history.

**Aim 3: Adaptation and trait evolution.** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits emerge and change over more than a millennium? Are there genomic regions showing signatures of selection that point to unexpected targets not previously identified in this breed?

Iceland has the dated external record to anchor the genomic signal: volcanic chronology, livestock censuses, and written sources going back to the settlement. Six trait systems will be tracked:

*Gait.* DMRT3 on ECA23 controls the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al., 2012). The allele was already present in 9th–11th century Icelandic specimens (Wutke et al., 2016), but when fixation occurred is unknown. STAU2 and RELN (Sigurðardóttir, 2025) will be tracked alongside it.

*Coat colour — full palette.* The Icelandic horse carries more than 40 recognized colours with over 100 Icelandic-language names for colour variants — an exceptionally wide palette by any breed standard. Unlike most breeds where only a narrow range of colours is permitted, the official Icelandic breeding goal explicitly preserves all naturally occurring colours; variety is actively encouraged. This breadth is itself a scientific question: was it present from the founding, or did it accumulate under deliberate Icelandic breeding selection across the post-settlement centuries? Wild horses before domestication showed virtually no colour variation, carrying predominantly bay or bay-dun coats; coat colour diversity increased sharply during domestication, driven by human selection (Ludwig et al., 2009, Science). Colour-associated loci across the genome — MC1R and ASIP (base coat), KIT (pinto and sabino patterns), MATP (cream dilute), TYRP1, TRPM1 (leopard complex), TBX3 (dun) — will be tracked through the temporal dataset to reconstruct the founding colour palette and identify when specific variants entered or rose in frequency. This is the first time the colour history of the Icelandic horse will have been addressed with genomic data.

*Coat colour and vision health — PMEL17.* PMEL17 Arg618Cys causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). When this allele entered the breed is unknown; tracking its frequency from 870 CE will show whether MCOA was a founding-era liability, with direct consequences for breeding management.

*Body size.* The Icelandic horse is compact — 125–145 cm at the withers — and has remained so since at least the Viking Age based on osteological records. HMGA2 on ECA6 is the primary genetic locus for body size variation in ponies and small horse breeds (Norton et al., 2019), in addition to its role in EMS. Whether the compact size of the Icelandic horse reflects the founding stock directly, or whether it was reinforced by nutritional selection during Iceland's recurrent famines, can be tested by tracking HMGA2 allele frequencies through time alongside osteological withers-height estimates from the archaeological specimens. Dr Joe Walser III will take standardised long-bone measurements from all sampled specimens, providing an independent phenotypic record alongside the genomic data.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (IBH) affects up to 50% of Icelandic horses on first exposure to *Culicoides* midges after export to mainland Europe — primarily temperate northern European countries such as the Netherlands, Germany, Sweden, and the UK, where the breed is most commonly kept (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). *Culicoides* midges are absent from Iceland, so horses there never encounter the trigger; the genetic susceptibility is widespread but the phenotype is entirely hidden. The temporal dataset will provide the first historical context for when these alleles entered the breed and whether the 79 MHC haplotypes exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019) reflect founding diversity or later balancing selection.

*Metabolic disease susceptibility.* EMS is well documented in Icelandic horses: insulin dysregulation, obesity, laminitis on rich pasture. FOXO1 on ECA17 and HMGA2 on ECA6 are the primary genetic candidates (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024; Norton et al., 2019). The hypothesis is that selection for metabolic thriftiness under Iceland's repeated famines produced allele frequency changes that now predispose the breed to EMS on modern pasture; correlating these changes with documented famine periods provides a direct test.

Genome-wide selection scans across the full temporal dataset will also search for signatures of positive and purifying selection at loci not yet studied in the Icelandic horse.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* aDNA extraction and library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well established and have been applied to 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science) and more than 200 (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation). Horse skeletal material from Icelandic archaeological contexts preserves aDNA well: Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) recovered a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA across 22 Viking Age specimens, and five pilot specimens processed by the applicant yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA at a mean depth of 3.6x. Strontium isotope analysis is likewise established within the group: the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study includes strontium measurements for many individuals from the same sites, performed at the University of Durham, and the Icelandic baseline is already in hand. There is no technical barrier to applying either workflow to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted at the National Museum of Iceland for approximately 150 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE. These come from burial mounds (kuml), animal bone deposits (dys), and open-site assemblages distributed across the country (Figure 2).

*Infrastructure.* DNA extraction and library preparation will take place at deCODE Genetics, Reykjavík, in a dedicated ancient DNA clean-room established for the applicant's group and used for no other purpose. Access is covered by an existing collaboration agreement. Sequencing will be performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); Prof. Orlando has confirmed additional sequencing capacity at CAGT Toulouse. Bioinformatic analyses will run on the deCODE high-performance computing cluster, with the University of Iceland cluster available if needed. All facilities are in place.

*International collaboration.* The main international collaborator is Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse), whose laboratory provides the horse imputation reference panel and whose expertise in ancient horse genomics is central to Aims 1 and 2. The working relationship already exists through co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review), so the reference panel, analytical pipeline, and collaboration are in place from day one. Prof. Orlando hosts one research visit per year at CAGT Toulouse and can provide sequencing as a fallback if needed. A letter of support is included.

**Originality.** Studies of the Icelandic horse have so far relied on mitochondrial DNA and microsatellites (Campana et al., 2012), present-day SNP array genotypes from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), pedigree records (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014), and two forms of single-locus ancient genotyping: sex chromosome read ratios (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al., 2019) and the DMRT3 variant (Wutke et al., 2016). None reaches the coverage needed for population-level inference. The only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses produced 22 low-depth genomes, of which only two entered a global population survey and neither was imputed (Fages et al., 2019). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse: the first capable of population structure inference, demographic reconstruction, and selection analysis across the full post-settlement history of the breed. The originality has four dimensions.

No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any North Atlantic horse population: not from Iceland, the Faroe Islands, or Greenland. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record has almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics. The genomic time series can be checked against specific, dated historical events, a resolution rarely achievable in the ancient genomics literature. The ancient genomes, WorldFengur pedigree records, SNP array data from 380 horses (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024), and 100–150 new present-day whole-genome sequences together span nearly the full history of the breed from 870 CE to the present. No other domestic animal comes close: we have the archaeology, the genealogy, the genomics, and a verified import ban, all for the same population.

Most long-term isolation studies face the same practical problem: records thin out, founding events are murky, and timelines are uncertain. Here, the founding is documented to within a generation, the import ban is verified, and the full historical record survives. Inbreeding dynamics, deleterious variant accumulation, and trait evolution in a completely closed breeding system can all be tracked against dated events, a resolution unavailable in most ancient genomics datasets.

Island populations generally show reduced diversity relative to their mainland sources (Frankham, 1997); the Icelandic horse is an acute case of this pattern. The most direct parallel is the human population that crossed the same ocean: Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science) showed how hard drift has worked on the Icelandic human gene pool across the same 1,100-year span.

The applicant's ongoing ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, 19 of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (kuml) documented in Kuml og haugfé (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur. Paired human–horse genomic data from the same Viking Age burial contexts, at this combination of temporal depth and sample size, exists nowhere else.

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including sheep, cattle, and dogs, and the work creates a template for future projects on those species.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse was at the heart of settlement-era society: a working mount, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and stallion contests, a companion in death (Eldjárn & Friðriksson, 2016; Leifsson, 2018). In Norse tradition horses carried deep mythological weight, and Icelandic burial archaeology reflects this: they were interred alongside their owners as companions rather than property. Where the founding horses came from is therefore also a question about the settlers themselves: their origins, their routes, and the livestock populations they had access to before crossing the North Atlantic.

The Icelandic horse industry is substantial, with more than 354,000 registered horses across 31 countries as of 2010 (Lorange, 2011), and the genetic health of the breed is a practical concern. Sweet itch affects up to 50% of exported horses (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019) and ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al., 2006; Andersson et al., 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will clarify when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current genetic uniformity reflects the original founding or later management decisions. FEIF (the International Federation of Icelandic Horse Associations, founded 1969) and Landssamband hestamannafélaga (founded 1949) jointly maintain WorldFengur, which integrates DNA parentage verification and health records for horses across 31 countries. Working with both puts these findings directly in front of breeders and creates a natural basis for follow-on funding in disease genomics and diversity management.

For native breed conservation, the findings carry a relevance beyond Iceland. Ahmed et al. (under review) identified a BRIT ancestry component concentrated in critically endangered British native breeds such as the Dartmoor and Exmoor pony. British native pony breeds have undergone severe genomic contraction since the 19th century; Dartmoor and Exmoor census populations are now among the lowest in the UK. The Icelandic horse, isolated since the 9th century, is a candidate repository for British-origin genetic diversity that no longer survives on the mainland. Both breeds are on the critical list of the Rare Breeds Survival Trust; Exmoor ponies are now widely used in rewilding and conservation grazing projects across northern Europe. Identifying which British-origin variants were carried to Iceland, and whether any are absent or rare in modern British native breeds, is a direct contribution to the conservation genomics of these endangered populations.

The long-term integrity of the Icelandic gene pool is not guaranteed. Ongoing discussions about Iceland's relationship with the European Union raise the question of whether the current importation ban could come under pressure from EU animal movement regulations. The genomic baseline established here would provide an irreplaceable record of the breed's genetic heritage before any such change, and a scientific foundation for evidence-based policy on managing the breed's diversity in the future.

Evolutionary biology and conservation science both grapple with what happens to populations that are small, closed, and isolated over long timescales. Icelandic horses are an unusually clean empirical case: the patterns this project reveals in inbreeding accumulation, founding admixture, and adaptive sweeps will be interpretable by researchers working on any insular or managed population. The GLIMPSE2 imputation pipeline is also directly transferable: it works for any species with a sufficient reference panel, and the analytical workflow built here demonstrates what population genomic inference looks like from fragmentary ancient material.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

With approximately 60 million horses distributed worldwide and more than 500 recognised breeds (Petersen et al., 2013; FAO FAOSTAT, 2024), the horse is among the most globally significant domestic animals, yet modern horse populations are dominated by a handful of intensively bred bloodlines. Thoroughbred, Arabian, and American Quarter Horse genetics are increasingly well characterised, and these breeds carry strong directional selection signatures from centuries of performance-focused breeding (Fages et al., 2019). The Icelandic horse became isolated before many of these dominant modern breeds took their current form, and may retain genetic diversity that has since been lost or reduced on the mainland. Few domestic animal populations combine its age with verified isolation, a well-documented historical record, and material amenable to population genomic inference.

Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, Science) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia, long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses, are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. The lower Volga-Don region (present-day southern Russia and Ukraine) has since been established as the origin of modern domestic horse ancestry, with a rapid expansion across Eurasia from around 2000 BCE (Librado et al., 2021, Nature). A study spanning five millennia of horse genomes revealed two now-extinct lineages and a marked drop in diversity at the point when modern breeds were formed during the 17th to 19th centuries (Fages et al., 2019, Cell). Much has also been gained from analyses of modern horses alone and through comparisons of ancient and modern genomes: Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and led by Prof. Orlando and Prof. Agnar Helgason, characterise seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from 901 modern horse genomes and 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that reference panel. They establish that Icelandic horses are characterised by the NORD ancestry component that emerged at approximately 810 CE — close to the time horses and humans first arrived in Iceland. Yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

The question of where the founding Icelandic horses came from has a long research history, and a coherent picture is emerging — though critical gaps remain. On origins, Norway has been the primary candidate from the start. Morphological comparisons pointed early to the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage (Eldjárn, 1991), and subsequent field comparisons confirmed that five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform gaits related to the tölt (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998) — though tölt performance in Nordland/Lyngen horses does not resolve whether the DMRT3 allele controlling it was shared through a common ancestral population or arrived independently. Microsatellite analysis later placed Icelandic and Nordland/Lyngen horses in the same phylogenetic cluster with strong bootstrap support (Bjørnstad et al., 2003), consistent with the historical record of Viking Age introduction from Norway. A British Isles contribution has also attracted sustained attention, supported initially by shared morphological features with Atlantic-fringe breeds — the so-called Celtic pony hypothesis (Aðalsteinsson & Sigurðsson, 1998) — and now by several converging lines of genomic evidence. The DMRT3 ambling allele was present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but largely absent from contemporary Scandinavian horses in the dataset, yet found in two horses from 850–900 CE at Jorvik (York), pointing to its arrival in Iceland via settlers coming from or through Britain (Wutke et al., 2016). The only two ancient Icelandic genomes with sufficient depth for global placement cluster closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses from Britain and Scandinavia, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse (Fages et al., 2019). Genomic analysis further identifies a shared Iron Age ancestry component (BRIT) between Icelandic horses and native British breeds including Dartmoor and Exmoor ponies, with SNP-based clustering placing the Icelandic horse in a shared pony clade with these breeds (Ahmed et al., under review; Petersen et al., 2013). Some Norse settlers came not directly from Scandinavia but from established communities in the British Isles, as indicated by the presence of Gaelic ancestry among early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al., 2018, Science), and horses carried on those voyages may have reflected mixed Norwegian and British pony ancestry. The import of horses was prohibited in Iceland under the medieval law code Grágás (Karlsson et al., 1992), a restriction often traced to the early Commonwealth period from 930 CE, though the precise date is uncertain (Stelter, 2014); this ban has kept the gene pool closed ever since. What none of the founding studies can establish is the exact proportional contribution of Scandinavian versus British Isles source populations to the founding stock, or whether horses of different origins arrived with different settler groups.

On diversity and population history, modern genomic and pedigree studies provide a detailed picture of the present-day breed, though largely limited to the past two centuries. Genome-wide SNP comparisons show Icelandic horses to be among the most genetically distinct of all horse breeds (Petersen et al., 2013). Notably, however, Icelandic horses cluster with Nordic breeds and occupy a position close to where most ancient horse samples are found in global genomic space, suggesting the breed may have retained more ancestral diversity than some of the most intensively managed modern breeds, rather than simply being low in diversity relative to all horses (Ahmed et al., under review). Sigurðardóttir et al. (2024) genotyped 380 Icelandic horses on the 670K array and put current Ne at 125 individuals, with genomic inbreeding (F_ROH) ranging 0.08–0.20. Ne reconstruction across 60 generations shows two pronounced drops: one approximately 23 generations ago, corresponding to the Laki eruption of 1783 which reduced Iceland's horse population by roughly 75%, and one approximately 8 generations ago, corresponding to early 20th-century mechanisation. ROH islands were strongest over DMRT3, with prominent additional signals over FOXO1 on ECA17 and HMGA2 on ECA6. The FOXO1 island is shared with Exmoor ponies (Sigurðardóttir et al., 2024); two isolated breeds sharing the same elevated region points toward selection for metabolic thriftiness rather than drift. HMGA2 on ECA6 recurs across EMS association studies in native ponies (Norton et al., 2019). Sweet itch (IBH) is caused by an IgE-mediated allergic reaction to Culicoides midge bites; midges are absent from Iceland, so susceptibility is phenotypically hidden in Iceland but affects up to 50% of horses on first exposure after export (Eriksson et al., 2008; Holmes et al., 2019). Associated loci have been identified on multiple chromosomes including the X chromosome (Shrestha et al., 2015) and ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al., 2012), and 79 MHC haplotypes are exclusive to Icelandic horses (Holmes et al., 2019). The 670K array was designed from whole-genome sequences of 153 individuals representing 24 horse breeds, predominantly North American and European performance breeds (Schaefer et al., 2017); the Icelandic horse was not among the design breeds, meaning the chip may not optimally tag Icelandic-specific variation. These chip data have not yet been analysed in the context of global horse variation or ancient horse genomes; this is a gap the project will close directly. Pedigree analysis puts Ne at approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, with an effective number of founders of 151 and an effective number of ancestors of only 42 — a handful of animals have disproportionately shaped the gene pool (Hreiðarsdóttir et al., 2014). Y-chromosomal analysis of Icelandic stallions, based on direct genotyping of 72 individuals and extrapolation using genealogical records, found only Icelandic-specific haplotypes with no sign of recent outside introductions (Weinzerl, 2024). Together, these studies show a breed that still carries the imprint of its founding and of subsequent demographic crises — but the temporal structure of that history, and everything that happened between 870 CE and the mid-19th century pedigree record, remains largely unknown.

Previous attempts to study ancient Icelandic horse DNA have confirmed the potential of the material and the limits of what single-locus or very low-depth approaches can achieve. Wutke et al. (2016) genotyped the DMRT3 variant across 90 ancient European specimens; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age specimens, confirming good preservation (mean 9.3% endogenous DNA); and Fages et al. (2019) included two Icelandic genomes in a global survey — the only ancient shotgun sequencing of Icelandic horses to date. None of these studies produces the depth or sample size needed for population-level inference: no imputed genomes, no admixture quantification, no temporal tracking of allele frequencies, no estimate of effective population size before the 19th century.

The central gap is the complete absence of a temporal genomic dataset from the Icelandic horse. Without it, we cannot quantify the proportional contributions of founding populations, determine when current patterns of inbreeding emerged, or test the relationship between documented historical events and genomic change. Two developments now make this gap closeable. GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023, Nature Genetics) removes the main sequencing cost barrier: ancient genomes at 1–3x coverage support full population genomic inference, an order of magnitude cheaper than traditional deep sequencing. The reference data are also now in place: Prof. Orlando's haplotype reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, together with 376 ancient horse genomes imputed using that panel — including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles spanning the Viking Age — provides both the reference needed for imputation and the temporal comparators needed for Aim 1. With genomic data from many ancient and modern Icelandic horses and from relevant breeds and ancient samples across Europe, it will for the first time be possible to quantify founding admixture proportions, track allele frequencies through 1,100 years, and explore population structure using PCA, IBD sharing, wIBS, and admixture graph modelling. Five quality-assessed pilot libraries yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA confirm the material is ready and the project can begin immediately. Both the Nordlandshest/Lyngen and a broad range of Atlantic-fringe and Scandinavian breeds — including Exmoor, Dartmoor, Welsh, Connemara, Kerry Bog Pony, Fjord, Eriskay, Fell, and Highland ponies — are represented in the reference panel, making it possible to test founding contributions genomically with a resolution unavailable to earlier microsatellite and single-locus studies.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT) AND DELIVERABLES

All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; sequencing at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq), with the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse as fallback; computing on the deCODE cluster or the University of Iceland cluster if needed. The main risk is that some specimens will yield too little endogenous DNA, but the large initial sample pool and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries reduce this considerably. The project is designed to generate at least 100 high-quality imputed genomes at approximately 0.1x coverage, sufficient for population genomic inference using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al., 2023).

Phase 1 begins with specimen selection, osteological assessment, and sampling under the coordination of Dr Joe Walser III. Silica-based DNA extraction proceeds in the deCODE ancient DNA clean-room (Rohland & Hofreiter, 2007); second or third molars from the same specimens are retained for strontium isotope analysis (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) at the University of Durham, which is performing the strontium work for the in-preparation ancient Icelandic human study. Libraries are screened by shallow shotgun sequencing using a standard aDNA pipeline (AdapterRemoval2, BWA, mapDamage2; Schubert et al., 2016; Li & Durbin, 2009; Jónsson et al., 2013), confirming ancient damage profiles and endogenous content. Libraries with at least 1% endogenous horse DNA proceed to deep sequencing at 2–5x target depth.

Phase 2 imputes ancient genomes using GLIMPSE2 with Prof. Orlando's reference panel. Aim 1 uses wIBS, PCA, ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al., 2009), and admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al., 2012) to test competing models of the founding event. Aim 2 tracks changes in the gene pool through identity-by-descent analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al., 2020) and ROH-based inbreeding coefficients, with effective population size reconstructed using PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++ (Terhorst et al., 2017) and interpreted against Iceland's documented livestock crises.

Phase 3 runs genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, iHS, PBS) and tracks allele frequencies through time at gait loci (DMRT3, STAU2, RELN), coat colour and vision loci (PMEL17, ASIP, MC1R), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al., 2015; Schurink et al., 2012), and the MHC region (Holmes et al., 2019).

## F. CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a real research project for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology — a field where no established training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland. Two project options are available.

**Option 1:** The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation (biology or biological anthropology). As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical, requiring no additional laboratory work, and is fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2** (archaeology or biological anthropology): paired human and horse genomics at Brimnes, Dalvík, one of the largest Viking Age burial sites in Iceland, where six of the thirteen humans have been sequenced at sufficient depth for population-level analysis as part of the ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation), and eight horse burials are associated. The student will examine genetic relationships among the horses and their connections to horses from other Icelandic sites. A student with a background in zooarchaeology could also help identify horse remains within the broader osteological collection at the Museum, where a substantial number of boxes are currently catalogued only as animal bones — screening work that would directly expand the pool of candidate specimens.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir and Prof. Agnar Helgason. Both projects are designed to result in a publishable manuscript within a 12-month research period. Six months of salary support is included in the budget.
