## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations in the world and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE (an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record), it has remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted what is one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of horses to Iceland [Claude comment: Stelter 2014 has been our citation for this claim — Sunna flags that a primary legal history source such as Grágás may be stronger; please confirm the best citation before submission]. Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. More than 1,100 years of unbroken isolation have produced a domestic animal of extraordinary scientific value: one whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown at the genomic level. Twenty-two Viking Age specimens have been sequenced, but all at very low depth of coverage; only two reached sufficient depth to be placed on a global phylogeny, and even then only tentatively (Fages et al. 2019; see Section B for detail). We do not know which populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in a single founding event or through multiple waves, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most celebrated traits — its unique gaits and exceptional hardiness — as well as its characteristic disease susceptibilities, evolved under more than a millennium of isolation. This project will potentially generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and will address three specific scientific aims.

---

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding:** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions?

The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock across the North Atlantic. Medieval sources suggest that Viking Age settlers in Britain and Ireland acquired and bred local horses before departing for Iceland [Claude comment: this claim needs a citation to a documentary or historical source — Stelter 2014 discusses horse transport in the Norse expansion and is probably the right reference here; Wutke et al. 2016 is a genetics paper and should not be cited for this historical point]. The founding Icelandic horse population was therefore likely a mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry, shaped by wherever the colonists had lived before crossing the Atlantic.

The ancient human genomics of Iceland supports this picture directly. The applicant's study of early Icelanders (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018, *Science*; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows they were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry in proportions of approximately 0.57 Norse : 0.43 Gaelic during the settlement period [Claude comment: please confirm the exact ratio from the in-preparation manuscript and whether it is stable enough to state here — if not yet fully analysed, cite only Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018 *Science*], with the Norse component disproportionately drawn from southern and western Norway, and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and Scotland. Many settlers did not depart directly from Norway but from Norse communities in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where Scandinavian and Celtic-speaking populations had lived alongside each other for a generation or more. If the people who settled Iceland were mixed Norse-Gaelic, the horses they brought very plausibly were too: a settler from the Hebrides would have brought horses carrying a mixture of Scandinavian and native British or Celtic pony ancestry.

The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds — small stature, extreme hardiness, dense coat — has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis" [Sunna: a citation is needed for this hypothesis — please confirm whether Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson 1998 or another source uses this term or concept explicitly]. Molecular evidence supports it. Genotyping of the DMRT3 ambling allele (the gait-keeper mutation responsible for the tölt) in 90 ancient horses from across Europe, including 13 archaeological specimens from Icelandic sites (Wutke et al. 2016), found the allele in 10 of those 13 early Icelandic individuals (77%), yet absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses; it appears instead in two English horses from 850–900 CE (Jorvik), placing its likely origin in Viking Age Britain and indicating it arrived in Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Phylogenetic analysis places the two ancient Icelandic horse genomes closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain and Viking Age British specimens (Fages et al. 2019). Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock, or in what proportions. This project will answer that question for the first time, using genome-wide data and a reference panel that includes ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Viking Age itself.

---

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation:** How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Modern genomic analysis of the contemporary Icelandic horse population has already detected two effective population size crashes in the genomes of living horses: one approximately 23 generations ago, consistent with the Laki/Skaftáreldar volcanic famine of 1783–1784 CE (which historical accounts estimate reduced the horse population by approximately 75%), and one during early 20th-century mechanisation. The current genomic effective population size is 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024). Pedigree analysis provides complementary estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that not all founders contributed equally to subsequent generations (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014).

But the genomes of modern horses cannot reveal what happened before the 18th century. The founding bottleneck, the medieval centuries, the catastrophic livestock mortality of the Black Death (~1402 CE), and the Sturlung Age civil conflicts remain genomically invisible. This project provides what modern genomics cannot: a direct, continuous genomic record through the full post-settlement history, calibrated against Iceland's uniquely precise documentary record. Particularly significant are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE — horse remains coinciding almost exactly with the Laki eruption — which will allow direct genomic observation of the population immediately before and after the catastrophic famine, testing whether the historically documented 75% reduction is detectable as a genomic signal in ancient horse genomes from that moment in time. Where the same archaeological contexts have yielded both horse and human remains, comparison with ancient human genomic data will provide additional context for interpreting the horse demographic signals.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution:** What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation?

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked through the temporal dataset.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al. 2012). It is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses, forming a clear ROH island present in over 90% of individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024), and was already at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century founding horses, with statistical support for positive selection thereafter (Bayes Factor = 8.68; Wutke et al. 2016). The key question is when fixation was reached and how rapidly selection operated. Additional gait loci (STAU2, RELN) suggest the genetic architecture of tölt quality is more complex than a single variant (Sigurðardóttir 2025 PhD thesis); the temporal dataset will track all relevant loci simultaneously.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition present in Icelandic horses at clinically significant frequencies (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). Whether this allele was present from founding or arose later under drift or selection is entirely unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a later development, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity and disease susceptibility.* Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), one of the most economically significant conditions affecting the breed, has GWAS-identified loci on ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, and ECA18 (Shrestha et al. 2015) and on ECA20 in the MHC region (Schurink et al. 2012) [Claude comment: please verify these chromosome assignments against the original papers before submission]. The temporal dataset will track allele frequencies at these loci, addressing whether disease-associated variants rose to high frequency during the founding bottleneck or under later drift or selection. Despite its severe bottleneck history, the Icelandic horse reportedly retains 79 distinct MHC haplotypes (Maccari et al. 2019) [Claude comment: this figure could not be verified from the available background files — please confirm before submission]. Whether this immune diversity was present from founding or has been maintained by balancing selection is directly addressable with the temporal dataset.

Aim 3 tracks pre-identified genetic variants rather than attempting discovery GWAS — a distinction important for feasibility at this sample size, and one that connects the findings directly to the breed's documented health and performance concerns.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Established methods.* Ancient DNA extraction and sequencing library preparation for degraded bone and dental material are well-established methodologies, developed by the aDNA research community over the past two decades and implemented in dedicated clean-room facilities worldwide (Schubert et al. 2016; Jónsson et al. 2013; Li and Durbin 2009) [Claude comment: please check the supplementary methods of Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018 *Science* and confirm which specific extraction protocol paper was cited there — this should be the primary citation for the extraction method used in this project]. The applicant has applied these protocols at scale in studies of 27 (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018, *Science*) and 201 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation). Horse skeletal material from Icelandic archaeological contexts has already been shown to preserve aDNA at excellent levels: the sexing study by Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019) recovered a mean of 9.3% endogenous horse DNA (range 0.4–39.8%) across 22 Viking Age specimens, and five Icelandic horse specimens processed by the applicant using these same protocols yielded 10–40% endogenous DNA. There is no technical barrier to applying established human aDNA workflows to horse material from the same burial contexts.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, covering burial mounds (*kuml*), animal bone deposits (*dys*), and open site assemblages from more than 35 archaeological sites across all major regions of Iceland [**Sunna: verify exact site count before submission**]. In northern Iceland, sites include burial grounds at Dalvík/Brimnes (Eyjafjörður), Enni (Skagafjörður), Glaumbær (Skagafjörður), Stærri-Árskógur (Eyjafjörður), Granastaðir (Þingeyjarsýsla), and Grímsstaðir near Mývatn. South Iceland is represented by Hemla (Landeyjahreppur), Traðarholt, Mörk (Landmannahreppur), Niðurföll (near Þykkvabæjarklaustur), and Lækur in Flói. East Iceland contributes specimens from Berufjörður, Breiðdalur, and Seyðisfjörður, and the Westfjords are represented by sites in Barðastrandarsýsla. Particularly significant for Aim 2 are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, coinciding almost exactly with the Laki famine, which will allow direct genomic observation of the population at the moment of that catastrophic event. Seven libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) have been prepared from five quality-assessed specimens and are ready to enter Phase 2 immediately [Sunna: verify this VHR library list against the Excel sample inventory before submission].

**Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al. 2012), molecular markers on modern breeds (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014), a candidate locus on ancient specimens (Wutke et al. 2016), sex chromosome read ratios for biological sex determination (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019), modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024), or low-coverage shotgun sequencing of 22 Viking Age specimens, of which only two reached sufficient depth to be included in a global population survey (Fages et al. 2019, without imputation). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse population: the first to generate data sufficient for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and selection analysis, and the first to examine any time period beyond the Viking Age founding. Its originality operates at four levels.

*Geographically*, no genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the Norse Atlantic world. Ahmed et al. (under review) established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened once that founding stock arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

*Temporally*, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single genetically isolated population with a precisely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient population genomics. The genomic time series can be calibrated against specific, dated historical events, a resolution that is rarely achievable anywhere in the ancient genomics literature.

*As a model system*, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions about the genomic consequences of long-term isolation: how founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how sustained inbreeding distributes deleterious variants, and what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case of this pattern, and this project will trace it empirically through more than a millennium.

*Through paired data*, the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation) is directly relevant here for two reasons. First, Icelanders and their horses shared the same geographic and demographic isolation for over 1,100 years, making the human and horse populations natural parallels for studying the genomic consequences of long-term bottlenecks. Second, and more concretely, the human study sampled skeletal remains from 58 archaeological sites across Iceland, at least 17 of which [**Sunna: verify this number against the cross-reference file before submission**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project, including pagan burial mounds (*kuml*) documented in *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) and later medieval sites such as Skriðuklaustur [Claude comment: please confirm that horse remains from Skriðuklaustur are in the sampling list and verify the site details]. Paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial contexts, at this temporal depth and sample size, is without parallel in the current ancient genomics literature [Sunna: consider softening to "without parallel at this temporal depth and sample size" if you are concerned about overclaiming — there are paired datasets from individual graves elsewhere, just not at this combination of depth and scale].

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources. The methods are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species, including cattle, sheep, and dogs [Sunna: there are ancient DNA studies of these species from Scandinavia and Britain — please check whether any specifically use Icelandic skeletal material; if so, narrow the claim accordingly], none of which have been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods applied to Icelandic archaeological material, creating a template for future work.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's role in settlement-era society was profound: a working mount, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and stallion contests (hestamót), and a companion in death (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016; Leifsson 2018). Reconstructing the founding genetics of these animals speaks directly to the public understanding of Iceland's Viking Age origins.

For the Icelandic horse industry — approximately 80,000–100,000 registered horses in Iceland and more abroad [**Sunna: cite WorldFengur/FEIF statistics**] — the genetic health of the breed is a practical concern. Sweet itch affects an estimated 30–40% of Icelandic horses exported from Iceland [Sunna: citation needed — please check whether this figure comes from a published epidemiological study or from FEIF/WorldFengur data]. Ocular anomalies linked to PMEL17 are a recognised breeding concern (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). The first historical genomic baseline will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context, clarifying when disease-associated alleles arose and whether current levels of genetic uniformity are a product of the original founding or of later management decisions.

For global science, the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to conservation biology and evolutionary theory well beyond the Icelandic horse. The methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse occupies a unique position in the genomics of domestic animals. It is not simply an ancient breed (many breeds have long histories) but an ancient, isolated, and completely documented one. The combination of genomic closure, environmental challenge, and historical precision makes it a rare natural experiment in how populations evolve under sustained isolation.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia (long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses) are in fact the ancestors of Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*) used a large dataset of ancient and modern horse genomes [Claude comment: please verify the specific number of ancient genomes in this study against the published paper before submission] to pinpoint the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, documenting rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. Most directly relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, and establishes that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE, yet none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data, confirmed that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds, a pattern fully consistent with over a millennium of gene pool closure. The most comprehensive modern genomic study to date (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024, *BMC Genomics*) used a 670K SNP array in 380 Icelandic horses to estimate a current effective population size of 125 individuals, a genomic inbreeding coefficient (F_ROH) of 0.08–0.20, and clear ROH islands over DMRT3 and several other genes associated with performance and adaptation. Pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014, *Icelandic Agricultural Sciences*) provides complementary estimates: a pedigree Ne of approximately 113 for Iceland-born horses, an effective number of founders of approximately 151, and an effective number of ancestors of approximately 42, indicating that a small subset of founders has disproportionately shaped the modern gene pool. Y-chromosomal analysis of 12,896 Icelandic stallions confirmed that only Icelandic-specific haplotypes are present in the breed, with no evidence of recent external introductions (Weinzerl 2024). Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic stability across the 19th century using museum specimens. Together these studies establish a picture of a breed carrying the imprint of its founding, sustained by only a fraction of its original founders, and shaped by a breeding programme that has steadily concentrated genetic material from a handful of highly influential lines.

**Earlier research on the origins of the Icelandic horse.** Icelandic researchers have long suspected a Norwegian primary ancestry. Eldjárn (1991) proposed the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage on morphological grounds, and Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson (1998) confirmed striking physical resemblance, including the tölt gait, through field comparisons in northern Norway. Five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform the tölt (Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson 1998); the physical similarity between the two breeds is striking enough that they can be nearly impossible to distinguish by eye. The first molecular evidence for a close relationship between the Icelandic horse, the Shetland pony, and the Nordland/Lyngen horse comes from genetic marker studies [Claude comment: please confirm which specific Bjørnstad paper (2000 or 2003) establishes the Icelandic/Shetland/Nordland relationship — the 2000 paper 'Genetic structure of Norwegian horse breeds' covers Norwegian breeds only and does not appear to include Icelandic horses in its dataset. It is possible the correct citation is Bjørnstad et al. 2003 (*Animal Genetics* 34:55–58) or another study]. The Shetland connection is geographically logical: Norse settlers were established in Shetland before moving onward to Iceland [Claude comment: a citation is needed for the ~800 CE Norse settlement of Shetland — please confirm the best source], and horses bred there would have shared ancestry with Norwegian stock. The open question of whether British or Celtic ancestry also contributed (the subject of Aim 1) cannot be resolved without ancient genomic data from Iceland itself.

**Ancient DNA work on Icelandic horses.** Four studies have generated molecular or genome-scale data from archaeological Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across multiple European sites, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses, with strong statistical evidence for positive selection (Bayes Factor = 8.68). This pattern is consistent with an origin of the ambling allele in Viking Age Britain and its transport to Iceland with British-affiliated settlers. Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts using low-depth shotgun sequencing (range: 1,010–2,180,600 mapped reads; mean endogenous DNA 9.3%), confirming a male bias in burial horses and demonstrating that Icelandic horse skeletal material preserves ancient DNA at excellent levels. The zooarchaeological context for those same specimens is provided by Leifsson (2018), who documents riding-related skeletal lesions, butchery evidence, and the social protocols governing horse burial in Viking Age Iceland, providing the cultural framework within which the genomic data from this project will be interpreted. Of the 22 specimens, only two (VHR031 from Granastaðir and VHR102 from Berufjörður) had sufficient sequencing depth (0.08× and 0.18× autosomal coverage) to be included in the global ancient horse genome survey of Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*). In that global context, phylogenetic analysis placed these two early Icelandic horses closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse — consistent with a founding stock that included a British Isles component alongside the primary Norwegian ancestry, as the DMRT3 evidence also suggests. Campana et al. (2012) demonstrated DNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone specimens. Together, these studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is tractable for ancient DNA analysis. What they cannot provide is the depth or temporal range required for systematic population genomic inference.

**The gap and what makes it addressable now.** Two developments make this project possible at this moment. The first is methodological: the GLIMPSE2 imputation framework (Rubinacci et al. 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3× coverage, reducing sequencing cost per genome by an order of magnitude and making a 90-specimen ancient genomics project tractable within a postdoctoral fellowship budget. The second is the reference panel: Prof. Orlando's collection of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles from the Viking Age itself, provides the dense reference needed for imputation and the direct comparators needed for Aim 1. The applicant's five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries, yielding 10–40% endogenous DNA, confirm that the material is suitable and that the project is ready to begin.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT, ETC.) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; computing runs on the deCODE cluster or, if required, on the University of Iceland cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CEH Copenhagen or CAGT Toulouse. The principal risk of low endogenous DNA in some specimens is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool (up to 90 specimens) and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, sufficient for robust population genomic inference.

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10).** The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread. Teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics; cortical and petrous bone are used where teeth are unavailable. Silica-based DNA extraction is performed in the deCODE dedicated ancient DNA clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing (Meyer and Kircher 2010). All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (~1 million reads per library) for quality screening: endogenous DNA content is assessed against the EquCab3 horse reference genome using BWA (Li and Durbin 2009) after adapter trimming with AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al. 2016), and characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends) are confirmed with mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al. 2013). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5× target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. Duplicates are removed using Picard MarkDuplicates; base-quality rescaling is applied with mapDamage2. Biological sex is estimated from X- and Y-chromosome read ratios. Contamination control relies on unique dual indexing, damage authentication, and endogenous content thresholds; the standard ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse material.

**Phase 2 — Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24).** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al. 2023) with Prof. Orlando's reference panel, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al. 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al. 2012) characterise founding ancestry and test competing models of the founding event. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al. 2020) tracks genealogical relationships across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC, Li and Durbin 2011; SMC++, Terhorst et al. 2017) reconstructs effective population size through time. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (~1402 CE).

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36).** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH, Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS, Voight et al. 2006; PBS, Yi et al. 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 and additional gait-associated loci (STAU2, RELN; Sigurðardóttir 2025), coat colour and vision loci (ASIP, MC1R, PMEL17; Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), and the MHC region (Maccari et al. 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

## F. IF APPLICABLE, CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics — a field in which no training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1 — The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology): As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical — no additional laboratory work is required — making it fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** (archaeology or biological anthropology): The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves — all four individuals have been sequenced in the applicant's ancient Icelandic human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) — and two separate horse burials associated with warrior graves (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) [Claude comment: please confirm the specific grave equipment details — sword/axes/spearhead in one grave, bridle bit and iron fittings in another — against *Kuml og haugfé* or the site report]. This makes Sílastaðir one of the most precisely documented assemblages of co-deposited human and horse remains in Iceland. The student will examine whether the two horses are genetically related, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the human interred alongside it — the protocols governing who received horse burial in Viking Age Iceland are documented by Leifsson (2018) — and whether the Sílastaðir horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Six months of salary support is included in the budget. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills directly applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.
