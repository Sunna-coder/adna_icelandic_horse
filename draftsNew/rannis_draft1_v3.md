## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse is among the most genetically isolated domestic animal populations on earth and one of Iceland's most enduring cultural symbols. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed by the archaeological record — it has remained separated from all other horse breeds for over 1,100 years. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted what is one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of horses to Iceland [citation needed]. Any horse that leaves Iceland cannot return. This legislation, maintained without interruption from the Viking Age to the present day, has produced a population of extraordinary scientific value: a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable from the genomic record alone.

Despite this unique combination of historical documentation, biological isolation, and cultural significance, the ancient genetic history of the Icelandic horse is entirely unknown at the genomic level. We do not know which Scandinavian populations contributed to the founding stock, whether horses arrived in one event or many, how the population responded to Iceland's volcanic famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most distinctive traits — its gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under isolation. These are not merely academic questions: the Icelandic horse is a living part of Iceland's national identity, a significant economic resource, and an exceptional scientific model for studying the genomic consequences of long-term isolation in a domestic species. This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding**: What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced in a single founding event or through multiple waves of immigration? What Scandinavian and European populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions? Did the composition of the founding population change across the settlement period (870–1000 CE)? The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock across the North Atlantic; documentary sources confirm that Viking raiding parties acquired horses locally rather than transporting them by sea, so the founding Icelandic horses came specifically with the colonists, reflecting their departure points. The ancient human genomics of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry — many settlers departing not directly from western Norway but from Norse settlements in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where Scandinavian populations had lived alongside Celtic-speaking people for a generation or more. If the people who settled Iceland were mixed Norse-Gaelic, the horses they brought very plausibly were too: a settler from the Hebrides would have brought horses carrying a mixture of Scandinavian and native Celtic or British pony ancestry. The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds — small stature, extreme hardiness, dense coat — has long suggested such an ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis," but it has never been tested with ancient genomic data. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE in the Viking Age, but does not resolve whether horses with Celtic or British ancestry also contributed to the founding stock. This project will answer that question for the first time.

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation**: How did inbreeding, genetic diversity, and effective population size change across 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises — the Black Death (~1402 CE), the catastrophic Laki volcanic famine (1783–1784 CE), which reduced the horse population by an estimated 70% within two years [citation needed], and periodic livestock epidemics — leave detectable signatures in the genomic record? The known historical timeline, with major demographic events precisely dated in written and documentary sources, allows a level of calibration between genomics and history that is almost never achievable in ancient population genomics. Whether such events are visible as reductions in effective population size, increases in runs of homozygosity, or shifts in allele frequencies is a question this project is uniquely positioned to answer.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution**: What genomic signatures of local adaptation and selective breeding are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How do the genetic variants associated with the breed's most distinctive traits change in frequency through time? The Icelandic horse's most celebrated trait, the tölt, depends on a single premature stop codon in *DMRT3* (Andersson et al. 2012): when did this allele reach fixation — was it selected from the founding stock, or did it rise to dominance gradually over centuries? Similarly, the *PMEL17* Arg618Cys mutation responsible for the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome — documented at clinically significant frequencies in the breed (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011) — has an unknown history in the Icelandic population. Sweet itch (insect bite hypersensitivity), which affects an estimated 30–40% of exported Icelandic horses [citation needed], is associated with genomic regions on ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, and ECA18 (Shrestha et al. 2015) and the ECA20 MHC region (Schurink et al. 2012). The temporal dataset will allow us to determine when susceptibility alleles first appeared and whether they rose through drift or selection. Notably, despite their bottleneck history, Icelandic horses display unexpectedly high MHC heterozygosity — a recent study found 79 distinct MHC haplotypes, attributed to a recombination hotspot mechanism maintaining immune gene diversity under isolation (Maccari et al. 2019) — and exceptional longevity (routinely 30–35 years, records of 40+), likely connected to this breadth of immune defence. Whether MHC diversity was present from founding or was sustained by balancing selection during isolation is directly addressable with the temporal dataset proposed here.

The project is highly feasible, resting on four pillars of prior work that together eliminate the main risks associated with ancient animal genomics projects. **Proven aDNA methods for Icelandic material**: the applicant has developed aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols specifically optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material and applied them at scale in Ebenesersdóttir et al. (*Science* 2018) to recover genome-wide data from 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals, including specimens over 1,100 years old; an expanded dataset of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation) further demonstrates the reliability and scalability of this approach. Crucially, five ancient Icelandic horse specimens have already been extracted and sequenced using the same protocol, yielding 10–40% endogenous horse DNA — far above the ≥1% threshold required for high-quality genomic analysis, and confirming that Icelandic horse bone and dental material preserves aDNA at excellent levels. **Existing sample collection**: sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE from more than 35 archaeological sites distributed across all major regions of Iceland [Sunna: please verify exact site count]. In northern Iceland, sites include burial grounds at Dalvík/Brimnes (Eyjafjörður), Enni (Skagafjörður), Glaumbær (Skagafjörður), Stærri-Árskógur (Eyjafjörður), Granastaðir (Þingeyjarsýsla), and Grímsstaðir near Mývatn. South Iceland is represented by Hemla (Landeyjahreppur), Traðarholt, Mörk (Landmannahreppur), Niðurföll (near Þykkvabæjarklaustur), and Lækur in Flói. East Iceland contributes specimens from Berufjörður, Breiðdalur, and Seyðisfjörður, and the Westfjords are represented by sites in Barðastrandarsýsla. Particularly significant for Aim 2 are several specimens from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE — horse remains coinciding almost exactly with the Laki famine, the catastrophic livestock crisis this project aims to detect in the genomic record. The collection spans burial contexts (*kuml*), animal bone deposits (*dys*), and other assemblages, providing the geographic breadth and temporal depth essential to all three aims. Seven libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) have been prepared, five of which have been quality-assessed and are ready for Phase 2. **Infrastructure and computing**: all wet laboratory work will be conducted at the deCODE Genetics ancient DNA facility, Reykjavík — the facility in which the applicant developed the protocols now used in this project; the collaboration agreement provides access to the dedicated aDNA clean-room laboratory, the Illumina NovaSeq sequencing platform, and the deCODE high-performance computing cluster; no new infrastructure investment is required. No genome-wide ancient DNA data suitable for population-genomic analysis exist for any horse population from the North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroe Islands, or the Norse Atlantic world. This project is the first genome-wide investigation of the Icelandic horse across time. Its originality rests on four dimensions. First, geographic and biological uniqueness: the Icelandic horse is the direct biological descendant of Viking Age Norse horse husbandry, preserved in genomic amber by the livestock import ban [citation needed]; the entire North Atlantic remains unsampled in the ancient horse genomic record, and Ahmed et al. (under review) has established the NORD ancestry component that defines Icelandic horses emerged at 810 CE — our project will be the first to examine what happened to that founding stock after it arrived in Iceland. Second, temporal depth and historical resolution: a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single isolated population with a fully documented historical record allows calibration of genomics against precisely dated historical events — a resolution level that is almost never achievable in ancient population genomics. Third, scientific model system: the Icelandic horse's combination of genetic isolation, demographic documentation, and phenotypic distinctiveness makes it a powerful model for questions central to evolutionary biology and conservation genetics (Frankham 1997) — how do founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants, what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. Fourth, paired human and horse genomic data: the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) has generated genome-wide data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals from 58 sites across Iceland, many of which also contain horse remains targeted in this project; combining these datasets creates paired human-horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events — a scientific resource that does not exist anywhere else in the world.

The impacts of this project will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, it establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources; the aDNA methods, bioinformatic pipelines, and reference data generated here are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods, creating both a scientific result and a methodological template for future work on Icelandic bioarchaeological material. For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's central role in settlement-era Icelandic society — as a working animal, a ritual object, a mark of social status, and a companion in death (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019) — is well documented in the sagas and the archaeological record, and reconstructing the founding of the breed in the Viking Age will contribute directly to public understanding of Icelandic history, disseminated through the National Museum of Iceland as a primary partner. For the Icelandic horse industry, there are approximately 80,000–100,000 registered Icelandic horses worldwide [citation needed]; sweet itch affects an estimated 30–40% of exported horses [citation needed] and ocular anomalies linked to *PMEL17* are a recognised breeding concern; the first historical genomic baseline for the breed will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context and to clarify when and how disease-associated alleles rose to their current frequencies. For global science, the Icelandic horse is an exceptional model for evolutionary biology and conservation genetics; the methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project, and the findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and selection under isolation are relevant to conservation biology broadly.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse occupies an exceptional position in both natural history and Icelandic culture. Descended from horses transported during the Norse settlement in the late 9th century CE, the breed has remained isolated for over 1,100 years under one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import regulations. It is characterised by low within-breed diversity consistent with its closed population history (Petersen et al. 2013), two unique gaits — the tölt and the flying pace — controlled by a *DMRT3* premature stop codon identified from genome-wide association analysis using Icelandic horses specifically (Andersson et al. 2012, *Nature*), a heritable disease profile including MCOA syndrome from the *PMEL17* Silver mutation (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011), and unexpectedly high MHC heterozygosity (79 distinct haplotypes) despite its bottleneck history, maintained by a recombination hotspot mechanism (Maccari et al. 2019). Despite this richness of phenotypic and genetic character, none of the breed's genomic history through time has ever been investigated.

The broader field of ancient horse genomics has advanced rapidly. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) demonstrated that the Botai horses of Central Asia are ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, not of modern domestic horses, resetting the field's understanding of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*), using 273 ancient horse genomes, pinpointed the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the homeland of domestic horses, with rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE — synchronous with the spread of spoke-wheeled chariots (see also Warmuth et al. 2012 for microsatellite-based evidence of the same dispersal). Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled 278 ancient equid genomes, identifying two now-extinct horse lineages (Iberian and Siberian) and documenting a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. The recent Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, identifies seven major genetic ancestries shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, and establishes that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic and Scandinavian breeds emerged at approximately 810 CE — the most direct scientific context for the proposed project.

Despite this progress, the entire North Atlantic remains unsampled. The only ancient DNA study of Icelandic horses (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) provided the first molecular sex-typing of 22 Viking Age specimens — confirming a male bias in burial horses and that the specimens yield recoverable aDNA — but generated no population-genomic data. A specific and unresolved question concerns the composition of the founding stock: many early Icelanders departed not from western Norway directly but from Norse settlements in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland, where horses already carried a mixture of Scandinavian and native Celtic or British pony ancestry. The physical similarities between the Icelandic horse and native Atlantic island breeds have long suggested the "Celtic pony hypothesis," but this has never been tested genomically. The present project will test it directly.

The applicant's study of 27 ancient Icelandic human genomes (Ebenesersdóttir et al., *Science* 2018) directly establishes the feasibility of the proposed horse project. The aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols developed and optimised for degraded Icelandic skeletal material at deCODE Genetics — that enabled genome-wide analysis of 1,100-year-old human remains — are directly transferable to horse specimens from the same archaeological contexts. These protocols have been applied to 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation), further validating their reliability and scalability. Preliminary work has confirmed their applicability to horse material: five Icelandic horse specimens yielded 10–40% endogenous horse DNA, far exceeding the threshold required for genomic analysis.

A key methodological innovation enabling this project is genotype imputation to increase the density of analysable variants from low-coverage sequencing data. The GLIMPSE2 framework (Rubinacci et al. 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 0.75–1× coverage, dramatically increasing the number of individuals that can be analysed per sequencing budget. This approach has been validated for ancient horse genomes in Ahmed et al. (under review) using Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, and the applicant has direct experience applying this framework in the Iceland2 human genomics study (manuscript in preparation).

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT, ETC.) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; all computing runs on the deCODE cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CAGT Toulouse as a contracted service under the Orlando collaboration. The principal risk — low endogenous DNA in some specimens — is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool (up to 90 specimens) and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is sufficient for robust population genomic inference using the imputation approach described below.

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing** (Months 1–10): The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to assess the available skeletal material and select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread across Iceland. Up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE will be selected; teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics, with cortical and petrous bone used where teeth are unavailable. All specimens remain at the Museum until sampled under the existing sampling permission. Silica-based DNA extraction — the protocol developed by the applicant and optimised for Icelandic skeletal material — is performed in the deCODE aDNA dedicated clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing. All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (~1 million reads per library) to assess endogenous DNA content and confirm characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends, characteristic fragmentation). Authentication is evaluated using mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al. 2013). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5× target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. The bioinformatic pipeline applies AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al. 2016), BWA mapping to the EquCab3 horse reference genome (Li and Durbin 2009), Picard MarkDuplicates, and mapDamage2 base-quality rescaling. Biological sex is estimated from the ratio of reads mapping to the X and Y chromosomes. Note on contamination estimation: the ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse specimens; contamination control for horse libraries relies on unique dual indexing (preventing cross-contamination between samples in the same sequencing run), damage pattern authentication by mapDamage2, and endogenous content thresholds.

**Phase 2 — Population structure, ancestry, and demography** (Months 9–24): Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al. 2023) and Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. The imputation pipeline was developed and validated by the Orlando laboratory in Ahmed et al. (under review) and will run in close collaboration with CAGT Toulouse. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al. 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al. 2012) characterise founding ancestry, quantify contributions of specific Scandinavian and European source populations, and test competing models of the founding event — single introduction versus multiple waves. Temporal changes in ancestry across the settlement period are examined to detect shifts in the founding population's composition. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis uses the approach validated in the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation); where applicable for horse genomes, published IBD frameworks such as hap-IBD (Zhou et al. 2020) will be employed. IBD estimates genealogical relationships between individuals and tracks the progressive accumulation of shared ancestry through time. Runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level, with the temporal profile of ROH lengths informing the timing and severity of population bottlenecks. Demographic modelling (PSMC; Li and Durbin 2011; SMC++; Terhorst et al. 2017) reconstructs changes in effective population size. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (~1402 CE).

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution** (Months 20–36): Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH; Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS; Voight et al. 2006; population branch statistic PBS; Yi et al. 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at *DMRT3* (tölt), *ASIP*/*MC1R*/*PMEL17* (coat colour and MCOA), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), and MHC/immune regions (Maccari et al. 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency, providing a population-level explanation for the breed's known disease profile.

## F. IF APPLICABLE, CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for **one Master's student** from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics — a field in which no trained Icelandic researchers currently exist — contributing directly to the development of national research capacity. Two candidate project definitions are outlined below; the final project will be selected based on the background and interests of the recruited student.

**Option 1 — The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** *(biology or biological anthropology)*: The *PMEL17* Arg618Cys mutation is responsible for both the Silver coat colour — one of the most visually distinctive and frequently selected phenotypes in the modern Icelandic horse — and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition documented at clinically significant frequencies in the breed (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). The history of this allele in the Icelandic population has never been investigated. The student will extract genotype calls at the *PMEL17* locus and a set of coat colour and trait-associated loci from the imputed ancient genomes generated in Phase 2 and track allele frequencies through time across the 870–1900 CE temporal range. No additional laboratory work is required — the project is entirely analytical, making it feasible within a 12-month research period. The output will be a self-contained Master's thesis and a targeted manuscript on the evolutionary history of a medically and economically important trait variant in an isolated domestic breed.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** *(archaeology or biological anthropology)*: The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves — all four individuals have been sequenced in the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) — and two separate horse burials: one at the lower end of Grave 1 (a richly equipped warrior burial with sword, axes, and spearhead), and one at the northern end of Grave 4 (a second warrior burial, with the horse accompanied by a bridle bit and iron fittings). This configuration makes Sílastaðir one of the most precisely documented assemblages of co-deposited human and horse remains in Iceland, uniquely suited to a study combining archaeogenomics with traditional archaeological interpretation. The student will examine whether the two horses are genetically related to each other or derive from different breeding lines; whether the genomic profile of each burial horse (sex, ancestry, inbreeding coefficient) correlates with the social status and grave-good assemblage of the human interred alongside it; and whether the Sílastaðir horses show genetic connections to horses at other Icelandic sites. The student will participate in the aDNA extraction and sequencing of the two Sílastaðir specimens during Phase 1 and carry out the focused population genomic analysis in Phase 2.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Though the student is expected to seek primary funding through University of Iceland student research grants, six months of salary support is included in this budget to strengthen the supervisory arrangement and ensure the project timeline is met. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit for Icelandic science: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills directly applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.

## G. CAREER DEVELOPMENT PLAN

This project builds on an established research programme in ancient genomics and represents the applicant's deliberate transition into ancient animal genomics as an independent research focus. Dr Ebenesersdóttir completed her PhD in 2023 at the University of Iceland and has published on ancient Icelandic human genomics at the scale of 27 genomes (*Science* 2018), with a companion study of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes currently in preparation. Co-authorship on Ahmed et al. (under review) marks her strategic extension into ancient horse genomics, the field this project is designed to develop as her primary independent research programme.

**Scientific development**: Leading this project will advance the applicant's skills in three specific areas that are strategically important for her research programme. First, horse-specific population genomic analysis — selection scans, breed structure inference, and the interpretation of functional variants — will expand her methodological repertoire beyond human population genomics. Second, the temporal analysis of trait-associated variants will develop her expertise in connecting genomic data to phenotype, an increasingly central skill in ancient genomics. Third, leading an international collaboration with a large, active laboratory (Orlando/CAGT) will develop her experience in managing multi-partner genomic projects. The active collaboration with Prof. Orlando provides access to the world's leading horse genomics environment, including annual research visits at CAGT Toulouse for direct technical exchange and the deepening of international networks.

**Conference participation and international visibility**: The applicant will present results at international ancient DNA and population genomics conferences throughout the project, including the International Symposium on Biomolecular Archaeology (ISBA) and the Society for Molecular Biology and Evolution (SMBE) annual meeting, as well as relevant Icelandic scientific meetings, building international visibility throughout the grant period.

**Institutional development**: This project strengthens the University of Iceland's position as an active contributor to ancient genomics — a field in which Iceland's unique archaeological and historical resources give it a natural comparative advantage. The applicant's growing international network and the collaborative infrastructure established through this project will position the University of Iceland to attract future collaborations and funding in this area. The training of a Master's student creates a concrete local capacity-building outcome that benefits the University beyond the lifetime of the project.

**Long-term trajectory**: The applicant's long-term goal is to establish an independent research group in ancient genomics at the University of Iceland, focused on the genomic history of Iceland's human and animal populations. The proposed project is the scientific foundation of this programme: it generates the core dataset, establishes the key international collaborations, and produces the publication record needed to compete for independent research funding at the European level. Specifically, the results of this project are designed to form the basis of a future application to the European Research Council (ERC Starting Grant), which would fund the expansion of the ancient animal genomics programme to additional Icelandic species and time periods.
