## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse has been separated from every other horse population on earth for over 1,100 years. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed in the archaeological record — the breed has remained in near-complete genomic isolation ever since. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the importation of new horses to Iceland (Stelter 2014). Any horse that leaves cannot return. The result is a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable through its own genome.

That history has never been systematically examined with ancient genomics. The only genome-scale data from ancient Icelandic horses consist of two Viking Age specimens sequenced to 0.08× and 0.18× autosomal coverage as part of a global survey (Fages et al. 2019) — far below what is needed for population structure, demographic history, or selection analyses, and representing only the founding period of a 1,100-year story. We do not know which Scandinavian or European populations contributed to the founding stock, whether the breed was established through a single colonisation event or through multiple waves of settlement, how the population responded to Iceland's recurring famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most celebrated traits — its unique gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under more than a millennium of isolation.

This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and will address three specific scientific aims.

---

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding:** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced through a single founding event or through multiple waves? What populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions?

The question of the founding stock's identity is more complex than it might first appear, and the answer has real implications for understanding the Norse world. A crucial distinction, supported by the documentary record [Claude comment: Stelter (2014) makes this argument drawing on documentary sources. If you want to cite primary annals or chronicles directly, the specific sources should be identified — otherwise '(Stelter 2014)' is sufficient and is what the existing literature uses], is that Viking raiding parties acquired horses locally at their destinations rather than transporting them by sea; horses were brought across the ocean specifically for colonisation (Stelter 2014). This means the ancestry of Iceland's founding horses directly reflects the departure points of those colonists. And those departure points were not all in Norway.

The companion ancient human genomics study of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018, *Science*; in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry in proportions of approximately 57:43 at the time of settlement [Claude comment: please confirm this figure is from the in-preparation manuscript and can be stated here — if it is not yet published, reviewers may ask for the basis of this specific number. Could be cited as 'Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation' with the caveat that it is a preliminary finding] — a majority of Norse settlers drawn disproportionately from southern and western Norway, alongside a substantial Gaelic-affiliated component from Norse communities established in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland. This mixed origin of the settler population is independently supported by the historical and archaeological record (McGovern et al. 2007). A settler departing from the Hebrides would have brought horses reflecting a mixture of Scandinavian and native Celtic or British pony ancestry. The horses were shaped by the same geography as the people.

The molecular evidence is consistent with this picture. The most direct study of the Icelandic horse's origins (Bjørnstad et al. 2000, *Journal of Animal Breeding and Genetics*) compared genetic markers across Norwegian breeds, the Shetland pony, British breeds, and a Mongolian horse, and found that the Icelandic horse and the Shetland pony are more closely related to each other than to any other breed in the study — and that both are most closely related to the Norwegian Nordland/Lyngen horse. The physical similarity between the Nordland/Lyngen horse and the Icelandic horse is striking enough that the two breeds can be nearly impossible to distinguish by eye, and five of nine Nordland/Lyngen horses tested could perform the tölt — the four-beat lateral gait that is one of the Icelandic breed's most celebrated characteristics (Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson 1998; Eldjárn 1991). The Shetland connection is geographically logical: Norwegian settlers were established in Shetland from approximately 800 CE [Claude comment: this date needs a citation — something like Graham-Campbell & Batey (1998) *Vikings in Scotland* or Woolf (2007) *From Pictland to Alba* would cover Norse settlement of the northern isles. Please confirm which source you prefer, or whether this can be cited to a more directly relevant reference], and the horses bred there would have shared ancestry with Norwegian stock before being taken onward to Iceland.

What is less clear is whether the founding population also received a contribution from British or Celtic stock. The most relevant indirect evidence comes from Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*), who found the DMRT3 ambling allele — which they trace to medieval England around 850–900 CE — already present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic horses from the 9th–11th century, but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses. This pattern is consistent with a significant British Isles contribution to the founding population alongside the primary Norwegian component. Phylogenetic analysis of the two ancient Icelandic horses in Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) similarly placed them closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse — again pointing to a genetically complex founding with a British Isles component. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, establishes that the NORD ancestry component characterising modern Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but does not resolve what proportions of Norwegian, Shetland-intermediary, or British-Celtic ancestry were present in the founding stock, nor whether those proportions changed across the settlement period (870–1000 CE).

Selection shaped the founding from the beginning. Settlers crossing the North Atlantic chose animals able to survive the voyage — animals built for robustness (Stelter 2014). [Claude comment: Stelter (2020, *Icelandic Horse Quarterly*) names the earliest Icelandic horses as relatives of the Dølehest, Coldblooded Trotter, and Fjord — all Norwegian breeds, consistent with our narrative. It could also go in Section B's origins paragraph.] Once in Iceland, natural selection continued: horses susceptible to harsh winters, volcanic ashfalls, fluoride poisoning from eruptions [Claude comment: 'fluoride poisoning from eruptions' — fluorosis from Laki tephra fallout — is well documented but should have a citation here. Thorarinsson (1979) or a veterinary/volcanological source would be appropriate; alternatively the Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024 paper may discuss this in the historical context section], and periods of severe feed shortage reproduced less successfully than those tolerant of these conditions. Over 1,100 years, this combination of deliberate choice and environmental pressure produced a breed genuinely adapted to Iceland's particular demands. This project will trace that process in the genomic record, and will directly test which source populations contributed to the founding stock using genome-wide data from founding-era horses compared against a reference panel that includes ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Viking Age itself — horses contemporary with Iceland's founders, not modern breeds that have themselves changed in the intervening millennium.

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation:** How did genetic diversity, inbreeding, and effective population size change over 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

The most comprehensive modern genomic study of the Icelandic horse (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024, *BMC Genomics*) analysed 670K SNPs in 380 Icelandic horses and estimated a current genomic effective population size of just 125 individuals — strikingly low, consistent with the breed's long history of isolation. That study detected two Ne crashes in the genomes of living horses: a major contraction corresponding to the Laki/Skaftáreldar volcanic famine of 1783–1784, visible approximately 23 generations ago [Claude comment: please verify — does Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024 state a specific percentage reduction (e.g. 70–75%) for this Ne crash, or only that a crash is detectable at ~23 generations? If the percentage is not in the paper, we should remove it and simply say 'a major Ne reduction']; and a second contraction associated with the early 20th-century collapse in demand for working horses as mechanisation replaced them. Pedigree-based analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014) estimated the effective number of founder genomes at approximately 27 and the pedigree Ne at approximately 113 [Claude comment: please verify these two figures — 'founder genomes ~27' and 'pedigree Ne ~113' — directly against Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014. The founder genome count also appears in Sunna's edits file, which supports it, but the Ne figure of ~113 should be double-checked] — consistent with the genomic estimate and indicating that the breed's low genetic diversity reflects both an initial founding bottleneck and subsequent demographic crashes.

But the genomes of living horses cannot reach beyond the 18th century. The founding period, the medieval centuries, and the catastrophic mortality of the Black Death (~1402 CE) remain genomically invisible. This project will provide what living-animal genomics cannot: a direct view into the post-settlement history of the breed, calibrated against a historical record of unprecedented precision. Iceland's documentary sources allow the genomic time series to be anchored to known historical events — the Laki famine, the Black Death, the Sturlung Age civil conflicts — in a way that is rarely achievable in ancient genomics.

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution:** What genomic signatures of selection are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation?

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked through the temporal dataset.

*Gait.* The DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus controlling the tölt and flying pace (Andersson et al. 2012). It is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses — present as an ROH island in over 90% of individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024) — and was already present at 77% frequency in 9th–11th century founding horses (Wutke et al. 2016, with strong statistical evidence of subsequent positive selection, Bayes Factor = 8.68). The key question is when fixation was reached and how rapidly: did breeders select for gait from the very beginning, or did fixation occur gradually over centuries? Recent work has shown that the genetic architecture of gait is more complex than a single locus. Two additional QTLs contributing to tölt quality and pace contain STAU2 and RELN — genes expressed in neural tissue — indicating that the coordination underlying the tölt involves more than a single mutation (Sigurðardóttir 2025). The temporal dataset will allow tracking of these additional loci alongside the primary DMRT3 signal.

*Coat colour and vision health.* The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation is responsible for both the Silver coat colour — one of the most visually distinctive phenotypes in the modern Icelandic horse — and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition documented at clinically significant frequencies in the breed (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). Whether this allele was present in the founding population or emerged later under drift is entirely unknown. Tracking its frequency from 870 CE onwards will clarify whether MCOA is a founding-era liability or a product of later selection, with direct implications for breeding management.

*Immune diversity, disease susceptibility, and longevity.* Despite the population's severe bottleneck history, Icelandic horses retain 79 distinct MHC haplotypes — the major histocompatibility complex encodes the proteins responsible for immune recognition and pathogen defence, and this level of diversity is exceptional given the low overall genomic diversity of the breed (Maccari et al. 2019). The breed is also known for exceptional longevity, routinely living 30–35 years [Claude comment: this claim needs a citation — is there a veterinary or breed-registry source for the 30–35 year lifespan figure? WorldFengur or FEIF statistics, or a veterinary journal reference, would be ideal], which may be connected in part to this preserved immune diversity. Whether this MHC variation was present from founding or was maintained by balancing selection across the subsequent millennium is directly addressable with the temporal dataset. The sweet itch GWAS loci (ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, ECA18, ECA20/MHC; Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012) [Claude comment: please verify these chromosome numbers — ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, ECA18, ECA20/MHC — against the Shrestha 2015 and Schurink 2012 papers directly. The two studies identified overlapping but not identical loci, and the specific chromosomes listed should reflect what the two papers together report] allow tracking of alleles associated with one of the breed's most significant veterinary conditions. More broadly, the temporal dataset, spanning multiple volcanic episodes and severe winters, offers the first opportunity to identify genome-wide signatures of natural selection for the specific ecological conditions of Iceland.

Aim 3 tracks specific, pre-identified genetic variants rather than attempting discovery GWAS on a new dataset — a distinction important for feasibility at this sample size, and one that directly connects the ancient genomic findings to the breed's documented health and performance concerns.

**Feasibility.** The project rests on four pillars.

*Proven methods.* The aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols developed by the applicant at deCODE Genetics were applied to recover genome-wide data from 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018, *Science*) and subsequently from 201 ancient Icelandic individuals spanning 870–2000 CE (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation). Five ancient Icelandic horse specimens processed using the same protocol yielded 10–40% endogenous horse DNA — well above the ≥1% threshold required for genomic analysis — confirming that Icelandic horse skeletal material preserves ancient DNA at excellent levels.

*Sample collection.* Sampling permission has been granted by the National Museum of Iceland for up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE, from more than 35 archaeological sites distributed across all major regions of Iceland [**Sunna: verify exact site count before submission**]. Coverage is geographically broad: northern Iceland sites include Dalvík/Brimnes, Enni and Glaumbær (Skagafjörður), Stærri-Árskógur, and Granastaðir; south Iceland is represented by Hemla, Traðarholt, Mörk, Niðurföll, and Lækur; east Iceland by Berufjörður and Breiðdalur; and the Westfjords by sites in Barðastrandarsýsla. Seven libraries (VHR031, VHR085, VHR089, VHR093, VHR100, VHR102, VHR105) have been prepared from five quality-assessed specimens and are ready to be incorporated directly into Phase 2.

*Infrastructure.* All wet laboratory work will be conducted at the deCODE Genetics dedicated ancient DNA facility in Reykjavík — the facility in which the applicant developed the protocols used in this project — under an existing collaboration agreement covering laboratory access, the Illumina NovaSeq sequencing platform, and the deCODE high-performance computing cluster [**Sunna: confirm the current status of the deCODE agreement and whether a signed copy can be attached**]. No new infrastructure investment is required.

*International collaboration.* The project is conducted in collaboration with Prof. Ludovic Orlando (CAGT, University of Toulouse) — active co-author on Ahmed et al. (under review) — whose laboratory provides the horse imputation reference panel (901 modern + 376 ancient horse genomes, including ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Viking Age) and a sequencing fallback at CAGT Toulouse if needed. The project is designed to proceed robustly with approximately 40–50 high-quality imputed genomes if the full 90 cannot be recovered.

**Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al. 2012), a limited set of molecular markers on modern breeds (Bjørnstad et al. 2000; Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014), single candidate loci on ancient specimens (Wutke et al. 2016; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019), modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024), or low-coverage genome-scale sequencing of two specimens as minor additions to a global survey (Fages et al. 2019, at 0.08× and 0.18× autosomal coverage without imputation). This is the first systematic ancient genomic investigation of the Icelandic horse population — the first to generate data sufficient for population structure inference, demographic history reconstruction, and selection analysis — and the first to examine any time period beyond the Viking Age founding. Its originality operates at four levels.

*Geographically*, the entire North Atlantic remains a blind spot on the ancient horse genomic map. Ahmed et al. (under review) establishes that a founding NORD ancestry component was already present by approximately 810 CE, but does not address what happened to that founding stock once it arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted across the following millennium, or which specific source populations contributed to it. This project traces the full post-arrival history for the first time.

*Temporally*, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single, genetically isolated population with a completely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient population genomics. Here, the genomic time series can be directly calibrated against precisely dated historical events — a resolution that is rarely achievable.

*As a model system*, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions about the genomic consequences of isolation: how founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how sustained inbreeding distributes deleterious variants, what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case of this pattern, and this project will trace it empirically across more than a millennium.

*Through paired data*, the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., manuscript in preparation) has generated genome-wide data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals from 58 sites across Iceland, at least 17 of which [**Sunna: verify this number against the cross-reference file before submission**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project. This creates paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events — including burial sites documented in the foundational catalogue *Kuml og haugfé* (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) — a resource without parallel in the current ancient genomics literature at this temporal depth and sample size.

---

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland using exclusively Icelandic resources — specimens, institutions, researchers, and computing infrastructure. The methods and pipelines are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods from Icelandic archaeological material, creating a methodological template for future work.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's role in settlement-era society was profound and multifaceted: a working mount connecting dispersed communities across Iceland's rugged landscape, a mark of social status, a ritual object in pagan sacrifice and horse-fighting, and a companion in death interred alongside warriors under strict protocol (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016; Leifsson 2018; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019). Reconstructing the founding genetics of these animals will speak directly to the public understanding of Iceland's Viking Age origins — which horse lineages the first settlers chose to bring, and what those choices reveal about where they came from.

For the Icelandic horse industry — approximately 80,000–100,000 registered horses in Iceland and more abroad [**Sunna: cite WorldFengur/FEIF statistics**] — the first historical genomic baseline will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context, clarifying when disease-associated alleles arose and whether they were present from founding or emerged later under drift or selection.

For global science, the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to conservation biology and evolutionary theory well beyond the Icelandic horse.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse has been shaped by two forces acting simultaneously for over 1,100 years: the selective pressure of a demanding environment, and the complete closure of the gene pool enforced by the Alþingi's livestock import ban. Both forces are exceptional in the history of domestic animal breeding. The breed's uniqueness is not simply that it is old — many domestic breeds have long histories — but that it is old, isolated, and completely documented. This combination makes it a rare opportunity for ancient genomics.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) established that the Botai horses of Central Asia — long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses — are in fact the ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*), using 273 ancient horse genomes [Claude comment: please verify this number against the published paper — Librado et al. 2021 used a combined dataset of modern and ancient genomes and the precise count of ancient genomes should be confirmed before submission], pinpointed the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, with rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE, synchronous with the spread of spoke-wheeled chariots. Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. Most directly relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, and establishes that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE. This is the scientific context into which the proposed project inserts the Icelandic founding horses — but none of these global studies has generated population-level data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data, confirmed that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds — a pattern fully consistent with over a millennium of gene pool closure. The most comprehensive genomic study to date (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024, *BMC Genomics*) and pedigree analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014) together establish the key parameters discussed above in Aim 2: an effective founding genome count of approximately 27, a current Ne of 125, and two detectable population crashes in the 18th and early 20th centuries. Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic stability across the 19th century using museum specimens. Critically, all of these studies work with modern horses or 19th-century material: the founding period, the medieval centuries, and the Black Death (~1402 CE) remain entirely invisible from modern genomes alone. The remarkable MHC diversity noted in Aim 3 (79 haplotypes despite the bottleneck; Maccari et al. 2019) [Claude comment: please verify '79 distinct MHC haplotypes' directly against the Maccari et al. 2019 paper before submission — this number appears in multiple sections of the draft and a wrong figure would be conspicuous to reviewers with immunogenetics expertise] adds to the picture of a breed whose genomic history cannot be inferred from living animals alone.

**Earlier research on the origins of the Icelandic horse.** Icelandic researchers have long suspected a Norwegian primary ancestry. Eldjárn (1991) proposed the Nordland/Lyngen horse as the likely founding lineage on morphological grounds, and Aðalsteinsson and Sigurðsson (1998) confirmed striking physical resemblance — including the tölt gait — through field comparisons in northern Norway. The first molecular study, Bjørnstad et al. (2000), provided genetic support: the Icelandic horse and the Shetland pony are each other's closest relatives, and both are most closely related to the Nordland/Lyngen horse. These findings, discussed in detail in Aim 1 above, constitute the best available pre-genomic picture of the founding, but are limited to modern breeds and a sparse marker set. The open question of whether British or Celtic ancestry also contributed — the subject of Aim 1 — cannot be resolved without ancient genomic data from Iceland itself.

**Ancient DNA work on Icelandic horses.** Four studies have generated molecular or genome-scale data from archaeological Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across multiple European sites, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens from the 9th–11th century but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses — a pattern they interpreted as reflecting an origin of the ambling allele in medieval England and its transport to Iceland with British-affiliated settlers, with strong statistical evidence for subsequent positive selection in Iceland (Bayes Factor = 8.68). Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts using minimal sequencing (most specimens under 350,000 mapped reads), confirming a male bias in burial horses and establishing the technical feasibility of working with Icelandic horse bone — and the critical precedent for aDNA analysis of this material. The same specimens are contextualised in the companion zooarchaeological study by Leifsson (2018), which documents riding-related skeletal lesions, butchery evidence, and the strict protocols governing who received horse burial in Viking Age Iceland — providing the cultural and social framework within which the genomic data from this project will be interpreted. Of the 22 specimens, only two — VHR031 (Granastaðir) and VHR102 (Berufjörður) — had sufficient sequencing depth (0.08× and 0.18× autosomal coverage) to be included in the global ancient horse genome survey of Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*). In that global context, phylogenetic analysis placed these two ancient Icelandic horses closest to pre-Viking Pictish horses from 6th–7th century Britain, Viking Age horses, and a 9th–10th century Estonian horse (Fages et al. 2019) — consistent with a founding stock that included British Isles ancestry alongside the primary Norwegian component, as suggested by the DMRT3 evidence. Campana et al. (2012) demonstrated DNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone. Together, these studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is tractable for aDNA analysis, and that the available evidence points towards a genetically complex founding population. What they cannot provide is the depth or temporal range required for systematic population genomic inference: the two specimens in Fages et al. (2019) were sequenced to 0.08× and 0.18× autosomal coverage without imputation — far below what is needed for ancestry modelling, demographic reconstruction, or selection scans — and both date to the Viking Age founding period alone.

**The gap and what makes it addressable now.** Two developments make this project possible at this moment. The first is methodological: the GLIMPSE2 imputation framework (Rubinacci et al. 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3× coverage, reducing sequencing cost per genome by an order of magnitude and making a 90-specimen ancient genomics project tractable within a postdoctoral fellowship budget. The second is the reference panel: Prof. Orlando's collection of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles from the Viking Age itself, provides the dense reference needed for imputation and the direct comparators needed for Aim 1. The applicant's existing aDNA protocols, developed and validated on Icelandic skeletal material over the past decade, translate directly to horse specimens from the same archaeological contexts — as already demonstrated by the five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries described above in the Feasibility section.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT, ETC.) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; all computing runs on the deCODE cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CAGT Toulouse as a contracted service under the Orlando collaboration. The principal risk — low endogenous DNA in some specimens — is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool (up to 90 specimens) and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is sufficient for robust population genomic inference using the imputation approach described below.

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10):** The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to assess available skeletal material and select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread across Iceland. Up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE will be selected; teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics, with cortical and petrous bone used where teeth are unavailable. All specimens remain at the Museum until sampled under the existing sampling permission. Silica-based DNA extraction — the protocol developed by the applicant and optimised for Icelandic skeletal material — is performed in the deCODE aDNA dedicated clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing. All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (~1 million reads per library) to assess endogenous DNA content and confirm characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends, characteristic fragmentation). Authentication is evaluated using mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al. 2013). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5× target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. The bioinformatic pipeline applies AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al. 2016), BWA mapping to the EquCab3 horse reference genome (Li and Durbin 2009), Picard MarkDuplicates, and mapDamage2 base-quality rescaling. Biological sex is estimated from the ratio of reads mapping to the X and Y chromosomes. Note on contamination estimation: the ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse specimens; contamination control for horse libraries relies on unique dual indexing, damage pattern authentication by mapDamage2, and endogenous content thresholds.

**Phase 2 — Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24):** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al. 2023) and Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. The imputation pipeline was developed and validated by the Orlando laboratory in Ahmed et al. (under review) and will run in close collaboration with CAGT Toulouse. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al. 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al. 2012) characterise founding ancestry, quantify contributions of specific Scandinavian and European source populations, and test competing models of the founding event — single introduction versus multiple waves. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis (hap-IBD; Zhou et al. 2020) tracks genealogical relationships between individuals across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC; Li and Durbin 2011; SMC++; Terhorst et al. 2017) reconstructs changes in effective population size. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (~1402 CE).

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36):** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH; Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS; Voight et al. 2006; population branch statistic PBS; Yi et al. 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 and additional gait-associated loci (STAU2, RELN; Sigurðardóttir 2025), coat colour and vision loci (ASIP, MC1R, PMEL17; Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), and the MHC region (Maccari et al. 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

## F. IF APPLICABLE, CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics — a field in which no training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1 — The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology): As described in Aim 3, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation causes both the Silver coat colour and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome, yet its history in the Icelandic population is entirely unknown. The student will track the allele's frequency through the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. The project is entirely analytical — no additional laboratory work is required — making it fully feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** (archaeology or biological anthropology): The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves — all four individuals have been sequenced in the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) — and two separate horse burials: one at the lower end of Grave 1 (a richly equipped warrior burial with sword, axes, and spearhead), and one at the northern end of Grave 4 (a second warrior burial with bridle bit and iron fittings) (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) [Claude comment: please confirm that the specific grave details — sword/axes/spearhead in Grave 1, bridle bit in Grave 4 — are correctly described from Kuml og haugfé or another source. If Sunna has the site report, that would be the stronger citation]. This makes Sílastaðir one of the most precisely documented assemblages of co-deposited human and horse remains in Iceland. The student will examine whether the two horses are genetically related, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the human interred alongside it (the protocols governing who received horse burial are documented in Leifsson 2018), and whether the Sílastaðir horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Six months of salary support is included in this budget. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit for Icelandic science: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills directly applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.
