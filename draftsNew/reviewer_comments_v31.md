# Reviewer Comments — Rannís Draft v31
# Bred by Vikings: the genomic history of the Icelandic horse through isolation

*These are internal reviewer-style comments on v31 before submission. Comments are numbered for easy reference and organised by section. Issues marked [MINOR] are small fixes; [MODERATE] need a paragraph-level revision; [MAJOR] need a structural or substantive change.*

---

## General — before reading individual sections

**G1 [MAJOR] Page limit.** The IRF Project Grant allows 12 pages of text (15 total including title and guidance pages). The current draft would run well over that. Significant trimming is needed across all sections before submission. Section B is the most likely target; Section A (Aims) also contains several passages that could be tightened.

**G2 [MODERATE] Ahmed et al. is still "under review".** This paper is cited repeatedly and carries a lot of weight — it is the source of the NORD/BRIT framing, the 376 ancient genomes figure, and the 810 CE date. By submission it should ideally be published or at least have a preprint DOI. If it is still under review at submission, a reviewer will ask: what happens to the framing if it does not pass peer review or is substantially revised? Consider adding a single sentence noting that the key findings cited here are independently corroborated by Fages et al. (2019) and Petersen et al. (2013).

**G3 [MODERATE] Ebenesersdóttir et al. in preparation.** Cited in multiple places as evidence of infrastructure and shared sites. By submission this should either have been posted as a preprint or should be described with a title and approximate completion date. Reviewers will ask how firm this "in preparation" is.

**G4 [MINOR] Open items throughout the text.** All 19 open items must be resolved and the bracket notes removed before uploading. Several are also scientifically material (e.g. Open Item 18 on horse height, Open Item 9 on the Campana DOI), not just administrative.

**G5 [MINOR] "SSE" and "AH" abbreviations.** These appear in the pre-submission checklist and budget but not consistently in the project description sections. Section D uses full names throughout, which is correct for the submission text. Make sure no abbreviations slip through in the final version.

## Section A — Specific aims, feasibility, originality, impact

**A1 [MINOR] Opening sentence — import ban phrasing.** "since at the Commonwealth era, or even earlier, importation has been forbidden" is hedged in an awkward way. Either commit to a more specific formulation ("from at least the Commonwealth period, around 930 CE") or cite both the early law code and the modern act without the hedge. The current wording invites a reviewer to ask what the actual date is.

**A2 [MINOR] Strontium paragraph — repeated phrase.** The paragraph contains the phrase "which horses were physically transported to Iceland and which were born there" twice: once in the opening sentence and once in the middle sentence beginning "For settlement-era specimens, strontium data paired with..." Cut one. The middle sentence starting "For settlement-era specimens..." is the stronger one; the opening sentence can end after "which were born there" (first occurrence only).

**A3 [MODERATE] Human parallel cited twice in close succession.** Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science) is cited in the Aim 1 rationale paragraph (for settler ancestry) and again immediately in the strontium paragraph (as the precedent for the method). This creates slight redundancy. Consider whether the human ancestry parallel and the strontium precedent can be folded into one economical reference rather than appearing as two separate points that both invoke the same paper.

**A4 [MODERATE] Aim 2 — what does the ARG actually add beyond allele frequency tracking?** The text states that the ARG makes questions "directly answerable" that standard methods can only estimate, and gives as the first example: "when did *DMRT3* reach fixation?" But fixation timing for a single biallelic variant can be determined from allele frequency trajectories through the temporal dataset without needing an ARG at all. The genuine added value of the ARG is for events that require genealogical resolution — identifying founder lines, dating admixture at the level of individual haplotypes, and detecting sweep signatures with higher resolution than population-level statistics. Reframe the examples so they illustrate what the ARG specifically enables, not what allele frequency tracking already provides.

**A5 [MODERATE] Aim 2 — confidence and feasibility claim.** "even a partially resolved ARG covering the post-founding period would be the first for any domestic animal anchored to a verified pedigree database" is a strong claim. Before submission, verify this is true. ARG methods have been applied to several livestock species and studbook cattle; the novelty claim may need qualification. "To our knowledge" is a safe hedge if you cannot do an exhaustive literature search before submitting.

**A6 [MODERATE] Aim 3 — data generation in the aims section.** The first paragraph of Aim 3 is primarily about generating present-day horse sequences rather than framing the scientific question. Data generation belongs in the Feasibility subsection or Section C; the Aims section should stay focused on questions and hypotheses. Consider moving the paragraph beginning "Whole-genome sequences will be generated for present-day Icelandic horses..." to the Feasibility subsection and replacing it with a brief statement of the demographic hypotheses being tested.

**A7 [MODERATE] Aim 4 — scope.** Six traits tracked across three phases is ambitious for a single aim. A reviewer will wonder whether the project can genuinely deliver on all six. Consider whether body size and metabolic disease can be merged into a single "thrifty genotype" sub-aim (both are primarily HMGA2/FOXO1), and whether gait and the PMEL17 vision locus can similarly be grouped under "trait genomics" vs. "health genomics." Presenting six separate trait histories may invite the critique that the project is too scattered.

**A8 [MINOR] HMGA2 — double role.** *HMGA2* appears in the body size sub-aim and again as a candidate for metabolic disease. This is correct scientifically (the locus has pleiotropy), but it is not clearly flagged as the same locus serving both phenotypes. Add a cross-reference: "HMGA2, which also underlies body size differences in ponies (Norton et al., 2019; Aim 4, body size)" so the reviewer sees the connection rather than wondering if the same locus is being counted twice.

**A9 [MINOR] Y chromosome source — Weinzerl 2024.** A diploma thesis from the University of Veterinary Medicine Vienna is the sole source for the claim that "Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes." This is a strong claim. Does it appear in a peer-reviewed publication? If not, either add a caveat or find a peer-reviewed source that corroborates it. Reviewers will notice that a diploma thesis is the only support for a factual claim in the aims section.

**A10 [MODERATE] Feasibility — GLIMPSE2 on horse material at 3.6×.** The text states that GLIMPSE2 has "reduced the sequencing requirement dramatically" and cites the UK Biobank human paper (Rubinacci et al., 2023). The co-proposer's pilot yielded a mean depth of 3.6×, which is adequate. However, GLIMPSE2's performance depends heavily on the quality and size of the reference panel and on the effective population size of the target. Reviewer will ask: has GLIMPSE2 specifically been validated on low-coverage ancient horse data against Orlando's panel? Ahmed et al. (under review) is cited as evidence that "the imputation pipeline works on horse material," but a brief explicit statement of what coverage the Ahmed et al. ancient genomes were imputed at, and what the concordance rate was, would strengthen this claim considerably.

**A11 [MODERATE] Originality — comparison with other ancient animal genomic datasets.** The claim that the dataset "has almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics" is plausible but needs a brief acknowledgement of the most comparable existing datasets (e.g. Roman-period cattle, ancient dog series from the British Isles, pig genomics through time) and a sentence explaining why the Icelandic horse case is still superior for the specific questions being asked. Without this, a reviewer who works on ancient animal genomics will push back.

**A12 [MINOR] Impact — "31 countries" repeated.** The phrase "31 countries" appears twice in close proximity: once in the sentence about sweet itch affecting exported horses, and once in the sentence about FEIF and WorldFengur. Vary the phrasing in one occurrence.

**A13 [MODERATE] Impact — British native breed conservation argument.** The FLAG in the text correctly identifies the problem: the conservation argument for British native breeds rests on Wutke (DMRT3) and Fages (ancient genome clustering) evidence, not on the Icelandic horse carrying the BRIT component. As currently written, the argument is: "Ahmed et al. identified BRIT in Dartmoor and Exmoor; the Icelandic horse may therefore carry British-origin variation that is now absent on the mainland." But if the Icelandic horse is NORD, not BRIT, this argument needs to be reconstructed from its foundations before submission. The relevant question is: does the Icelandic horse carry any haplotypes of British Isles origin that are absent or rare in modern British native breeds? This could be framed around Wutke's DMRT3 finding (the allele was common in early Iceland and in Jorvik horses but rare in contemporary Scandinavia) and around the ancient genome clustering in Fages — but the BRIT component itself is not the right hook. Either restructure the argument on that basis or cut it and rest the conservation angle on the IBH/MHC diversity points, which are more solid.

## Section B — Present state of knowledge

**B1 [MINOR] Generic opening sentence.** "Ancient DNA research has greatly expanded knowledge of horse evolution over the past decade" is a very generic way to open a section that is meant to position the project in its field. Consider opening with a more specific statement that orients the reader immediately — e.g. where the field was before 2018 (microsatellites, single-locus genotyping) and what the key papers from 2018–2024 established.

**B2 [MODERATE] NORD/BRIT passage — logical flow needs one bridging sentence.** The current passage reads: "Ahmed et al. characterise the Icelandic horse as defined by the NORD ancestry component... The native British breeds that cluster most closely with the Icelandic horse — Dartmoor and Exmoor — are characterised by the BRIT ancestry component... SNP-based clustering places the Icelandic horse in a shared clade with these breeds (Petersen et al., 2013)." A reviewer will find this confusing: you have just said that Icelandic horses are NORD and Dartmoor/Exmoor are BRIT (different components), and then immediately say they cluster together in SNP analysis. These two statements are not contradictory but the connection is not explained. Add one sentence clarifying that NORD and BRIT are defined within the Ahmed et al. framework from a specific set of ancient comparators, whereas broader SNP-based clustering in Petersen et al. captures a deeper shared ancestry that predates the NORD/BRIT divergence. Without this bridge the passage reads as self-contradictory.

**B3 [MAJOR] Section B diversity paragraph — too dense.** The paragraph beginning "On diversity and population history..." runs to approximately 400 words and covers at least six distinct topics: SNP comparison findings, genome space positioning, Sigurðardóttir et al. Ne and bottlenecks, IBH susceptibility, MHC diversity, 670K array design, and pedigree analysis. This is too much for one paragraph and will exhaust a reviewer before they reach the end. Split into at least two paragraphs: (1) genetic structure and diversity from modern data, including the two bottlenecks and Ne estimates; (2) trait-associated and disease loci (DMRT3, HMGA2, FOXO1, IBH, PMEL17). A paragraph break after "remains largely unknown" or after the FOXO1/HMGA2 sentence would help.

**B4 [MINOR] Bjørnstad et al. (2003) — weight of evidence.** This microsatellite paper is more than 20 years old and is the primary evidence for the Nordland/Lyngen relationship. It should be presented as historical corroboration that is now superseded by genomic evidence (Fages, Ahmed), not as a co-equal line of evidence. A reviewer may note that microsatellite phylogenetics is a weak foundation compared to the genomic evidence that follows.

**B5 [MINOR] 670K array design breeds.** The text states "the Icelandic horse was not among the design breeds." This was flagged as an open item in an earlier version and then resolved in v25 (note says the design breeds are confirmed in Schaefer et al. 2017). Confirm this claim is accurate before submission — if the Icelandic horse was in fact included in the design, the sentence must be removed.

**B6 [MINOR] "These chip data have not yet been analysed in the context of global horse variation or ancient horse genomes."** This is a strong claim. Sigurðardóttir et al. (2024) did analyse these data in the context of other breeds, including Exmoor. Check whether this sentence accurately characterises what has and has not been done, and narrow it if necessary to "have not been analysed together with the ancient genome time series developed in Ahmed et al."

## Section C — Research plan

**C1 [MAJOR] Milestone table inconsistency with 4-paper structure.** The milestone table was not updated when the paper plan was expanded from 2 to 4 articles. M6 says "First manuscript submitted" and M8 says "Both manuscripts submitted" — but Section E now describes four articles with submission targets at months 27, 30, 32, and 34. This is a clear internal inconsistency that any reviewer will catch. The table must be revised to reflect the 4-paper plan. At minimum: M6 should reference Article 1 only; add intermediate milestones for Articles 2–3; M8 should reflect submission of the final article and the master's student paper, or be explicit that Articles 3 and 4 are submitted after month 36.

**C2 [MODERATE] PSMC for low-coverage ancient genomes.** Phase 3 mentions "effective population size is reconstructed using PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++." PSMC requires a single high-coverage genome (typically >10×); it does not work well on imputed low-coverage genomes. For the ancient specimens at 2–5×, PSMC is not appropriate. SMC++ can use multiple lower-coverage individuals and is more suitable. Either remove PSMC from the ancient dataset analysis and reserve it for the high-coverage modern sequences only, or clarify which tool applies to which part of the dataset. A computational reviewer will flag this immediately.

**C3 [MINOR] Relate vs tsinfer.** Phase 2 states "Aim 2 constructs the ancestral recombination graph using Relate and tsinfer." Relate and tsinfer have different input requirements, assumptions, and outputs. Relate works from phased sequence data with a known demographic model; tsinfer builds a tree sequence from unphased or phased data without requiring a demographic model. Both can incorporate ancient samples. A sentence clarifying how the two tools are used together (e.g. tsinfer for initial topology, Relate for branch length dating) would prevent a reviewer from wondering whether you actually know what you're doing with these methods or have just listed two ARG tools.

**C4 [MINOR] GLOBE Institute listed as sequencing fallback.** Section C states sequencing fallback is "the GLOBE Institute, University of Copenhagen." In Section D, Tom Gilbert is at GLOBE. This is fine if he has confirmed this, but it should match the language in Section D. Confirm that Gilbert/GLOBE has specifically confirmed sequencing capacity, not just computational support.

## Section E — Publication and data storage

**E1 [MAJOR] Internally contradictory stance on 4 papers.** The passage "Publishing four papers in 36 months is ambitious; Articles 1 and 3 cover closely related questions and could be combined if needed, without losing the core scientific content" immediately undercuts the 4-paper proposal. A reviewer will read this as: the applicants themselves are not confident in the 4-paper plan. Either defend 4 papers and remove the hedge, or commit to 3 papers. The hedge makes the application look uncertain. If you keep 4 papers, justify why they are separable (ARG as a methods paper in a different venue; trait paper as a different audience). If you commit to 3, combine Articles 1 and 3 from the start.

**E2 [MINOR] "section f" — capitalisation.** "see section f" should be "see Section F" for consistency with the rest of the document.

**E3 [MINOR] Article submission timeline vs milestone table.** Article 1 is targeted for Month 27, Article 2 for Month 30, Articles 3 and 4 for Months 32 and 34. The milestone table at the end of Section C must reflect this. Currently it does not (see C1 above).

## Section F — Master's students

**F1 [MODERATE] Six options — consider trimming.** Six project options is generous but may signal to a reviewer that the project is not well enough focused to identify the single best Master's student project. Consider presenting three or four strongest options, noting that others are available depending on the student's background. Options 1 (PMEL17), 2 (paired human/horse at Brimnes), and 3 (osteometric body size) are the most distinctive and best matched to different disciplinary backgrounds. Options 4, 5, and 6 all require bioinformatics and could be condensed or described more briefly.

**F2 [MINOR] Option 2 — "eight associated horse burials exist."** No source is given for this number. Cite the source (Leifsson 2018, Eldjárn & Friðriksson 2016, or the sampling list) so a reviewer can verify the claim.
