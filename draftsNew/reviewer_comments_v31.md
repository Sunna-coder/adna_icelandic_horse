If a preprint exists, add the DOI now.

**G3 [MODERATE] Ebenesersdóttir et al. in preparation.** Cited in several places as evidence of infrastructure and shared sites. By submission this should either have a preprint DOI or a title and approximate completion date. "In preparation" alone will invite reviewer scepticism.

## Section A — Specific aims, feasibility, originality, impact

**A1 RESOLVED.** Import ban phrasing is now clean (Act citation in parentheses, no awkward hedge).

**A2 [MINOR] Strontium paragraph — phrase repeated.** Line 107: "which horses were physically transported to Iceland and which were born there" appears in the first sentence and then again in almost identical form ("which horses were physically transported to Iceland and which were bred there") two sentences later. Cut the first occurrence and let the paragraph open differently.

**A3 [MODERATE] 150 specimens stated twice in close succession.** The opening paragraph and Aim 1 (line 103) both state "approximately 150 Icelandic specimens spanning 870–1900 CE." Since Aim 1 comes immediately after the opening, the repeat is noticeable. In Aim 1, replace with "modelling genome-wide ancient DNA from the Icelandic specimens against modern horses from more than 100 breeds and ancient specimens from Britain and Scandinavia spanning the Viking Age."

**A4 [MODERATE] Human parallel cited twice in close succession.** Ebenesersdóttir et al. (2018, Science) is cited at the start of Aim 1 (settler ancestry) and again two paragraphs later (strontium precedent). Consider whether both uses need the full citation or whether one can be an in-text cross-reference.

**A5 [MODERATE] Aim 2 — what does the ARG add beyond allele frequency tracking?** "When did DMRT3 reach fixation?" can be answered from allele frequency trajectories through the temporal dataset without needing an ARG. The genuine added value of the ARG is for events requiring genealogical resolution: identifying founder lines carrying specific haplotypes, dating admixture at the level of individual tracts, detecting sweep signatures with finer resolution than population statistics. Reframe the examples to illustrate what the ARG specifically enables.

**A6 [MODERATE] Aim 2 novelty claim.** "Even a partially resolved ARG covering the post-founding period would be the first for any domestic animal anchored to a verified pedigree database" is a strong claim. Add "To our knowledge" as a hedge, or verify that no ARG study has been published for studbook cattle or other livestock before submission.

**A7 [MODERATE] Aim 3 — data generation in the aims section.** The first paragraph of Aim 3 describes generating present-day horse sequences rather than framing a scientific question. Data generation belongs in Feasibility or Section C. Replace with a brief statement of the demographic hypotheses being tested and move the sequencing detail.

**A8 [MODERATE] Aim 4 — scope.** Six traits is ambitious. A reviewer may question deliverability. Body size and metabolic disease both centre on HMGA2/FOXO1 and could be grouped as a "thrifty genotype" sub-aim. Gait and vision (PMEL17) could be grouped under "trait genomics." This reduces apparent scatter without losing content.

**A9 [MINOR] HMGA2 — double role not flagged.** HMGA2 appears in both body size and metabolic disease sub-aims without a cross-reference. Add a note such as "(also tracked for body size above)" in the metabolic disease sub-aim so a reviewer does not wonder if the same locus is being counted twice.

**A10 [MINOR] Weinzerl 2024 — diploma thesis as sole source.** The claim that "Present-day Icelandic stallions carry only Icelandic-specific Y-chromosomal haplotypes" rests on a diploma thesis from the University of Veterinary Medicine Vienna. Either find a peer-reviewed corroboration or add a caveat. Reviewers will notice.

**A11 [MODERATE] GLIMPSE2 — validation on horse data not demonstrated.** The text states GLIMPSE2 dramatically reduces coverage requirements and cites the UK Biobank human paper. Ahmed et al. is cited as evidence "the imputation pipeline works on horse material," but a brief explicit statement — what coverage the Ahmed et al. ancient genomes were imputed at and what the concordance rate was — would substantially strengthen this feasibility claim.

**A12 [MODERATE] Originality — no comparator to other ancient animal datasets.** "Almost no equivalent in ancient animal genomics" is plausible but needs brief acknowledgement of the most comparable datasets (e.g. Roman cattle, ancient dog time series from the British Isles, pig genomics). One sentence explaining why the Icelandic horse still surpasses these for the specific questions asked will pre-empt pushback from reviewers who work on ancient animal genomics.

**A13 [MINOR] "31 countries" repeated.** Lines 169: "breeders and breed organisations across 31 countries" and then "health records for horses across 31 countries" appear in the same paragraph. Vary the second occurrence.

**A14 [MODERATE] British native breed conservation argument.** The conservation argument rests on the Icelandic horse preserving British-origin haplotypes now lost on the mainland. This is supported by Wutke (DMRT3) and Fages (ancient genome clustering), but the BRIT ancestry component (from Ahmed et al.) belongs to Dartmoor/Exmoor, not the Icelandic horse. If citing Ahmed et al. in this context, be careful not to imply the Icelandic horse carries BRIT ancestry — it carries NORD. Frame the argument strictly around the Wutke and Fages evidence, which is solid.

## Section B — Present state of knowledge

**B1 [MINOR] Section B second paragraph opens generically.** "Ancient DNA research has greatly expanded knowledge of horse evolution over the past decade" is a very generic opener. Consider replacing with a more specific statement of where the field stood before 2018 and what the key advances since then have been.

**B2 [MODERATE] NORD/BRIT — logical gap.** The current passage says Icelandic horses are defined by NORD ancestry and Dartmoor/Exmoor by BRIT (two distinct components), then immediately says SNP-based clustering (Petersen et al., 2013) places them in a shared clade. A reviewer will find this contradictory without a bridging sentence explaining that NORD and BRIT are defined within the Ahmed et al. framework from specific ancient comparators, while the Petersen et al. clustering reflects a deeper, pre-divergence shared ancestry.

**B3 [MAJOR] Paragraph at line 189 — too dense.** This paragraph covers SNP comparisons, global genomic positioning, Ne and bottlenecks (Sigurðardóttir et al.), IBH susceptibility, MHC diversity, 670K array design, pedigree analysis, and Y-chromosome evidence — roughly 450 words. Split after the FOXO1/HMGA2 sentences: (1) genetic structure, diversity, and demography; (2) trait and disease loci plus pedigree. A paragraph break will prevent reviewer fatigue.

**B4 [MINOR] Bjørnstad et al. (2003).** A 20-year-old microsatellite study should be framed as historical evidence now superseded by genomic data, not as a co-equal line of evidence. A reviewer may note the weakness of microsatellite phylogenetics relative to what follows.

**B5 [MINOR] 670K array — Icelandic horse not among design breeds.** Confirm this is accurate before submission. If Schaefer et al. (2017) included Icelandic horses in the design, this sentence must be removed.

## Section C — Research plan

**C1 [MAJOR] Milestone table inconsistent with four-paper plan.** M6 says "First manuscript submitted" and M8 says "Both manuscripts submitted" — but Section E now lists four articles with submission targets at months 27, 30, 32, and 34. This is a direct contradiction that any reviewer will catch. The table must be updated to reflect four papers. At minimum: rename M6 to reflect Article 1 only; add intermediate milestones for Articles 2 and 3; clarify that Article 4 is submitted after month 34 (i.e. slightly beyond the 36-month reporting period, or that M8 covers only final data deposit and the report).

**C2 [MODERATE] PSMC inappropriate for low-coverage ancient genomes.** Phase 2 (line 205) lists "PSMC (Li & Durbin, 2011) and SMC++" for effective population size. PSMC requires a single high-coverage genome (typically >10×) and does not work on imputed low-coverage genomes. For ancient specimens at 2–5×, PSMC is not appropriate. Reserve PSMC for the high-coverage modern sequences only and use SMC++ for the ancient dataset. A computational reviewer will flag this immediately.

**C3 [MINOR] Relate vs tsinfer — distinction not explained.** Relate and tsinfer have different input requirements and outputs. Add one sentence clarifying how they are used together (e.g. tsinfer for tree sequence topology, Relate for branch length dating) so a reviewer does not wonder whether you know the difference.

**C4 [MINOR] GLOBE Institute as sequencing fallback.** Confirm Tom Gilbert/GLOBE has specifically confirmed sequencing capacity (not just computational support) before listing this as a fallback. Language in Section C and Section D should be consistent.

**C5 [MINOR] 0.1× coverage threshold.** Line 201 states "at least 100 imputed genomes at 0.1x coverage, which is adequate for population-genomic analyses using GLIMPSE2." GLIMPSE2 can work at 0.1× but performance depends heavily on reference panel quality. Verify this threshold is defensible for the Orlando horse panel specifically, or revise to "at least 1× coverage" which is the more commonly cited minimum.
