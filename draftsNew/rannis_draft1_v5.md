## A. SPECIFIC AIMS OF THE PROJECT, RESEARCH QUESTIONS/HYPOTHESES, FEASIBILITY, ORIGINALITY AND IMPACT

The Icelandic horse has been separated from every other horse population on earth for over 1,100 years. Brought to Iceland by Norse settlers in the late 9th century CE — an event documented in the medieval sagas and confirmed in the archaeological record — the breed has remained in almost complete genomic isolation ever since. Around 982 CE, the Alþingi enacted one of the world's oldest continuously enforced livestock import bans, prohibiting the introduction of new horses to Iceland [**Sunna: citation needed — Grágás or the standard legal history reference used in your National Museum proposal**]. Any horse that leaves cannot return. The result, maintained without interruption from the Viking Age to the present day, is a domestic animal whose entire post-settlement history is, in principle, traceable through its own genome.

That history has never been investigated. No genome-wide ancient DNA data exist for the Icelandic horse — or for any horse population from the North Atlantic. We do not know which Scandinavian or European populations contributed to the founding stock, whether the breed was established through a single colonisation or multiple introductions, how the population responded to Iceland's recurring famines and livestock epidemics, or how the breed's most celebrated traits — its unique gaits, its hardiness, its disease profile — evolved under more than a millennium of isolation. These are not merely academic questions. The Icelandic horse is a living national symbol, an economic resource, and a model biological system of unusual scientific clarity. This project will generate the first comprehensive ancient genomic dataset for the Icelandic horse, using up to 90 archaeological specimens from the National Museum of Iceland spanning 870–1900 CE, and address three specific scientific aims.

**Aim 1 — Origins and founding:** What was the genomic composition of the horses that arrived with Norse settlers? Were they introduced through a single founding event or through multiple waves? What populations contributed to the founding stock, and in what proportions?

The identity of the founders is a genuinely open question. The Norse expansion of the 9th–10th centuries CE involved the deliberate transport of livestock across the North Atlantic, and many of Iceland's settlers did not depart directly from Scandinavia — they came from Norse communities that had lived for a generation or more alongside Celtic-speaking people in Orkney, the Hebrides, and Ireland. The companion ancient human genomics study of Iceland (Ebenesersdóttir et al. 2018; in preparation) shows that early Icelanders were a mixture of Norse and Gaelic ancestry in proportions of approximately 0.57 : 0.43 at the time of settlement, with the Norse component drawn disproportionately from southern and western Norway and the Gaelic component pointing primarily to Ireland and Scotland. Wutke et al. (2016) found that Viking Age settlers in Britain and Ireland were already breeding horses with local Celtic stock before departing for Iceland — making the founding Icelandic population a likely mosaic of Scandinavian and British-Celtic ancestry. If the people were mixed Norse-Gaelic, the horses very plausibly were too. The physical resemblance of the Icelandic horse to native Atlantic island breeds — small stature, extreme hardiness, dense coat — has long been taken to suggest a significant Celtic ancestry contribution, sometimes called the "Celtic pony hypothesis" [**Sunna: you may wish to cite Bjørnstad et al. 2000 or the Stelter 2014 thesis here, or simply leave as a hypothesis without a specific citation**]. The indirect genomic evidence is suggestive: Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) found the DMRT3 ambling mutation — which they trace to medieval England (850–900 CE) — already present in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic horses but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian specimens, consistent with a significant British Isles contribution to the founding stock. Ahmed et al. (under review), co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando, established that the NORD ancestry component characterising modern Icelandic horses emerged around 810 CE, but this does not resolve the question of Celtic or British contributions to the founding stock, or in what proportions.

This project will answer that question directly, using genome-wide data rather than a single locus and comparing Icelandic founding horses against a reference panel that includes ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the Viking Age itself — horses contemporary with Iceland's founders, not modern breeds that have themselves changed in the intervening millennium. [**Comment 8 addressed: the Orlando reference panel includes ancient Scandinavian and British specimens from the relevant period, so comparison is against the right historical reference, not modern descendants. This is why ancient reference samples are essential — modern Norwegian and British breeds are not representative of what horses looked like in 870 CE.**]

**Aim 2 — Demographic history under isolation:** How did genetic diversity, inbreeding, and effective population size change over 1,100 years of geographic isolation? Did Iceland's major historical crises leave detectable signatures in the genomic record?

Modern genomics has already detected two population crashes in the genomes of living Icelandic horses: one corresponding to the Laki/Skaftáreldar eruption of 1783–1784 (an approximately 75% reduction in horse numbers, visible approximately 23 generations ago in the modern genome) and one to the industrialisation of transport in the early 20th century — with the current genomic effective population size estimated at just 125 individuals (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024). But the genomes of living horses cannot reach back further than the 18th century. What happened during the founding period, the medieval centuries, and the Black Death (~1402 CE) — all periods with precise documentary records — remains invisible to modern genomics alone.

Only a temporal ancient genomic dataset can trace the full trajectory. The value of the Icelandic case for this question is exceptional: unlike most ancient genomics projects, every major demographic event is documented in writing, with calendar dates and, in many cases, quantitative records of livestock losses. That means the genomic signal and the historical record can be directly compared — an opportunity that is almost never available in ancient population genomics. Among the most scientifically significant specimens in the collection are several from the Kjalvegur highland route dated to approximately 1780 CE, which represent horses that died during or immediately after the Laki famine. These specimens will allow direct genomic observation of the population just before and after the most catastrophic demographic event in the breed's documented history. [**Comment 6 addressed: the post-medieval time period contains fewer specimens, but the purpose of those specimens is not to characterise a stable population — it is to capture specific historical moments, particularly the Laki famine. Even a small number of individuals from this period, anchored to a precisely dated event, can document genomic change when compared against the earlier time series. Power comes from the temporal calibration, not from sample size within a single period.**]

**Aim 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution:** What genomic signatures of selection are detectable in ancient and historical Icelandic horses? How did the breed's most distinctive traits evolve under isolation?

Three sets of traits are targeted, each with a well-characterised genomic basis that can be tracked directly through the temporal dataset. For the tölt and flying pace, the DMRT3 premature stop codon on ECA23 is the primary locus (Andersson et al. 2012); it is the dominant region of homozygosity in modern Icelandic horses (present as an ROH island in >90% of individuals; Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024) and was already at 77% frequency in the 9th–11th century founding population (Wutke et al. 2016). The question this project can answer — when did the allele reach fixation, and how rapidly? — tells us whether breeders were already selecting for gait from the outset or whether fixation occurred gradually over centuries. Beyond DMRT3, recent work has identified additional QTLs contributing to tölt quality and pace containing STAU2 and RELN — genes expressed in neural tissue — indicating that the genetic architecture of gait is more complex than a single locus (Sigurðardóttir 2025). The temporal dataset will allow tracking of these additional loci as well.

For coat colour and vision, the PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation responsible for both the Silver phenotype and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies syndrome (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011) has an entirely unknown history in the Icelandic population. Whether this allele was present from founding or emerged later is a question no previous study has addressed. For immune function and disease, the sweet itch GWAS loci (ECA1, ECA3, ECA15, ECA18; Shrestha et al. 2015) and the MHC region (ECA20; Schurink et al. 2012) offer a window into how immune diversity was maintained through isolation. Despite the population's bottleneck history, Icelandic horses retain 79 distinct MHC haplotypes — the major histocompatibility complex encodes the proteins responsible for immune recognition and pathogen defence, and this region is one of the most variable in vertebrate genomes — a pattern attributed to a recombination hotspot mechanism (Maccari et al. 2019). The temporal dataset will reveal whether this diversity was present from founding or was sustained by balancing selection, and whether susceptibility alleles for sweet itch rose through drift or were under selection.

Aim 3 tracks specific, pre-identified genetic variants rather than attempting discovery GWAS on a new dataset — a distinction that is important for feasibility at this sample size and that directly connects the ancient genomic findings to the breed's documented health and performance concerns.

**Feasibility** rests on four pillars of prior work that together eliminate the main risks of this kind of project. The most fundamental risk in ancient DNA research is preservation failure. Here, that risk is largely already resolved. The aDNA extraction and sequencing protocols used in this project were developed by the applicant at deCODE Genetics and applied at scale to 27 ancient Icelandic human individuals (Ebenesersdóttir et al. *Science* 2018), including specimens over 1,100 years old. An expanded study of 201 ancient Icelandic human genomes (manuscript in preparation) has further confirmed their reliability and scalability on exactly this type of material. The same protocols have since been applied directly to Icelandic horse bone, with all five specimens tested yielding endogenous horse DNA at 10–40% — well above even the more conservative 5% quality threshold, and far exceeding the minimum required for GLIMPSE2 imputation-based analysis [**Comment 3 addressed: at 10–40% endogenous DNA, all five specimens substantially exceed both the 1% minimum and the 5% high-quality threshold**]. **Originality.** Previous studies of the Icelandic horse have used mitochondrial DNA (Campana et al. 2012; Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014), single candidate loci (Wutke et al. 2016 — DMRT3; Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019 — sex-typing), or modern SNP arrays (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024; Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014). This project is the first to generate whole-genome-scale ancient DNA data for the Icelandic horse, and the first to extend any genomic analysis of the breed back to the founding period. Its originality operates on four levels.

Geographically, the entire North Atlantic remains a blank on the ancient horse genomic map. Ahmed et al. (under review) establishes that a founding NORD ancestry component was already present by approximately 810 CE — but it does not address what happened to that founding stock once it arrived in Iceland, how it diversified or contracted over the following millennium, or which specific populations contributed to it. This project will trace the full post-arrival history for the first time.

Temporally, a dataset spanning 1,100 years from a single, genetically isolated population with a completely documented historical record is almost without parallel in ancient population genomics. Most ancient genomic studies work with fragmentary sampling across large geographic areas and poorly documented timelines; here, the genomic time series can be directly calibrated against precisely dated historical events, creating a resolution that is rarely achievable.

As a model system, the Icelandic horse combines all the properties needed to address fundamental questions in evolutionary biology and conservation genetics — how do founder effects shape long-term genetic architecture, how does sustained inbreeding distribute deleterious variants, what is the relationship between phenotypic selection and genomic change in a closed breeding population. Island populations consistently show reduced genetic variation relative to their mainland source populations (Frankham 1997); the Icelandic horse is an extreme case of this pattern, and this project will trace it empirically across more than a millennium.

Finally, the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al. in preparation) has generated genome-wide data for 201 ancient Icelandic individuals from 58 sites across Iceland, at least 17 of which [**Sunna: verify this number against the cross-reference Excel file before submission**] also contain horse remains targeted in this project. This creates paired human–horse genomic data from individual Viking Age burial events — a resource without parallel in the current ancient genomics literature at this temporal depth and sample size [**Sunna: if you are concerned about overclaiming, soften to "at this temporal depth and sample size"**].

**Impact** will be felt across multiple communities. For Icelandic science, this project establishes ancient animal genomics as an active field in Iceland, using exclusively Icelandic resources — specimens, institutions, researchers, and computing infrastructure. The methods, bioinformatic pipelines, and reference data generated here are directly transferable to other Icelandic archaeological species — cattle, sheep, and dogs — none of which have yet been studied using whole-genome ancient DNA methods applied to Icelandic archaeological material, creating a methodological template for future work across Iceland's bioarchaeological collections.

For Icelandic cultural heritage, the horse's role in settlement-era society — as a working animal, a mark of social status, and a companion in death, documented systematically in the foundational catalogue of Viking Age burials (Eldjárn and Friðriksson 2016) and by molecular zooarchaeology (Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. 2019) — means that reconstructing the founding of the breed will speak directly to the public understanding of Iceland's Viking Age origins.

For the Icelandic horse industry, with approximately 80,000–100,000 registered horses worldwide [**Sunna: cite WorldFengur/FEIF statistics**], the first historical genomic baseline for the breed will allow breeders and veterinarians to place current health and diversity data in proper historical context — clarifying when disease-associated alleles such as those for sweet itch and MCOA arose and whether they were present from founding or emerged later under drift or selection.

For global science, the methodological advances in imputation-based ancient genomics demonstrated here are applicable to any non-human ancient genomics project, and the empirical findings on founder effects, long-term inbreeding, and adaptation under isolation are relevant to conservation biology and evolutionary theory well beyond the Icelandic horse.

## B. PRESENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE IN THE FIELD

The Icelandic horse has been shaped by two forces acting simultaneously for over 1,100 years: the selective pressure of a demanding environment, and the complete closure of the gene pool enforced by the Alþingi's livestock import ban. Both forces are exceptional in the history of domestic animal breeding. The breed's uniqueness is not simply that it is old — many domestic breeds have long histories — but that it is old, isolated, and completely documented. This combination makes it a rare opportunity for ancient genomics.

**The broader field.** Ancient horse genomics has transformed our understanding of the species over the past decade. Gaunitz et al. (2018, *Science*) established that the Botai horses of Central Asia — long assumed to be the ancestors of modern domestic horses — are in fact the ancestors of modern Przewalski's horses, forcing a complete revision of domestication origins. Librado et al. (2021, *Nature*), using 273 ancient horse genomes, pinpointed the lower Volga-Don region of the Western Eurasian steppes as the primary source of modern domestic horse ancestry, with rapid expansion across Eurasia from approximately 2000 BCE, synchronous with the spread of spoke-wheeled chariots (Warmuth et al. 2012 reached a similar conclusion from microsatellite data). Fages et al. (2019, *Cell*) assembled an extensive ancient genome time series covering five millennia, documenting two now-extinct lineages and a substantial loss of genetic diversity coinciding with modern breed formation. Most directly relevant to this proposal, Ahmed et al. (under review, co-authored by the applicant and Prof. Orlando) characterises seven major genetic ancestry components shaping modern horse breeds from a dataset of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, and establishes that the NORD ancestry component defining Icelandic horses emerged at approximately 810 CE. This is the scientific context into which the proposed project inserts the Icelandic founding horses — but none of these global studies has generated data from Iceland itself, or from any other North Atlantic horse population.

**What is known about the Icelandic horse.** The genetics of the modern breed has been examined through several complementary approaches. Petersen et al. (2013, *PLOS ONE*), in a large international comparison of horse diversity using genome-wide SNP data, confirmed that Icelandic horses are among the most genetically distinct of all modern breeds, with low within-breed heterozygosity consistent with their closed population history. The most comprehensive modern genomic study (Sigurðardóttir et al. 2024, *BMC Genomics*) used a 670K SNP array on 380 Icelandic horses and found a current genomic effective population size of 125 individuals — strikingly low, but consistent with the breed's long history of isolation — and identified two Ne crashes detectable in the genomes of living horses: approximately 75% corresponding to the Laki eruption of 1783–1784 (~23 generations ago) and a second crash associated with the early 20th-century decline in demand for working horses. This same study identified DMRT3 on ECA23 as the dominant region of homozygosity in the modern breed (present as an ROH island in >90% of individuals), the clearest genomic signature of sustained artificial selection. Pedigree-based analysis (Hreiðarsdóttir et al. 2014) estimated the effective number of founder genomes at approximately 27 and the pedigree Ne at approximately 113 — broadly consistent with the genomic estimate, and indicating that the breed's low genetic diversity reflects both an initial founder bottleneck and subsequent population crashes. Campana et al. (2012, *Animal Genetics*) demonstrated genetic continuity over 150 years by comparing mtDNA from 19th-century museum specimens to modern horses, and estimated Ne at approximately 1,000 over that shorter time window — consistent with a genetically stable 19th-century population before the crashes detectable in modern genomes. Critically, all of these studies work with modern horses or 19th-century museum material: the founding period (870–1000 CE), the medieval centuries, and the Black Death (~1402 CE) remain completely invisible.

**Ancient DNA work on Icelandic horses.** Three studies have generated molecular data from archaeological Icelandic horse specimens. Wutke et al. (2016, *Current Biology*) genotyped the DMRT3 gait variant in 90 ancient horses across multiple European sites and time periods, finding it in 10 of 13 (77%) early Icelandic specimens (9th–11th century CE) but absent from all contemporary Scandinavian horses — a pattern they interpreted as reflecting an origin of the ambling allele in medieval England and its transport to Iceland with British-affiliated settlers, with strong statistical evidence for subsequent positive selection in Iceland (Bayes Factor = 8.68). This is the most direct evidence to date that the founding Icelandic horse population was not purely Scandinavian in ancestry. Nistelberger, Pálsdóttir et al. (2019, *Journal of Archaeological Science*) applied molecular sex-typing to 22 Viking Age horse specimens from Icelandic burial contexts, confirming both a male bias in burial horses and the technical feasibility of ancient molecular work on Icelandic horse bone — and establishing the key precedent of shipping Icelandic horse specimens to a European laboratory for aDNA analysis. Campana et al. (2012), as noted above, demonstrated DNA recovery from 19th-century Icelandic museum bone. Together, these studies confirm that Icelandic horse skeletal material is tractable for aDNA analysis, that it can yield interpretable molecular data, and that at least one locus has already been shown to carry signatures of selection in the founding period. What they cannot provide is genome-wide data: the full population structure, ancestry composition, demographic history, and multi-locus adaptation landscape that require sequencing entire genomes.

**The gap and what makes it addressable now.** Two developments make this project possible at this moment. The first is methodological: the GLIMPSE2 imputation framework (Rubinacci et al. 2023, *Nature Genetics*) enables robust population genomic inference from ancient genomes sequenced to as little as 1–3× coverage — reducing the sequencing cost per genome by an order of magnitude relative to high-coverage approaches and making a 90-specimen ancient genomics project tractable within a postdoctoral fellowship budget. The second is the reference panel: Prof. Orlando's collection of 901 modern and 376 ancient horse genomes, including specimens from Scandinavia and the British Isles from the Viking Age itself, provides the dense reference needed for imputation and the direct comparators needed for Aim 1. The applicant's existing aDNA protocols, developed and validated on Icelandic skeletal material over the past decade, translate directly to horse specimens from the same archaeological contexts. Five specimens have already been extracted and all five yielded endogenous horse DNA at 10–40%, establishing definitively that the protocol works on Icelandic horse bone and that the National Museum collection is of sufficient quality for genomic analysis.

## C. RESEARCH PLAN (TIME AND WORK PLAN, METHODOLOGY, MILESTONES, PRESENT STATUS OF PROJECT, ETC.) AND DELIVERABLES

The project proceeds in three overlapping phases: Phase 1 (laboratory, Months 1–10), Phase 2 (ancestry and demography, Months 9–24), and Phase 3 (adaptation and traits, Months 20–36). All laboratory work is conducted at the deCODE Genetics aDNA facility; all computing runs on the deCODE cluster. Sequencing is performed at deCODE (Illumina NovaSeq); if capacity is unavailable, at CAGT Toulouse as a contracted service under the Orlando collaboration. The principal risk — low endogenous DNA in some specimens — is substantially mitigated by the large initial sample pool (up to 90 specimens) and preliminary data confirming 10–40% endogenous DNA in five quality-assessed Icelandic horse libraries. The project is designed to proceed with a minimum of approximately 40–50 high-quality genomes, which is sufficient for robust population genomic inference using the imputation approach described below.

**Phase 1 — Sample processing and sequencing (Months 1–10):** The applicant will work with curators at the National Museum of Iceland to assess the available skeletal material and select specimens maximising temporal coverage and geographic spread across Iceland. Up to 90 specimens spanning 870–1900 CE will be selected; teeth are prioritised as the substrate with the best aDNA preservation characteristics, with cortical and petrous bone used where teeth are unavailable. All specimens remain at the Museum until sampled under the existing sampling permission. Silica-based DNA extraction — the protocol developed by the applicant and optimised for Icelandic skeletal material — is performed in the deCODE aDNA dedicated clean-room (physically isolated, UV-decontaminated, positive air pressure, full protective clothing). Double-stranded Illumina sequencing libraries are prepared with unique dual indexing. All libraries undergo shallow shotgun sequencing (~1 million reads per library) to assess endogenous DNA content and confirm characteristic ancient DNA damage patterns (cytosine deamination at read ends, characteristic fragmentation). Authentication is evaluated using mapDamage2 (Jónsson et al. 2013). Libraries with ≥1% endogenous horse DNA and confirmed damage patterns proceed to deep sequencing (2–5× target average depth) on the Illumina NovaSeq. The bioinformatic pipeline applies AdapterRemoval2 (Schubert et al. 2016), BWA mapping to the EquCab3 horse reference genome (Li and Durbin 2009), Picard MarkDuplicates, and mapDamage2 base-quality rescaling. Biological sex is estimated from the ratio of reads mapping to the X and Y chromosomes. Note on contamination estimation: the ANGSD X-chromosome contamination method used in human aDNA pipelines is not applicable to horse specimens; contamination control for horse libraries relies on unique dual indexing, damage pattern authentication by mapDamage2, and endogenous content thresholds.

**Phase 2 — Population structure, ancestry, and demography (Months 9–24):** Ancient genomes are imputed to high density using GLIMPSE2 (Rubinacci et al. 2023) and Prof. Orlando's reference panel of 901 modern horse genomes, enabling population-genomic inference comparable to high-coverage data. The imputation pipeline was developed and validated by the Orlando laboratory in Ahmed et al. (under review) and will run in close collaboration with CAGT Toulouse. For Aim 1 (Months 12–22), principal components analysis (PCA), ADMIXTURE (Alexander et al. 2009), and f-statistics with formal admixture graph modelling (ADMIXTOOLS2; Patterson et al. 2012) characterise founding ancestry, quantify contributions of specific Scandinavian and European source populations, and test competing models of the founding event — single introduction versus multiple waves. For Aim 2 (Months 12–24), identity-by-descent (IBD) analysis tracks genealogical relationships between individuals across time; runs of homozygosity (ROH) quantify inbreeding at the individual level; and demographic modelling (PSMC; Li and Durbin 2011; SMC++; Terhorst et al. 2017) reconstructs changes in effective population size. Results are interpreted against the historical record of Iceland's livestock crises, particularly the Laki famine (1783–1784 CE) and the Black Death (~1402 CE).

**Phase 3 — Adaptation and trait evolution (Months 20–36):** Genome-wide selection scans (XP-EHH; Sabeti et al. 2007; iHS; Voight et al. 2006; population branch statistic PBS; Yi et al. 2010) identify regions showing signatures of positive selection in the Icelandic lineage relative to European outgroup populations. Targeted variant tracking follows allele frequencies through time at DMRT3 (tölt), ASIP/MC1R/PMEL17 (coat colour and MCOA), sweet itch GWAS loci (Shrestha et al. 2015; Schurink et al. 2012), and MHC/immune regions (Maccari et al. 2019). ROH-based analysis identifies genomic regions where inbreeding may have driven deleterious recessive variants to elevated frequency.

## F. IF APPLICABLE, CONTRIBUTION OF DOCTORAL AND MASTER'S STUDENTS TO THE PROJECT

This project will provide a substantive research component for one Master's student from the University of Iceland, recruited from programmes in biological anthropology, archaeology, or biology. The student will gain genuine, hands-on experience in ancient animal genomics — a field in which no training infrastructure currently exists in Iceland.

**Option 1 — The history of the Silver/PMEL17 mutation** (biology or biological anthropology): The PMEL17 Arg618Cys mutation is responsible for both the Silver coat colour — one of the most visually distinctive and frequently selected phenotypes in the modern Icelandic horse — and Multiple Congenital Ocular Anomalies (MCOA) syndrome, a heritable eye condition documented at clinically significant frequencies in the breed (Brunberg et al. 2006; Andersson et al. 2011). The student will track the PMEL17 allele frequency through time across the 870–1900 CE temporal range using the imputed ancient genomes from Phase 2. No additional laboratory work is required — the project is entirely analytical, making it feasible within a 12-month research period.

**Option 2 — Paired human and horse genomics at Sílastaðir** (archaeology or biological anthropology): The Viking Age burial ground at Sílastaðir (Glæsibæjarhreppur) contains four human graves — all four individuals have been sequenced in the companion ancient human genomics study (Ebenesersdóttir et al., in preparation) — and two separate horse burials: one at the lower end of Grave 1 (a richly equipped warrior burial with sword, axes, and spearhead), and one at the northern end of Grave 4 (a second warrior burial with bridle bit and iron fittings). This makes Sílastaðir one of the most precisely documented assemblages of co-deposited human and horse remains in Iceland. The student will examine whether the two horses are genetically related, whether each horse's genomic profile correlates with the social status of the human interred alongside it, and whether the Sílastaðir horses show genetic connections to horses from other Icelandic sites.

The student will be co-supervised by Dr Ebenesersdóttir (primary supervisor) and Prof. Agnar Helgason (co-supervisor). Both projects are scoped to be achievable within a 12-month research period and are designed to result in a publishable manuscript (Article 3 above). Six months of salary support is included in this budget to strengthen the supervisory arrangement and ensure the project timeline is met. Training one Master's student in aDNA methodology applied to Icelandic archaeological animal material creates a concrete long-term benefit for Icelandic science: the student will be among the first Icelandic researchers with practical experience in ancient animal genomics, with skills directly applicable to future projects on other Icelandic archaeological species.
